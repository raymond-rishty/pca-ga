**CASE 2023-22**

**TE** **SAM JUNG SUH**

**v.**

**KOREAN SOUTHEASTERN PRESBYTERY**

#### DECISION ON APPEAL

**March 6, 2025**

**CASE SUMMARY**

This case came before the SJC on the Appeal of TE Sam Jung Suh. The Appeal
arose from the censure of indefinite suspension imposed by Korean
Southeastern Presbytery (KSEP) after trial proceedings.

The Panel heard the Appeal in person on October 16, 2024, at the Westin
Atlanta Airport. Appellant, TE Sam Jung Suh, appeared at the meeting with
his representative, TE Mike Suh. KSEP, the Appellee, was represented by TE
Eddie Lim.

The Panel was composed of TE David Coffin (Chairman), RE Jim Eggert
(Secretary), TE Paul Bankson, John Bise (alternate), and TE Paul Lee
(alternate). Having heard the matter for about two and a half hours, and upon
their close review of the Record, and a series of meetings for deliberation, the
Panel have decided to recommend that the decision of KSEP be reversed in

whole.

**I.** **SUMMARY OF THE FACTS**

09/26/22 Three Teaching Elders signed a “Formal Petition” to Korean
Southeastern Presbytery (“KSEP”) alleging a “formal charge
of financial misconduct” against TE Suh, the pastor of Korean
First Presbyterian Church (“KFPC”). The document claimed
that TE Suh had “publicly admitted his wife deposited church
funds from the sale of church property into her and his
personal bank accounts.”

10/04/22 At its stated meeting, KSEP considered what it called a
“petition for KSEP to investigate allegations of financial

misconduct” against TE Suh and formed an investigative
committee to investigate “the nature of these allegations”
(“the IC”)

10/05/22 The IC could not initially come to an agreement regarding the
scope of the investigation. Some members believed the scope
should be limited to the video of TE Suh’s apology on July 3,
2022, while others believed the investigation “should include
TE Suh’s alleged financial misconduct as senior pastor.”

10/31/22 At a called meeting, KSEP instructed its committee to
determine the scope of the investigation.

11/04/22 The IC decided to “investigate allegations of financial
misconduct as reported by KFPC’s transition committee.”

11/11/22 The IC decided to request documents from the church's
transition committee that supported “their claims of TE Suh's
alleged embezzlement and money laundering.”

11/17/22 The IC met with the Transition Committee for about 3.5

hours.

12/18/22 The IC met with the KFPC Session for approaching 3.5 hours.

12/19/22 The IC met with TE Suh and his wife for a little over 3 hours.

02/08/2023 The IC completed its report stating it believed there was a
strong presumption of guilt against TE Suh. The IC reported
that it held 23 meetings lasting about 33 hours. The Report
stated, “The investigation focused on the video footage of
Pastor Sam Jung Suh submitted as evidence by the accusers,
the ‘Financial Story’ of the senior pastor and his wife and the
suspicions mentioned in the report of the transition committee
formed by the session of Korean First Presbyterian Church.”
The IC also “investigated additional suspicions.” The Report
noted that it requested “additional evidentiary material” from
TE Suh and his wife but “did not receive everything we asked
for.” The Report “confirmed that there was numerous

financial misconduct [ _sic_ ].” Noting that the IC “believes there
is a strong presumption of guilt,” it recommended Presbytery
institute four charges (including factual specifications)
against TE Suh.

02/13/2023 At a called meeting, KSEP received the report of the IC and
concluded: “As the IC reported a strong presumption of guilt,
KSEP will institute process according to the _BCO_
(determining whether the trial will be conducted as a whole or
by a judicial commission and the procedure of indictment and
entering a plea thereafter) at the upcoming spring stated
meeting.”

04/10/2023 After allowing TE Suh to address the stated meeting of
Presbytery, KSEP voted to indict TE Suh (the vote was 3217-1), formed a judicial commission to try the case, and
appointed a prosecutor. The Presbytery set the judicial
commission at five members, inclusive of two alternates (3
TEs and 2 REs). The Presbytery elected two “Koreanspeaking commissioners” and one “English-speaking
commissioner.”

05/01/23 The Moderator and Stated Clerk signed the First version of
the “Charges and Specifications against TE Suh (“the Original
Indictment”).

06/13/23 The General Assembly adopted _BCO_ 15-1 and 15-3, stating
that the commission rather than the Presbytery is to decide the
judgment in cases delegated to a judicial commission. Judicial
matters committed to a commission would no longer return to
the commissioning Presbytery for vote: “The commission
shall try the case in the manner presented by the Rules of
Discipline and shall submit to the Presbytery a full statement
of the case and the judgment rendered. The judgment of the
commission shall be the decision of the Presbytery, and the
statement of the case and judgment printed in its minutes.”

07/24/23 A Korean translation of the Original Indictment (originally
written in English) was delivered via certified mail to TE

Suh’s residence. The Original Indictment contained three
charges, claiming that TE Suh (1) violated the Ninth
Commandment, (2) violated the Eighth Commandment, and
(3) failed to uphold ordination vows three, four, and seven as
senior pastor and moderator of the Session, breaking the Fifth
Commandment.

08/07/23 The arraignment occurred, and TE Suh pled “not guilty to all
charges.”

08/25/23 The Prosecutor presented and the Moderator, Stated Clerk and
the Prosecutor executed a new version of the Original
Indictment (“the Amended Indictment”).

The Amended Indictment struck the first specification of
Charge 1, which had stated that TE Suh falsely informed the
church Session that $150,000.00 was owed on a church loan,
even though the lender had forgiven the loan.

In place of the former specification, the Amended Indictment
added a “representative incident” that TE Suh falsely
represented to the congregation that his wife had negotiated a
reduction in the outstanding church loan in a lesser amount
than she actually had, so as to make it appear that the church
still owed $250,000.00 when the church only owed
$200,000.00.

08/26/23 TE Suh received the Amended Indictment from the

Prosecutor. The Chairman of the judicial commission emailed
TE Suh (in Korean) notifying him that the Prosecutor had
provided a “revised indictment,” and represented that the
amendment made “no change in the 3 main subjects” of the
original. The email added: “There are some revised
specifications in those subjects, noting the “removed
specification has a line through it and the added one is
underlined.” The Chairman concluded, “I believe you
understand English, so I have not translated it and sent the
prosecutor’s original.”

09/01/23 The trial was conducted over the course of approximately nine
hours. The Prosecutor presented two witnesses as well as
corroborating evidence (13 documentary exhibits, 1 video,
and 2 audio files). TE Suh presented ten witnesses.

09/06/23 The Judicial Commission rendered its decision in the case
finding TE Suh guilty on all three counts and recommended
alternate censure depending on whether TE Suh was deemed
to be repentant.

09/30/23 TE Suh received the verdicts.

10/02/23 KSEP met to consider the report of the judicial commission
recommending alternate censure. The commission reported its
guilty verdict on all three counts and “recommended” censure
in the alternate, dependent upon the Presbytery's finding of TE
Suh’s repentance:

- “If TE Suh is repentant and satisfies the court as to his

repentance” then “definite suspension from the
sacraments and from office be imposed for a period of one
year.”

- “if TE Suh is not repentant” then “indefinite suspension

from the sacraments and office and to form a shepherding
committee to shepherd TE Suh toward a sense of his sin
and to repent.”

TE Suh was permitted to address the judgment and indicated
that he believed the one-year suspension was “excessive,” but
he “no longer wants to make this a matter of controversy to
the Presbytery and does not intend to complain to the General
Assembly.” He added that he had “decided to accept the
Presbytery’s ruling as is but does not admit to any wrong
doing. [ _sic_ ]” TE Suh then “excused himself from Presbytery,
without seeking permission, stating fatigue and his need to
rest,” having offered no “written statement of confession or
repentance.” KSEP found him “impenitent as his address did
not satisfy the court as to his repentance,” and censured him
with indefinite suspension from the sacraments and the

exercise of his office as a teaching elder” until he gives
satisfactory evidence of repentance. The judicial commission
was then dissolved.

10/7/23 TE Suh was emailed the decision and censure.

11/06/23 TE Suh filed a timely appeal from the judgment.

12/13/23 The SJC Officers appointed a panel consisting of TE David
Coffin, RE Sam Duncan, and TE Paul Bankson, with RE Jim
Eggert and TE Paul Lee as alternates.

09/06/24 After finalizing the Record of the Case, the Panel found the
case to be judicially in order and ready to proceed to final
hearing. (When RE Sam Duncan rolled off the SJC in June
2024, the SJC Officers appointed RE John Bise as Alternate
and RE Jim Eggert (original Alternate) assumed RE Sam
Duncan’s original position as Principal.)

10/16/24 The Panel heard oral arguments of the parties. Appellant was
represented by TE Mike Suh and Appellee was represented by
TE Eddie Lim.

**II.** **STATEMENT OF THE ISSUE**

Did Korean Southeastern Presbytery (KSEP) fail in its constitutional
obligations when it initiated judicial process against TE Sam Jung
Suh?

**III.** **JUDGMENT**

Yes. The decision of KSEP finding a strong presumption of guilt on
the part of TE Suh, and thus to institute process, appoint a prosecutor
to prepare an indictment and to conduct the case, the work of the
Judicial Commission (JC) and its judgment, and the censure imposed,
is reversed in whole. TE Suh is herein restored to his standing in KSEP
as it was before Presbytery entertained the “charges,” and no judicial

process, with respect to any matter in the proceedings reversed, is
permitted hereafter.

**IV.** **REASONING & OPINION**

In his Appeal TE Suh set forth specifications of error with respect to KSEP’s
actions: (1) in the pre-investigation; (2) in the _BCO_ 31-2 investigation and the
finding of a strong presumption of guilt; (3) in the indictment, trial proceedings
and judgment; and (4) in the censure.

This Court finds that the pre-investigative phase, the _BCO_ 31-2 investigation,
and the commencement of process, were marred by a number of grave
constitutional errors. This finding fully justifies the vindication of the
Appellant, and a decision to reverse in whole.

Thus, although TE Suh, in his Appeal, brought many other specifications of
error with respect to these proceedings—specifications of varying merit, and
some of which, of themselves, might have warranted this judgment—these
need not be taken up in this Decision, as the whole proceeding is reversed on
the ground of the unconstitutional initiation of judicial process.

THE FIRST specification of error alleges that KSEP failed to require that those
bringing charges do so in compliance with _BCO_ 27-5. Without speaking to TE
Suh, three Teaching Elders of the Presbytery brought charges against him in a
Petition to the Presbytery (September 26, 2022). They did so based on a
YouTube video (July 3, 2022) wherein TE Suh addresses the congregation of
Korean First Presbyterian Church (KFPC). TE Suh was first informed about
the charges by receipt of the agenda three days prior to the Presbytery meeting
at which the charges were to be considered (October 4, 2022).

This specification of error is sustained.

A Minute Explanatory. _BCO_ 27-5 identifies as crucial to Scriptural discipline
“Individual’s responsibility to admonish one another,” citing both Matthew
18:15 and Galatians 6:1. The latter directs that “if anyone is caught in any
transgression, you who are spiritual should restore him in a spirit of
gentleness.” The alleged transgression in view in this case appears to be a
general ( _BCO_ 29-3), public ( _BCO_ 29-4) offense. The Rules of Discipline set

forth distinct procedures to follow with respect to the kind of offense in
question. _BCO_ 31-5 insists that “An injured party shall not become a
prosecutor of personal offenses without having tried the means of
reconciliation and of reclaiming the offender, required by Christ,” citing Mat.
18. However, _BCO_ 31-7 grants that “When the prosecution is instituted by the
court, the previous steps required by our Lord in the case of personal offenses
are not necessary.”

Clearly the three charging Teaching Elders were not pursuing personal
offenses, but rather were charging a public, general offense, with respect to an
act they specifically identify. Consider:

“Formal Petition Submitted to the Korean Southeastern Presbytery of
The Presbyterian Church In America,” September 26, 2022 (ROC 23,
emphasis added)

. . . It is with humble and sad hearts that we bring _a formal charge_
against TE Sam Jung Suh regarding financial misconduct with church
funds during his time as Senior Pastor of Korean First Presbyterian
Church.

In a publicly broadcast and recorded speech
(https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=s6LHr-nngDg, dated July 3,
2022), _TE Sam Jung Suh publicly admitted that his wife deposited_
_church funds from the sale of church property into her and his_
_personal bank accounts_ . This action is _financial misconduct_ with
church funds and is a chargeable offense. . . .

However, just as clearly, the court itself was not instituting process; on the
contrary, it was responding to charges laid. Consider:

October 4, 2022, KSEP Meeting Minutes (ROC 31, emphasis added)

74-14. New Business

1. Petition for KSEP to investigate allegations of financial misconduct
against TE Sam Suh.
_**MSC**_ _to vote by secret ballot to determine if KSEP will consider_
_allegations of financial misconduct against TE Sam Suh. 24-8-1_

_**MSC**_ _to form a committee to investigate the nature of these allegations_
_and to report to Presbytery. TEs Thomas Oh. Jongsu Hong, Sang Joon_
_Kim and RE Yoonho Kang shall serve as committee members. 19-5-1_

Note that the motion employs the language of the Petition.

Thus, is Mat. 18 compliance required of the chargers, or no? It is instructive
to note that _BCO_ 31-7 goes on to say that “There are many cases, however, in
which it will promote the interests of religion to send a committee to converse
in a private manner with the offender, and endeavor to bring him to a sense of
his guilt, before instituting actual process.” Thus, a form of Mat. 18
compliance is urged as a course of wisdom, at the least, even when not
required.

beth a ta sit may, under “General Provisions” the Rules return to Galatians 6:

_BCO_ 32-1, “Brethren, if a man is overtaken in any trespass, you who
are spiritual restore such a one in the spirit of gentleness, considering
yourself lest you also be tempted.”

The goal is to restore, the manner is with gentleness. _BCO_ 32-2 sets forth two
different paths possible for a case of process: “some person or persons
undertake to make out the charge”; or “the court finds it necessary, for the
honor of religion, itself to take the step provided for in _BCO_ 31-2.”

In this case some persons _have_ undertaken to make out a charge, and, at the
very least, they had the duty under Galatians 6 to do what they could to restore
with gentleness and wisdom. Had KSEP required evidence of such effort, the
accusing Teaching Elders, having inadvertently misrepresented TE Suh’s
comments—i.e., TE Suh apologizing for taking money from the sale of church
property—could have been easily corrected, as restoration, wisdom and
gentleness required. The undisputed claim of the Appellant is that the charging
TEs were completely removed from the situation and had no context for
understanding what they saw and heard on the video. Further, the person they
were accusing had faithfully served at his church for 46 years without scandal
and certainly was due the judgment of charity. He was a founding member and
father of the Presbytery and a distinguished member of it until the accusations
were brought. The Presbytery honored him upon his retirement in May of
2022. And yet, in this instance, instead of brothers coming to TE Suh, to restore

him ina spirit of gentleness, TE Suh first learned of the charges through an
agenda emailed a few days before the October 2022 presbytery meeting that
was to act on the charging Petition.

The misrepresentation of the charge _was_ finally corrected, in fact the original
charge was not included in the Indictment. Further, at the Hearing on the
Appeal, Presbytery generously acknowledged that the initial charge was
without merit:

Presbytery accedes to the Appellant’s claim that the allegations were
indeed a misunderstanding of the Appellant’s public confession on the
YouTube video. Absent from the indictment is any reference to a
charge or specification related to _the sale of church property_ . The
investigative committee looked into financial aspects regarding the
sale of a piece of property owned by the church but was unable to
proceed further due to lack of documentation (ROC 51, lines. 16-24,
emphasis added).

Yet that admission was too late: Grievous harm had been done, as scandalous
charges were received against TE Suh—not on slight grounds—but on no
grounds at all (cf. _BCO_ 34-2). And that groundless charge became, in the work
of the Investigative Committee (IC), an unlimited license for intrusion into the
life of TE Suh and the Session of KFPC.

THE SECOND specification of error alleges that KSEP failed to correct its
Investigative Committee (IC) for violations of constitutional responsibility,
i.e., _BCO_ 31-8 requires that

Great caution ought to be exercised in receiving accusations from any
person who is known to indulge a malignant spirit towards the
accused; who is not of good character; who is himself under censure
or process; who is deeply interested in any respect in the conviction of
the accused; or who is known to be litigious, rash or highly imprudent.

This specification of error is sustained.

A Minute Explanatory. IC relied upon testimony of 3 people who had recently
departed from the church and relied on the testimony of one witness that the
Record of the Case (ROC) clearly shows should have been treated with “great

caution”. Yet there is no evidence of the IC acting with “great caution”. On the
contrary, the IC gave greater weight to these sources than it did to TE Suh, a
minister of 46 years of unblemished reputation, and to the very Session of the
Church he served, the Session that would have been harmed by his dereliction.

THE THIRD specification of error alleges that at its meetings on February 13
and April 10-11, 2023, KSEP failed to comply with _BCO_ 31-2 in approving
the Report of its IC, finding “a strong presumption of guilt” with respect to TE
Suh. IC’s _BCO_ 31-2 investigation was constitutionally deficient because it
failed to comply with the provisions of _BCO_ 31-2. [42]

This specification of error is sustained.

A Minute Explanatory.

a. KSEP did not have before it “reports,” (i.e., “common fame”

information deleterious to reputation); it had “charges” (see
specification 1).

b. KSEP, treating “charges” as “reports,” failed to comply with the

leading direction of the _BCO_ 31-2: To “demand” _from TE Suh_
satisfactory explanations of the report (i.e., the charge of the Petition.).

c. KSEP allowed an investigation of TE Suh that, having abandoned the

original charge as untrue, then without warrant, expanded to matters
having nothing to do with the specificity of original report.

d. KSEP’s _BCO_ 31-2 “investigation was constitutionally deficient

because it failed to comply with that provision’s ‘due diligence’
requirement.” Here it is critical to understand that “[d]etermining the
