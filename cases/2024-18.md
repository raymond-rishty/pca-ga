**CASE 2024-18**

**v.**

**WISCONSIN PRESBYTERY**

#### DECISION ON COMPLAINT

**March 6, 2025**

**CASE SUMMARY**

Presbytery received allegations from two former members of a PCA Church
in Wisconsin regarding the Session's alleged improper removal of them from
the rolls and some other unrelated Session decisions. Presbytery appointed an
investigative committee, which eventually reported some advice regarding the
Session, but also recommended Presbytery "commence process" against the
Church's pastor. Presbytery found a strong presumption of guilt and appointed
a nine-member judicial commission and a prosecutor. Three months later, the
prosecutor issued two indictments related to allegations about the quality of
the minister's pastoral care. The TE pled not guilty, and after several pre-trial
meetings, the judicial commission voted 8-0 to dismiss both Charges. The
prosecutor filed a Complaint against those actions. Presbytery heard and
denied the Complaint, and Complainant then carried the Complaint to the SJC.

**II. STATEMENT OF THE ISSUES**

1. Did the Presbytery err on July 9, 2024 when its Judicial Commission

dismissed Indictment 1?

2. Did the Presbytery err on July 9, 2024 when its Judicial Commission

dismissed Indictment 2?

**III. JUDGMENT**

2. No.

**IV. REASONING AND OPINION**

**1. Dismissal of Indictment 1**

The JC requested, and the Accused voluntarily provided a written plea,
including reasons the Accused pled not guilty. The written plea provided

contextual information related to the Charges. In pretrial meetings, the JC
requested, and the Accused provided, lengthy explanations regarding the
background and circumstances giving rise to the Charges. In consequence of
these meetings, the JC dismissed each indictment.

First, we note that under our Constitution, a court (or judicial commission
assigned to try the case) may, in the exercise of its discretion, dismiss an
indictment where circumstances warrant. See Case 2007-14: _PCA v. Louisiana_
_Presbytery_ ( _M36GA_, p. 128) and Case 2010-16: _Lyons v. Western Carolina_
_Presbytery_ ( _M39GA_, p. 594). [55]

Below is the written judgment from the JC’s report.

As to Indictment #1: The "neglect and failure of his ordination
vows to be faithful and diligent in the exercise of all duties as a
Christian and a minister of the Gospel," the charge is dismissed
with the recommendation that [the Church] hire [a private
organization] for both training and policy making.

The Complainant's primary objection to this dismissal, as expressed in his
Complaint, was the court's reliance upon _BCO_ 32-20.

32-20. The accused or a member of the court may object to the
consideration of a charge, for example, if he thinks the passage
of time since the alleged offense makes fair adjudication
unachievable. The court should consider factors such as the

gravity of the alleged offense as well as what degradations of
evidence and memory may have occurred in the intervening
period.

In its Report, the JC reasoned:

The seven years between the event and the trial, the transition
that occurred at Gibbsville Church moving from the Reformed
Church in America to the Presbyterian Church in America, and
the subsequent changes in how the pastor and elders engage

The specifications supporting Indictment 1 describe events that occurred in
2017. The SJC judges the JC’s decision to be one in a matter of discretion and
judgment and does not find that such decision was clearly erroneous. Thus, we
afford the great deference required by the standard of review stipulated in _BCO_

The lower court was authorized, in the exercise of its discretion, to dismiss the
charges because of the passage of time. Notwithstanding, several of the
reasons articulated by members of the JC in the JC’s pretrial meetings appear
troublesome, if not erroneous. Some members of the court appeared to confuse
the status of persons as witnesses/offended parties and complainants in a prior
case. In addition, some members of the JC expressed skepticism regarding the
motives of some of the witnesses when they declined to participate in
mediation.

The mere filing of a complaint, proffering allegations, or identifying offenses
does not disqualify one from testifying as a witness at trial, nor do any of these
permitted actions automatically mark a person’s testimony as dubious
according to _BCO_ 31-8. _BCO_ 31-8 describes patterns of improper conduct
which are distinct from the presentation of complaints and allegations via
proper process. From their pretrial comments in the transcript, it appears some
of the Commission members did not appreciate this important distinction. Our
standards of review recognize broad discretion afforded the lower court in this
matter, and they require us to defer to the lower court’s decision, even though
the Record shows that some members of the lower court may have held
erroneous reasons leading to the decision.

**2. Dismissal of Indictment 2**

As indicated in the reasoning regarding Indictment 1, the rights of a Prosecutor
to proceed with a case are subject to the proper exercise of judicial power by
a judicial commission. Any ecclesiastical body trying a case has _some_ inherent
power to dismiss a case pretrial.

The JC, in its written judgment dismissing indictment 2, stated:

"The neglect and failure of his ordination vows to be zealous and
faithful in maintaining the truths of the Gospel and the purity and
peace and unity of the church, the charge is dismissed as the matter
should be referred to the lower court.”

Further, in its report, after citing _BCO_ 11-4 and 31-1, the judicial commission

reasoned:

The aggrieved parties must first seek resolution through the court of
original jurisdiction and, if so desired, they may appeal that decision
to the higher court, in this case, the Presbytery.

There is error in this reasoning. Even though ministerial functions of a church
fall on both the ruling elder sofa church and the teaching elders serving a
church ( _BCO_ 12-5) the courts of original jurisdiction for ruling elders and
teaching elders differ. a member o fa church feeling aggrieved by a teaching
elder must first go to the Presbytery and is not compelled to first go to his or
her local Session.

However, despite the fact that the reasoning for dismissal is flawed, the act of
issuing the dismissal is not. The Record indicates that the subject matter of
Indictment 2 was not investigated nor acted upon by the Investigative
Committee. In fact, the indictment itself shows that the alleged facts behind
the indictment did not even occur until after the Investigative Committee was
discharged. As a result, it is apparent that the Prosecutor unilaterally added
this charge to his indictment.

The specifications of Indictment 2 further show that the lone charge of this
indictment arose over what is alleged to be a personal and private offense,
which never came to Presbytery as a “general fame” report. ( _BCO_ 31-2) As
this is a private offense, even if learned about by the Prosecutor through his
interviewing of potential witnesses, the Prosecutor cannot just make the
offense public by putting it in an indictment and then taking the case to trial.
The Prosecutor must first ensure that the accuser addresses the matter with the
minister in private. ( _BCO_ 27-5, 34-3) There is no indication this happened. As
such, the dismissal was authorized.
