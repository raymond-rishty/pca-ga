**CASE 2024-02**

**MR. GREG GLEASON**

**v.**

**HOUSTON METRO PRESBYTERY**

#### DECISION ON COMPLAINT

**October 17, 2024**

**CASE SUMMARY**

Mr. Gleason filed a _BCO_ 43-1 Complaint with his Session alleging the Session
violated the Ninth Commandment. He contended the offense was the Session's

purported approval of the contents of a Personnel Committee Statement that
was later read to the congregation by the Chairman of that Committee in a
meeting to consider voting on the dissolution of an Associate Pastor's call. The
Session ruled the Complaint out of order, and Mr. Gleason carried it to the
Presbytery, which also ruled it out of order. he carrie ditto the SJC.

**RULING**

This matter is judicially out of order and cannot be put in order. _BCO_ 43 is not
a constitutionally permissible avenue for processing accusations of offenses.

**REASONING AND OPINION**

In general, while a speaker a ta congregational meeting might be liable to
church discipline for violating the Ninth Commandant, such a matter would
involve an “offense.” an offense is defined as “the proper object of judicial
process,” being “anything in the doctrines or practice of a Church member
professing faith in Christ which is contrary to the Word of God” ( _BCO_ 29-1).
But such cases of process are not the proper subject of _BCO_ 43 proceedings,
which is the mode in which the instant matter is presented to us.

Indeed, the Complaint in this Case alleges an offense for which it seeks
redress. The Complaint suggests the Session ostensibly sinned, by violating
the Ninth Commandment when the Personnel Committee Statement ("PCS")
was read to the congregation. More fundamentally, however, the gravamen of

the Complaint is that individuals in the leadership allegedly committed sins
against the Associate Pastor before the congregational meeting such that the
PCS was “misrepresenting the history” of supposed offenses by the leadership.
The “Preamble” to the Complaint said, “[I]t is my sincere hope and prayer that
the following complaint is taken in such a way as to bring repentance where it
is needed, and thereby healing to the Lord’s church,” adding, “[w]hile the
Session has erred in this matter, the road to repentance and forgiveness is wide
open to all who repent and believe.” The Complaint’s concluding section is
titled, “Sin, Confession, and Repentance,” wherein the Session is called to
“confess the truth,” revise and republish the PCS, and “ask for the forgiveness”
of both the Associate Pastor and the congregation.

The grievances that the Complaint seeks to adjudicate cannot be addressed via
the instant “mode of review.” For the reasons detailed below, a complaint like
this one commits a category error in seeking a ruling that either a session has
sinned or individuals have sinned and requesting that a higher court require
such to “confess” and “repent.”

individuals accused of sinning in such a manner are subject to protections ina
“case of process,” after the preliminary exhaustion of progressive
confrontation required by Matthew 18 or in such cases that “the previous steps
required by our Lord in the case of personal offenses are not necessary”
because “prosecution is instituted by the court” rather than by an individual
accuser ( _BCO_ 31-7). _BCO_ 43, being a “mode of review” to challenge the acts
or decisions of a court of the Church, certainly cannot redress the alleged sins
of individuals.

The idea that _BCO_ 43 might permit a member o fa congregation to redress his
or her sense that a Session, rather than an individual, has sinned is even more
problematic. Contending that sin as it relates to a court of the Church can be
treated like the sin o fan individual is a category error ak into supposing that a
Church court can be effectually called, justified, adopted, sanctified, have
saving faith, repent unto life, persevere to the end, do good works, and have
assurance of salvation. Certainly, a court’s individual members can and do
experience all these things, but just as there is no coherent principle that makes
a court of the Church as such either an object of salvation or of damnation in
God’s salvation economy, so also our polity does not treat a court of the
church as a subject o fa “case of process” in the same way it treats an
individual.

Thus, at least with respect to _BCO_ 43 complaint proceedings, we do not
evaluate whether a court of the Church has sinned so much as whether it has

erred. To be sure, when courts err, they show the effects of sin in the Church,
yet the errors of Church courts do not subject them to church discipline via a
case of process in the same way that sin does to individuals. Because that mode
of review we call a complaint is a “written representation made against some
act or decision of a court of the Church” ( _BCO_ 43-1) that remedy is only
available against a court’s joint action, or what _BCO_ 3-2 calls the exercise of
the power of “jurisdiction.” Complaints cannot redress alleged sinful actions
by individual church officers because complaints only reach the “acts or
decisions” of a court.

_BCO_ 39-1 makes it clear that the “acts and decisions of a lower court are
brought under the supervision of a higher court” only “in one or another of the
following modes: (1) Review and Control, (2) Reference, (3) Appeal, and (4)
Complaint.” Our Rules of Discipline provide no procedure to treat corporate
sin of church courts as ecclesiastical discipline in the same manner as an
individual. The closest analogy is _BCO_ 40-5, which provides that a higher
court may, among other things, “censure” a “delinquent court,” but even then,
it is only f oran “important delinquency or grossly unconstitutional
proceeding,” not for a _BCO_ 29-1 “offense.” Regardless, this Case has not come
before us through the procedures prescribed by _BCO_ 40-5, so that is not before

us.

A complaint under _BCO_ 43, as filed in this Case, cannot redress alleged sin in
local church leadership as the Complaint seeks. In complaint proceedings, the
higher court has power at most only to “annul the whole or any part of the
action of a lower court against which complaint has been made, or to send the
matter back to the lower court with instructions for a new hearing” ( _BCO_ 4310). The higher courts must avoid adjudicating allegations of sin and
grievances within a congregation or its leadership that can only be fairly
adjudicated with the special protections afforded in a case of process. In _BCO_
43 proceedings, the higher court has no power at all to investigate, much less
to censure, a lower court, nor may it assume jurisdiction to decide whether
particular individuals have sinned and whether a lower court needs to “confess,
repent, and ask forgiveness.” Adjudicating the allegations in this Complaint
would deny alleged offenders the protections of a case of process under our
_Rules of Discipline_ .

A preliminary ruling was sent to the Complainant, and he filed a two-page
Review Brief. However, he declined to address the concerns in that
preliminary ruling regarding accusations of sin and _BCO_ 43 complaints. The
_BCO_ provides other avenues for persons who feel aggrieved by sin, but _BCO_
43 is not one of them.
