**Case 2: Complaint of Teaching Elder Rod Mays Against New River Presbytery**

Nov. 22, 1979

Mr. Bob Kresge
Stated Clerk, New River Presbytery
Presbyterian Church in America

I, Rod Mays, a teaching elder of the New River Presbytery of the Presbyterian Church in America do hereby issue a complaint to the General Assembly concerning the failure of the New River Presbytery to deal with one of its ministers (Mr. Linwood Wilkes) who was reported to be guilty of writing slanderous letters concerning another minister in New River Presbytery, (Mr. Bill Fitzhenry).

Whereas, these accusations of slander were reported to the Presbytery’s committee on Ministers and Candidates,

Whereas, that committee moved to take action against Mr. Wilkes,

Whereas, before the accusations were brought before Presbytery, a motion was moved, seconded, and approved to drop the discussion of matters relating to the accusations of Mr. Wilkes,

Therefore, I do protest and complain (BCO-44) about this failure of presbytery to deal with reported scandalous activity involving one of its ministers.

See § 8-13, 2; p. 62, and § 8-84; p. 111.

Rod S. Mays

**Case 3: Complaint of Teaching Elders Byron Snapp and James M. Hope against Calvary Presbytery.**

The text of a complaint against the actions of Calvary Presbytery in the matter of accepting a candidate for ordination under the extraordinary clause when there was no reason for using the extraordinary clause. The said candidate had chosen not to take more than one quarter of Hebrew as he did not at that time plan to come into the PCA.

The undersigned hereby file a complaint regarding the following motion that was adopted by Calvary
Presbytery at its stated winter meeting Thursday, Jan. 24, 1980: "That (the candidate) be examined by
Presbytery under the extraordinary clause (Book of Church Order 22 A ) "
Respectfully submitted,
Byron Snapp
James M. Hope
Date, January 28, 1980
REASONS FOR THE COMPLAINT
1) It appears that more than one quarter of Hebrew is required to meet the standards set forth in BCO 22-4
in light of the following: a) All the approved Seminaries of the PCA recognize the need of more than one quarter
of Hebrew to meet the Hebrew requirement set forth in BCO 22-4; b) On page 190 of the Minutes of the 7th
General Assembly of the PCA the 2t2 program has five quarters of Hebrew and Hebrew exegesis recommended
as a necessary part of the curriculum; c) By bringing the candidate in under the extraordinary clause, the
Presbytery, in effect, went on record as stating that his Hebrew preparation was insufficient to meet the
requirements of BCO Tl-A.
2) The candidate took but one quarter of Hebrew. He passed it. He did not take further courses although
further courses were available. In light of this fact, the use of the extraordinary clause appears to us to be
improper.
3) We believe that the extraordinary clause is to be used only in extraordinary cases. We do not believe
this to be an extraordinary case as a) the candidate is a young man;b) he passed his first quarter of Hebrew; c) he
could be a student supply at the church which called him and continue his study of Hebrew at Columbia Bible
College Graduate School, Columbia, S.C. of which he is an alumnus, as heist o be pastor of northeast chapel
in Columbia, S.C. ;d) The reasons which the candidate gave for not taking more Hebrew are as follows: “ To be
honest, i took only one quarter of hebrews imply because i thought i would fail the next quarter. Grades were
give nona curve basis and i was always at the bottom. I might add that the PCA was no part of my vocational
plans at the time, so my main motive in securing a hebrews kill was simply so that i could lookup words and
stumble through commentaries, not taking any certain number of hours.' ’
4) If the extraordinary clause is used in this manner, the precedent is set for the relaxing of any
requirement set forth in BCO 22-4.
5) We desire to have the following question answered: "What is the proper use of the extraordinary
clause?''.
See § 8-13, 3; p. 63, and § 8-36; p. 77.

Whereas, as the Judicial Commission of the Mid-Atlantic Presbytery, Presbyterian Church in America,
a ta hearing on november 2, 1979, on four charges against Robert C. Schoof, did render an injustice in the
judgment and censure, which action is contrary to the BCO, and possible manifestation of prejudice in the case,
the undersigned hereby complains to the General Assembly of the Presbyterian Church in America of this
action of the Mid-Atlantic Presbytery in the above entitled matter, and for reasons in support of such complaint,
states the following:
Charge 1. The Commission found Mr. Schoof guilty of violating the civil laws of Virginia and Section
60-1 BCO. I do not complain of this finding. The Commission found Mr. Schoof not guilty of violating Sect.
60-2 and 60-6. These later findings are in conflict with the first, as the civil laws of Virginia require that a
lawful minister perform the marriage, as does Sect. 60-2. By not convicting Mr. Schoof of violating 60-2,
Mid-Atlantic Presbytery states that a seminary student is an ecclesiastical official, that is, a lawful minister.
Sect. 60-6 requires ministers to be careful they transgress neither the laws of God, nor the laws of the
community.
Charge 2. The Commission found Mr. Schoof not guilty of signing an official document stating he had
joined his daughter and her husband in marriage, when the official document, bearing Mr. Schoof s signature,
was introduced in evidence.
Charge 3. The Commission found Mr. Schoof not guilty of conducting an illegal Congregational
meeting, when two of four Elders present at Session meeting setting forth the call for the Congregational
meeting testified otherwise.
Charge 4. The Commission found Mr. Schoof not guilty of violating Sect. 57-2 and 57-6 when
testimony introduced showed no ruling Elder present at the Baptism and only a selected group of less than eight
members of the congregation present.
