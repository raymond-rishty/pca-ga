inflicted and then regained when it is time to restore the fallen brother (which
is what the Complainant’s position would require), but is active throughout the
entire time the case is “under judicial consideration until the declaration of
restoration has been pronounced” {BCO 37-8). Thus, BCO 36-2 says “the
degree of censure and mode of administering it shall be within the discretion of
the court.” If the court determines in its mercy that it is going to inflict the
lowest censure possible in the beginning and move to higher censures only if
necessary, that discretion is within their authority and should not be overturned
by the higher court “unless there is clear error on the part of the court” (BCO 3
9-3).
7. In a letter to North Florida Presbytery on May 15, 2000 Dallison
requested the privilege to demit the ministiy according to BCO 38-2 which
necessarily required that court to lift their censure against him. That request
was tantamount to an admission by the Complainant that presbytery has the
power to loose, though his complaint denies she has the power to bind. Thus,
Dallison included in his request statements that he had “fulfilled the
stipulations of the original censure of May 1997” and had “endeavored to
comply fully with the terms of the original censure” (ROC, p. 75). These
statements were a request for North Florida Presbytery to loose the original
censure against him on the basis of his professed fulfillment of their terms.
They constitute a petition that presbytery exercise its discretion in determining
appropriate adjustments to the censure in his case. His complaint, though,
attempts to divide presbytery’s binding power from her loosing power. Yet, the
power which Christ gives His church includes both and the one cannot be
severed from the other without undermining the whole authority.
8. The Complainant’s brief sets forth numerous principles in support of
his position with which all Christians should agree; such as, the right of the
accused to a fair trial, the calling of witnesses, the cross examination of
witnesses, etc. Yet, those principles are not germane to the issues in this case
because presbytery was simply exercising its authority and discretion in the
mode of administering censure against a confessed offender whom it judged to
still be impenitent. Those principles would be germane only if a new trial for
new charges had been required by our constitution and had been neglected by
North Florida Presbytery. Such was not the situation in this case.

9. If this complaint were upheld, it could lead to the harshest form of
censure being administered by courts from the outset in violation of the true
nature of discipline which “is to be exercised under a dispensation of mercy
and not of wrath” (BCO 27-4). The Complainant’s representative stated at the
hearing before this panel that Central Florida Presbytery could have deposed
Dallison and indefinitely suspended him from the sacraments on May 27, 1997
but North Florida Presbytery did not have the power to do so without a new
trial to prove his impenitence on January 12, 2001. Presbytery’s decision in the
first instance not to suspend Dallison from the sacraments is no waiver of
presbytery’s power to proceed to more severe censures at a later time. The
Complainant’s representative also argued before this panel that North Florida
Presbytery had the power to lift the censure of suspension from the ministry
against him, but not the power to increase the censure. The Complainant’s
argument is not supported by the BCO, and on that basis the complaint is
denied.
