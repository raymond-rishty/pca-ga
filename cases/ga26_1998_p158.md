# Case p158 — SJC judicial decision (GA 26, p.158)

**Court:** SJC  ·  **Assembly:** 26th (1998)  ·  **Disposition:** dismissed  ·  **Dissent:** yes

*Source: [ga26_1998 pp. 158–159](../markdown/ga26_1998.md)*

---

## II. Statement of the Issue

1. Did Central Carolina Presbytery err when it declared null and void the appointment of a ruling elder to serve on a presbytery committee because his Session had failed to act on presbytery’s request to approve him?

## III. Judgment in the Case

1. Yes.

## IV. Reasoning and Opinion

The circumstance in this case centers on a request by a presbytery to a Session to “approve” a ruling elder to serve on a presbytery committee while he was not currently serving on the Session.

The issue is this: Did the presbytery err when it declared null and void the appointment of a ruling elder to serve on a presbytery committee because his Session had failed to act on presbytery’s request to approve him?

The Record of the Case [ROC] reflects that on 1-6-97, the moderator of Central Carolina Presbytery [CCP] made an interim appointment of a ruling elder from Christ Covenant Church [CCC] to serve on the Candidates Committee of the presbytery. According to the bylaws of CCP, the appointment was subject to the final approval of the presbytery at its next stated meeting [ROC, p. 5].

At the next Stated Meeting of CCP on 1-25-97, the presbytery adopted a motion to approve the interim appointment that included this amendment: “pending approval by the CCC Session.” [ROC, p. 12]. The rationale for CCP seeking the Session’s approval was based on the fact that the elder was “on sabbatical” from his active service as an elder on the Session of CCC. The presbytery was informed that the church had a stated policy that ruling elders should not serve on presbytery and General Assembly committees duties during the tenure of their sabbatical. The presbytery solicited the Session’s approbation before taking final action to approve the moderator’s interim appointment of the ruling elder to the presbytery committee.

There is no evidence in the ROC that the Stated Clerk of CCP ever officially or formally communicated this request to the Session of CCC. According to the ROC, the Session of CCC took no action on the CCP request to approve the ruling elder for service on the presbytery committee prior to the next stated meeting of CCP, which was held on 4-26-97.

At that meeting, the moderator ruled on the status of the ruling elder’s appointment to the Candidates Committee of Presbytery. We believe that the moderator incorrectly interpreted CCC Session’s failure to act as constituting a “disapproval” of the ruling elder’s appointment. As a result, the moderator was in error when he ruled “null and void” the ruling elder’s interim appointment to the Candidates Committee of CCP.

The Session’s failure to act should have been interpreted by the moderator for precisely the action that it was: a failure to act on a request by presbytery. The moderator should have called the court’s attention to the

Session's failure to act and ruled that the ruling elder's interim appointment to the Credentials Committee of CCP would stand until the Session of CCC had taken action to formally "approve" or "not approve" the appointment, as the presbytery had requested. It is significant that no time frame was attached to the request for the Session of CCC to act on the CCP's request. And while it may have been assumed that approval of the elder's appointment was being sought by the next Stated Meeting, failure to achieve such approval due to inaction by the Session should not have been the basis for his appointment being rendered null and void. His interim status could have been continued until the Session complied with the presbytery's request at its earliest convenience.

In his Brief, the Respondent argued that the reason CCC Session took no action to confirm or nullify the appointment of the ruling elder was because: 1. The ruling elder had not made known to the Session his appointment to the presbytery committee and a request for action, and 2. He had removed his name from consideration at the 1-25-97 Stated Meeting of CCP. The ROC, however does not support this argument. It was not the ruling elder's responsibility to notify the Session of the presbytery's request for action on his interim appointment. It was the responsibility of CCP to formally notify the Session of CCC of the action it had taken. If formal communication did not take place between CCP and CCC, that would have served to only strengthen the necessity for the moderator to defer action on the status of the interim appointment. There is no evidence on the ROC that such communication between CCP and the Session of CCC took place. It would appear that the court erred in its failure to adequately communicate its action to the Session. Accountability for that resides with the court, not the individual who was the object of that proposed action.

Furthermore, the ROC does not indicate that the ruling elder in question had withdrawn his name from consideration for the committee appointment, as the Respondent contends.

The Session's failure to take action on the request for approval was just that: a failure to act. Failure to act, however, is not an action to disapprove. If the parliamentary motion that was approved by the presbytery had been "to lay the nomination on the table, pending action by the Session..." then the moderator's ruling would have been in order. But the motion was not to lay on the table. It was to request action by the Session. The ruling of the moderator, that the failure of the Session of CCC to act on the presbytery request rendered the ruling elder's nomination "null and void since no session approval was given" [ROC, p. 14] was an improper interpretation and application of the facts and constitutes reversible error in this case.

For the above stated reasons, we dissent in this decision. We believe that the complaint should have been sustained and remanded to presbytery for the proper action to be taken.
