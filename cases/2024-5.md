**IV. REASONING AND OPINION**

This Complaint involves five separate but related Complaints, each of which
has multiple underlying allegations. In judging these Complaints, we were
obliged to apply different review standards to the individual Complaints. We
find that Complaints 1, 3, 4, and 5 (Issues 1, 4, 5, and 6) are primarily matters
of discretion and judgment for which we must “ordinarily exhibit great
deference to [the] lower court” ( _BCO_ 39-3.3). The Presbytery’s out of order
rulings on Complaints 2-5 and Complaint 2 (Issues 2 and 3) are primarily
matters of constitutional interpretation for which we “are not obliged to give
the same deference to [the] lower court” ( _BCO_ 39-3.4).

**Reasoning for Issue 1 (Complaint 1)**

Complaint 1 alleges 11 actions on which NFP erred. The core of this
Complaint, however, is the allegation that “NFP erred in approving the
dissolution of the call of ‘Assistant Pastor of Christ Church East’ while the call

on file was that of ‘Planting Pastor for Christ Church Beaches’.”

Complainant argued that, while the original call may have, in effect, been as
Assistant Pastor at CCE, the intent was always that he would be “Planting
Pastor” at CCB, and that was the call that was before NFP at its stated meeting
of May 9, 2019. Complainant stated that he never understood the call that was
approved by Presbytery to be for an Assistant Pastor, and he stated he never
signed a call to be an Assistant Pastor. In support of his allegation that he was
called as “Planting Pastor” he cited the fact that the words “Assistant Pastor”
do not appear in the call, that the Order of Service for his ordination and the
minutes of the Commission that conducted the Service both state that the

constitutional questions were propounded to the Congregation of CCB rather
than the Session of CCE, that the Directories of NFP list him as “Planting
Pastor” of CCB, that the minutes of NFP list him only as “Organizing Pastor”
of NFP, that he was able to vote in CCE Session meetings only on matters
involving CCB, that he functioned as the Pastor of CCB for four years, and
that _BCO_ 5-4 does not allow for an Assistant Pastor to provide pastoral
ministry for a mission church.

We grant that some of the documents are not as clear as they should have been.
This is particularly true with regard to the minutes of the Session of CCE
surrounding their approval of TE Bigelow’s call. But one salient fact
remains—there is only one call document in the ROC. That call clearly came
from the action of the Session of CCE in the spring of 2019. The document,
which Presbytery’s minutes clearly label as a call to an Assistant Pastor,
contains all required terms of call and was the basis of TE Bigelow’s
ordination ( _BCO_ 20-1). There is no evidence in the ROC that this call from the
Session of CCE was ever dissolved prior to February 8, 2024, or that it was
ever transformed into a different call from some different calling body,
whether i tb ea pres byte ry ora congregation.

Further, there is evidence throughout the ROC that indicates NFP understood
TE Bigelow’s call to be an Assistant Pastor at CCE. That evidence includes
the clear language of the minutes of the NFP meeting of May 9, 2019, which

repeatedly refers to the call as that of an Assistant Pastor, the minutes of the
Ordination Commission which show that the questions to the “Congregation”
were in the form to be posed in the installation of an Assistant Pastor, the notes
of the Chairman of the Ordination Commission that show he understood the

installation was of an Assistant Pastor and that the questions should be directed
toward the elders, and the fact that NFP Directories consistently list TE
Bigelow as Assistant Pastor of CCE and “Planting Pastor” for CCB.

In addition, if TE Bigelow thought the May 9, 2019, NFP minutes incorrectly
recorded his call or that several years of Presbytery Directories were incorrect
in listing him as Assistant Pastor of CCE, he had plenty of opportunity to seek
corrections of those records. There is no evidence that he did so.

Finally, even Complainant’s own statement indicates his call was as Assistant
Pastor at CCE. On January 23, 2024, he wrote to the Provisional Session of
CCB asking that body to “Support my petition to MNA to transfer my call to
the NFP. It’s hard to imagine going through mediation with integrity with my
termination by East being held over my head.” This indicates that even in
January of 2024, Complainant understood his call was from CCE. Since there
is no evidence that the Congregation of CCE ever voted on any call to TE
Bigelow, such a call could only have been as an Assistant Pastor.

The procedure for the dissolution of a pastoral relationship is clear in _BCO_ 231. When either the pastor or the congregation (the calling body) desires to
terminate the pastoral relationship, the Presbytery shall hear from both parties
and then determine if the pastoral relation should be dissolved. In this case, the
Record makes clear, in spite of some confusion, that the call to TE Bigelow
was as Assistant Pastor of CCE and that the calling body was the Session of
CCE. The fact that TE Bigelow’s primary assignment by Session, and with the
concurrence of Presbytery, was to seek to plant a church in the Beaches area
of Jacksonville does not change the fact that the call was as Assistant Pastor.
As such, Session had every right, for whatever reasons seemed prudent to it,
to ask Presbytery to dissolve the call. It was incumbent on Presbytery, in turn,
to decide whether there was sufficient reason for the requested dissolution.
That decision is a matter of discretion and judgment, and we see no clear error
that would require the reversal of Presbytery’s action.

The other 10 actions alleged to be in error in Issue 1 (Complaint 1) were either
part of the action of dissolving the call or grew out of that dissolution. Thus,
they stand or fall with the validity of the action of NFP to dissolve the call.

For these reasons we find no clear error on the part of NFP in their decision to
dissolve TE Bigelow’s call and Complaint 1 is denied.

**Issue 2 (NFP’s Out of Order Rulings for Complaints 2-5 (Issues 3-6))**

At its meeting of May 9, 2024, NFP ruled out of order TE Bigelow’s
Complaints 2, 3, 4, and 5. Complaint 2 was ruled administratively out of order
on the grounds that, “since TE Kevin Bigelow was ordained as an assistant
pastor at Christ Church East, Jacksonville, FL, and that the Christ Church East
Session, not the North Florida Presbytery, lawfully dissolved the pastoral
relationship between Christ Church East and TE Kevin Bigelow.” Complaints
3-5 were ruled judicially out of order on the grounds that the actions
complained against were not the acts or decisions of Presbytery. On each of
these rulings Presbytery erred.

As was discussed above, it is true that the Session of CCE was the calling body
for TE Bigelow. It is not correct, however, that this fact means that the Session
of CCE could or did dissolve the Assistant Pastoral relationship between itself
and TE Bigelow. _BCO_ 23-1 is clear that it is Presbytery that makes the final
determination on the dissolution of a pastoral call. Presbytery’s understanding
of that point was evidenced by the fact that they acted at their meeting of
February 8, 2024, to “approve [Clerk’s note: support, as explained in the MNA
Committee’s report] Christ Church East Session’ ( _sic_ ) dissolution of TE
Bigelow’s call with Christ Church East as Assistant Pastor/Beaches Church
Planter ( _BCO_ 22-4, Cf.23-2).” Since it was the action of Presbytery that
ultimately determined that TE Bigelow’s call would be dissolved, he had every
right to complain that this action was not rightly brought before Presbytery.

Complaint 3 raised concerns about whether the dissolution of the pastoral
relationship was brought about as a form of church discipline. Since Presbytery
is the final arbiter with regard to whether a call should be dissolved, TE
Bigelow was within his rights to complain to Presbytery alleging they
dissolved his call as a form of discipline.

Complaints 4 and 5 raised concerns about the work of Presbytery’s Mediation
Commission and Presbytery’s actions with regard to mediation efforts. The
work of a Commission is the action of Presbytery. Further, in this case, part of
the complaints, in essence, raised concerns about the instructions and powers
Presbytery granted to the Mediation Commission. Thus, TE Bigelow was
within his rights to complain about the mediation efforts of Presbytery.

Normally, Presbytery’s error in ruling these Complaints out of order would
have resulted in us sending the matter(s) back to the lower court with
instruction that they conduct a new hearing. In this case, however, Presbytery’s
representative said during the hearing that if the SJC found Presbytery erred in
their out of order rulings he would prefer that the SJC immediately rule on the
substance of the Complaints. For that reason, the parties, in their briefs and
oral arguments, focused on the substance of Complaints 2-5. As a result, those
Complaints remain before the SJC for adjudication.

**Issue 3 (Complaint 2)**

Complaint 2 alleges 14 actions whereby Presbytery erred. The core issue in
Complaint 2 is Complainant’s allegation that NFP erred “in dissolving Christ
Church Beaches ( _sic_ ) relation with her pastor, TE Bigelow, ‘Planting Pastor of
Christ Church Beaches’, without holding a congregational vote to ascertain her
desire to be relieved of her pastor, without the tendered resignation of TE
Bigelow, and without TE Bigelow being under any form of formal discipline
or process.” Complainant further noticed that under _BCO_ 5-5 the members of
CCB “shall be understood to assent to the call of that minister and to affirm

the promises made to the pastor in _BCO_ 21-10.” The Complaint argues NFP
erred “in not recognizing that the receiving of members according to _BCO_ 55 into Christ Church Beaches established the pastoral relation directly between
Christ Church Beaches and her pastor, TE Bigelow, not TE Bigelow and any
Session.” Thus, according to Complainant, “The NFP erred in depriving Christ
Church Beaches of the pastoral care of her pastor when the pastoral relation
between Christ Church Beaches and TE Bigelow had never been
constitutionally dissolved.”

Part of Complaint 2 turns on the question of whether TE Bigelow’s call was
as an Assistant Pastor at CCE or the “Planting Pastor” at CCB. We dealt with
that question under Issue 1. There is a larger constitutional question in Issue 3
(Complaint 2). That is the question of the rights of members of a mission

church with regard to the dissolution of the pastoral relationship with the man
who has been pastoring them.

Complainant argued that the provisions of _BCO_ 5, especially 5-5, read in light
of Preliminary Principle 6, mean that members of a mission church have the
same relationship to their pastor, and, thus, the same rights in the case of an
attempt to dissolve that pastoral relationship, as do members o fan organized
church. If that assertion is correct, it would mean that, regardless of how a man
was originally engaged to pastor at a mission work, once members were
received the pastor could not be removed from the mission work absent a vote
of the congregation of the mission work or some act of discipline.

Much of Complainant’s argument is embodied in the following quote:
“consistent with the SJC Harrel ( _sic_ ) et al ruling, _BCO_ 5-2 mission churches
are identical in every way to particular churches save for their need for
temporary governance ( _BCO_ 4-1) while the ‘polity governance between
members of the mission church and its temporary government is the same as
between any congregation and its officers, but only during the time from the
mission church’s inauguration to the time of its particularization.’” This must
mean, according to Complainant, that pastors of mission works can be
removed only in the way that a pastor o fa particular church can be removed.

There are several problems with Complainant’s use of the Harrell decision.
First, the language quoted and the reasoning surrounding that language comes
not from the Harrell decision but from a concurring opinion signed by only
one judge. Thus, this cannot be said to be the ruling of the SJC. Further, the
point being made in the concurring opinion at the point cited by Complainant
is not about how pastoral relationships are dissolved in mission works or the
rights of members in that process. Rather, the concurring opinion is focusing
on the rights of members o fa mission work to call a pastor at the time of
particularization. While Complainant argues that CCB was on a path to
particularization at the point his call was terminated, the fact remains that the
case before us is about the dissolution of a call, not about the rights of a mission
church when they seek to call a pastor at particularization.

Of course, it is true that _BCO_ 5-5 says that members received in a mission
church “shall be understood to assent to the call of that minister and to affirm

the promises made to the pastor in _BCO_ 21-10.” But that language exists to
make clear that the members of the mission work have voluntarily agreed to

place themselves under the proper authority of a minister properly charged to
labor at the mission work, thus satisfying the principle that no man can be
placed over a congregation without their consent. That says nothing about how
a man can be removed from pa storing a mission work. By definition, a mission
work has a temporary form of government, and that government is provided
under arrangements determined by Presbytery, not by the congregation (see
_BCO_ 5-3). Until the point of particularization, the members of a mission work
have no right to determine the governing structure that will operate in the
mission work, no right to elect the men who will govern then, and no mandate
to consent to the dissolution of a man who is governing them, whether that
man beat e oran RE.

Complainant also argues that there must have been a pastoral relationship
between himself and CCB, which could be dissolved only with the consent of
the Congregation of CCB because the governing mechanisms set forth in _BCO_
5-4 do not make provision for an Assistant Pastor to function under any of the
arrangements. Presbytery argues that _BCO_ 5-4 i snot intended to be exhaustive,
hence the language of “may be provided,” so that Presbytery was within its
rights to assign an Assistant Pastor to work at a mission church. That may be
true, but the fact remains that even if it could be shown that Presbytery had
erred in assigning TE Bigelow, whose call was as an Assistant Pastor, to pastor
at CCB, that did not create a formal pastoral call between TE Bigelow and the
CCB congregation. Such a call could be established only at the time of the
particularization of CCB and only by a vote of the CCB congregation (and the
affirmation of Presbytery). None of those conditions were met in this case.

Of course, nothing in this analysis means that a Presbytery could not consult
with the congregation of a mission work before determining what governing
arrangement would be established, what men would be placed in that
government, or when a decision to remove a man from that government is
being considered. Indeed, such a course will often be wise. But saying that
Presbytery may, if it chooses, seek the advice of the congregation of a mission
work prior to removing a man from pastoring that work, is very different from
saying the congregation of the mission work has the right to vote on such a
removal. Nothing in our Constitution provides that right to the congregation
of a mission church. Thus Complaint 2 is denied.

**Issue 4 (Complaint 3)**

Complaint 3 alleges three actions whereby Presbytery erred. At the heart of
this Complaint is the allegation that Presbytery unconstitutionally used the
removal of TE Bigelow from pastoring CCB as an act of church discipline,
particularly when the removal was justified with the CCE Session, before
Presbytery, and to the CCB Congregation in terms of “the substantial conflict
surrounding Kevin.”

Putting the Complaint and Complainant’s brief together makes clear that there
are three prongs to this argument. The first prong is the argument that unless
there is a disciplinary process against the pastor, the only ways a pastoral
relationship can be dissolved are those set forth in _BCO_ 23-1—either the pastor
must tender his resignation or the Congregation must vote to ask to be relieved
of their pastor. Since TE Bigelow, the planting pastor of CCB, did not tender
his resignation, and since the Congregation of CCB did not vote to ask
Presbytery to dissolve the call, the only basis for the dissolution of the
relationship would have to be by discipline. There was clearly no formal
process in this matter, so it must have been that the dismissal constituted
discipline without process. As we discuss above, however, while TE Bigelow
was charged to work as a church planter at CCB, the only call in the ROC is
the call of CCE to TE Bigelow to be their Assistant Pastor. In that case the
Session of CCE is the calling body ( _BCO_ 22-3) and thus the Session of CCE
is the body that had the right to petition Presbytery to dissolve the relationship
( _BCO_ 22-4).

The second prong is that the rationale of the CCE Session for asking for the
dissolution, the rationale offered by Presbytery for approving the dissolution,
and the explanation of the dissolution to the CCB Congregation all focus on
things that TE Bigelow was perceived to have done wrong and the conflict
surrounding him. It is a sad reality that many pastoral relationships end
because of broken relationships, often accompanied by accusations against the
pastor. It is not true, however, that when a calling body seeks to have the
pastoral relationship dissolved due to unhappiness with the pastor or broken
relationships that Presbytery has exercised formal discipline that requires
process when it approves that dissolution. It is important to remember that
nothing in _BCO_ 23-1 (para 2) sets any limits on the reasons a calling body may
have for desiring to have the pastoral relationship ended, nor does 23-1 specify
what factors the Presbytery may or may not consider in determining whether

there is sufficient reason to dissolve the relationship. Thus, the Session of CCE
was entirely within its rights in this case to ask Presbytery to dissolve Session’s
relationship with TE Bigelow, and Presbytery was within its rights to grant
that request. Nothing in our Constitution requires that a pastor must be found
guilty of some offense before Presbytery can accede to the calling body’s
request that the pastoral relationship be dissolved. Additionally, no censure
was imposed in this matter. Nothing in Presbytery’s action prevents TE
Bigelow from seeking another call. Indeed, if or when CCB comes to organize,
nothing would prevent CCB from seeking to call TE Bigelow as their pastor.
Presbytery would then have to determine whether agreeing to that call would
be in the interest of religion, but that is a very different matter from the exercise
of formal church discipline.

The third prong focuses on statements that said either these actions were a
“form of discipline” or that discussed specific (formal) disciplinary measures
that one author thought the Presbytery might choose to take with regard to TE
Bigelow. With regard to the first of those concerns, it is important to remember
that _BCO_ 27-1 sets forth two aspects of church discipline. One refers to the
“whole government, inspection, training, guardianship, and control which the
church maintains over its members, officers, and courts.” The other has “a
more restricted and technical sense, signifying judicial process.” If a calling
body raises questions about its relationship with its pastor and if Presbytery
determines the relationship is so broken as to necessitate the dissolution of the
call, then Presbytery has exercised its disciplinary powers under the first
definition of _BCO_ 27-1, but it has not used the second definition. We agree
with Presbytery’s representatives that the word “discipline” or the phrase “in
some degree it is [discipline],” when used in the explanations of Presbytery’s
actions, was reflecting the fact that Presbytery had responded to the conflicts
surrounding TE Bigelow as allowed by the first use of the word “discipline”
in _BCO_ 27-1. We further note that the document that does suggest that formal
church discipline might be necessary is labeled as being part of a report from
a committee, and there is evidence that it may be the work of only one
individual. In either case, Presbytery did not take any action on that part of the
committee’s report, so it cannot be said to represent the action, or even the
opinion, of Presbytery.

Complaint 3 is, therefore, denied. There is not evidence that the action of the
CCE Session to seek the dissolution of the call was beyond their bounds, or
that the action of Presbytery in approving the dissolution was beyond their

proper authority. Further, while there is plenty of evidence that these actions
were taken to deal with a situation in which conflict had become problematic,
there is no evidence that any _BCO_ 30 censure was imposed. TE Bigelow was
and is still free to take the Lord’s Supper, to preach, and to seek another call.

**Issue 5 (Complaint 4)**

Complaint 4 alleges seven actions where the Mediation Commission of NFP
erred. Among the issues alleged are that the Mediation Commission did not
use a third party mediator, that it ended its efforts too soon, that it did not
require certain individuals to meet with each other, that it did not require
individuals to “own their sins” or to name the sins they believed others had
committed, and by not “engaging in ‘instruction in the word’ and admonishing
one another, the first steps in biblical discipline and reconciliation.”

While a formal mediation effort may often be useful in situations where there
is conflict, nothing in our Constitution requires mediation in the formal sense
discussed here, nor does our Constitution mandate what should be involved in
such mediation efforts. Thus, this Complaint involves a matter in which we
must grant great deference to the lower court. Presbytery erected and
empowered the Commission, and Presbytery gave the Commission the
suggestion of using a third-party mediator. Presbytery did not mandate the use
of a third party nor did Presbytery mandate that any set of individuals meet
with each other or the Mediation Commission (assuming Presbytery even has
that power). The Mediation Commission did the work it felt it needed to do.
That work may not have satisfied all parties, but that does not mean it was
unconstitutional, or even unwise. Further, there is no evidence that Presbytery
was dissatisfied with the work of the Commission.

In view of the above considerations, and given that this is an issue on which
we must give great deference to the lower court ( _BCO_ 39-3.2,3), we find no
clear error that would require us to sustain this Complaint. Complaint 4 is,
therefore, denied.

**Issue 6 (Complaint 5)**

Complaint 5 alleges two errors with regard to the mediation efforts of NFP.
One alleged error was that the process demonstrated partiality in that the
Administrative Committee, empowered as a commission to establish the

Mediation Commission, allowed itself to be “circularized” by meeting with
the Session of CCE and hearing additional concerns about TE Bigelow. This,
in Complainant’s view, led some members of the soon-to-be-appointed
Mediation Commission to call for TE Bigelow’s resignation. In Complainant’s
view, this partiality also went to failures on the part of the Mediation
Commission to appropriately seek truth from ones with whom they were
involved, and to give “a substantially incomplete summary report” to NFP.
The second errant action was in ending the mediation effort without the
involvement of a third party.

We have already discussed the latitude a Presbytery has in determining
whether to establish a mediation process and in how that process is to be
conducted. The Mediation Commission, acting for Presbytery, had that same
latitude, so long as they operated within the parameters set by Presbytery when
the Commission was established. While Complainant may have wished the
Commission would have acted in different ways, there is no evidence that there
was any constitutional violation. Further, the requirement that one not
“circularize the court” is used in the _BCO_ only in the context of formal process
(see 42-4 and 43-2). As there was no formal process in view here, there was
no bar to anyone talking to either Commission or to whom either Commission
could speak. Finally, while Complainant may not have gotten the result he
wanted from the Mediation Commission, there is no evidence that any action
was taken against him by the Commission. Certainly it is true that Complainant
argues that sins against himself and his family were left unredressed, but the
SJC has no ability to assess or redress that based on a Complaint about the
work of the Mediation Commission. Therefore, Complaint 5 is denied.

**Final Comments**

We are certainly saddened to see this evidence of conflict in NFP. We do not,
however, have the authority or the information to seek to redress, based on
these Complaints, whatever wrongs were committed that originated or
prolonged the conflict that led to this Case. As such, this Decision makes no
comment on allegations raised by or against any of the parties involved, nor
do we reach any conclusion as to whether the various decisions of NFP were
wise. Our decision is limited to an assessment of the constitutional issues

raised by Complainant and the question of whether Presbytery committed
“clear error” in any of its decisions. We conclude that Complainant’s argument
about the relationship established between a mission work and the church

planting pastor does not withstand constitutional scrutiny. We also find that
NFP erred constitutionally in declaring four of the Complaints to be out of
order, but such error was harmless in this case in that the merits of the
Complaints were argued before and decided on by this body. Further, we
conclude that on the other issues either the facts favored the position of
Respondent Presbytery or there was no evidence to indicate Presbytery had
committed clear error.

Finally, we do note that had Session done a better job of documenting its call
process in its minutes, had some documents been more clearly written and
labeled, and had some chosen words more carefully, some of these issues
might not have had to come before us. Thus, we urge all courts of the PCA to
take more care to be sure their records are clear and that they include all the
information that might be necessary in case a Complaint should arise against
them.

**CASE 2024-05**

**TE KEVIN BIGELOW v.**

**NORTH FLORIDA PRESBYTERY**

#### DECISION ON COMPLAINT

#### Concurring Opinion
TE David F. Coffin, Jr., joined by RE Steve Dowling

March 24, 2025

I readily concurred with the Statement of the Issues, the Judgment, and
the Reasoning and Opinion in the above-named decision. However, in the
Summary of Facts, at “08/26/23,” one finds:

NFP held a called meeting to “discuss the relationship between TE
Kevin Bigelow and the Christ Church East Session and to receive
recommendations from the Administrative Committee regarding these
matters.” TE Keith Dickerson, Pastor of CCE, presented a
communication on behalf of Session wherein he laid out some of

Session’s concerns regarding TE Bigelow. He also stated that, based
on a conversation with [an authority], . . . Session was of the opinion
that it could either deal with its concerns regarding TE Bigelow via
formal process or “according to BCO 12-5-e, [it] could use the
authority granted to a session to remove a teaching elder from the
pulpit.” (SJC 2024-05 Bigelow v. North Florida Presbytery, Decision
on Complaint, p. 3.)

Although according to the Record of the Case, Presbytery, rightly, _did not_
_accede_ to this claim of authority, Presbytery did so on other grounds, and did

not need to address the interpretation alleged. That notwithstanding, it is of
some importance to point out the error of the Session’s interpretation of _BCO_
12-5.e.:

. . . to ensure that the Word of God is preached only by such men as
are sufficiently qualified ( _BCO_ 4-4, 53-2, 1 Timothy 2:11-12). . . .

Some, apparently even some with standing in such matters, have supposed that
this provision gives the Session the right, at any time, to inspect the
qualifications of the installed minister of the church, and if he is found
wanting, the Session, by majority vote, may remove him from the pulpit. This
is a serious mistake, and I would not have my concurrence be understood to
give it credence. such a view is a profound threat to our scriptural polity, and
to the purity and peace of the church.
First, as a matter of our polity overall, this _is not_, and _cannot_ be, the
meaning of this provision. Under our polity the Session has nothing
whatsoever to do with the qualifications or fitness of the minister. In most
cases, the Congregation, exclusively, calls the minister to the pulpit, and
Presbytery, exclusively, approves his qualifications and installs the minister.
The minister may not, as a censure, be removed against his will from that work
apart from due process exclusively before the Presbytery ( _BCO_ 34-1; 13-9.a;
13-9.c.; 13-9.d.). He may be removed from the work without censure, but only
at his own request, or at the request of the Congregation, and, in either case,
the concurrence of the Presbytery is necessary ( _BCO_ 23-1). The only exception
to this analysis is the case of an Assistant Pastor. Here the Session calls the
minister and Presbytery approves the qualifications and installs the minister.
Yet here too, the minister may not, as a censure, be removed against his will
from that work apart from due process exclusively before the Presbytery. He
may be removed from the work without censure, but only at his own request,
or at the request of the Session, and, in either case, the concurrence of the
Presbytery is necessary.
Second, to understand what _BCO_ 12-5.e. _does_ provide for, one must
understand something of its historical context, as it is relatively new to the
_BCO_ . The language of _BCO_ 12-5.e., “. . . to ensure that the Word of God is
preached only by such men as are sufficiently qualified ( _BCO_ 4-4, 53-2, 1
Timothy 2:11-12). . . .” was added to chapter 12 at the 30 [th] General Assembly
in 2002 (cf. _M30GA_, 30-10, Item 6, p. 71-72). The addition was proposed to
remedy the problem that some ministers and Sessions in the PCA apparently
believed that so long as they agreed not to have women _in office_, women could,

nonetheless, be allowed to preach or teach in services of the church that include
the men and women of the church. The proposal in question came only two
years after an infamous case that brought such a view before the Assembly
judicially, causing confusion and controversy in its wake.
An overture was proposed to the 29 [th] GA in 2001 as Overture 27, from
the Presbytery of Philadelphia to “Amend _BCO_ 12-5e to Prohibit Women from
Preaching” (See _M29GA_, 29-44, III, 18, p. 223). The Overture was answered
in the affirmative, as amended by a Minority Report of the Bills and Overtures
Committee, and sent down to the presbyteries. The requisite number of
presbyteries approved.
The rationale for the amendment was set forth as follows:

GROUNDS:

1. It is absolutely clear that Scripture prohibits women preaching;
2. Some within the PCA have questioned whether our standards

prohibit women from preaching.
3. This amendment places responsibility for ensuring that women do

not preach at the feet of the session where it belongs; and
4. The amendment properly belongs in the section of the _BCO_ which

discusses the duties of the session.

One can clearly see from this account, as the amendment was put
before the Assembly and the Presbyteries, this provision has nothing to do with
the Session inspecting the qualifications of the _installed_ minister of the church,
but only has to do with making sure that only qualified _men_ fill the pulpit in
the minister’s absence (cf. _BCO_ 53-6, “No person should be invited to preach
in any of the churches under our care without the consent of the Session.”).
