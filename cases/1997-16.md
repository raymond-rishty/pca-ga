From the opinion of my learned and eminent brothers, I must respectfully
dissent. this i do in two respects:
1 Concurring with six other brothers, I am persuaded that at the very least
this case required a remand to Central Carolina Presbytery (CCP) in
view of the Moderator’s improper conclusion that Ruling Elder Dollar’s
appointment was “null and void.”
2. Far more serious and far reaching, I must further dissent because the
majority decision strikes a blow atthe essence of Biblical
Presbyterianism in denying to CCP the rightto choose its own
committee members (which would also include officers and commission
members) from among those qualified to serve in such positions
i am cognizant that in my view on (2) I apparently stand alone among my
distinguished brothers. However, I have served 37 years as a Presbyterian elder, and
the notion that an elder in good standing and called by God can be precluded from the
congregation strikes me as most novel and dangerous.
The record of the case (ROC) is brief, for indeed the basic facts are not
complicated. Significant additional material included in the Respondent’s Brief was
required to be deleted by the Panel, and properly so, because it could not be found in the
ROC. I will first discuss the facts and their implications chronologically.

On January 6, 1997, the moderator o fcc p appointed re dollar to the
Candidates Committee pursuant to the presbytery’s Manual IV A. This was an
“interim” appointment subject to final approval at the next Stated Meeting. An interim
appointment is one made “for the time in the interval.” It was not conditional or
contingent. The appointment having been properly made by the Moderator, any action
by RE Dollar while serving on the Candidates Committee in the interim until the next
Stated Meeting was presumably valid. The fact that RE Dollar was not actively serving
as an elder under the congregation’s policy of required periodic sabbaticals doe snot
detract from the fact that he was qualified by virtue of his ordination He was in good
standing and not under any discipline, as admitted by the Respondent
On January 11, 1997, CCP’s Administrative Committee approved RE Dollar’s
interim appointment and recommended that CCP affirm their approval However, when
the CCP met on January 25, 1997, an amendment was approved to the motion to the
effect that approval be given “pending approval” by RE Dollar’s Session.
Much discussion was given by the SJC as to what was intended by “pending
approval.” It would seem that the best understanding would be that “pending” meant
“not yet approved.” In the circumstances, then, since the interim appointment was only
valid until the next Stated CCP meeting, a motion to appoint RE Dollar “pending”
approval by his Session, regardless of whether such was a proper action for CCP to
take, had the effect of removing Mr. dollar from his status as a member of the
committee until such time as his Session acted or it could be determined what length of
time the appointment could be left “pending.”
Sadly, in the interval between the stated meeting of january 25, 1997 and the
next Stated Meeting on April 26, 1997, the session of re Dollar’s church, Christ
Covenant (CC), did nothing whatsoever respecting the appointment. Respondent in its
brief dated January 2, 1998, argued that Session’s inaction was because Mr. Dollar had
not made known any nomination or appointment to the Session and “had removed his
name from consideration at the january meeting of Presbytery.” The latter statement
has no support in the ROC and must be disregarded.
a full quotation from the minutes of the stated meeting o fcc p on april 26,
1997 (ROC 14), as distinguished from the Majority’s abbreviated version, shows as
follows:
“Regarding the interim appointment of RE Chris Dollar to the

The sentence is grammatically incorrect in that no subject is given to predicate clause
“was null and void.” It seems clear, however, that the intended subject was “the interim

PRELIMINARY COMMENT - It was argued by some Commission members
that CCP’s action of January 25th with respect to the Moderator’s appointment can not
now be challenged, since more than 30 days had elapsed before it was said to be
erroneous. This seems to the undersigned to be a very hypertechnical reading of the
events before us. The whole pattern of events beginning with the Moderator’s initial
interim appointment and extending through the April CCP meeting constitutes one
fabric and the statute of limitations should not be tolled on what is merely a segment of
that fabric. The mandate of the SJC to bring about Biblical justice under Scripture and
t hep ca framework of church order should not be frustrated on the basis of such a
highly technical argument in a closely interrelated pattern of events such as is here
present
Issue 1 At the April 1997 meeting of CCP, the Moderator ruled that the January
25th appointment of RE Dollar, which the January Presbytery had only
confirmed “pending” Christ Covenant’s “approval,” was null and void because
no sessional approval had been given. The difficulty with this “null and void”
ruling, however, is that no reference point had been established for the word
“pending.” Contrary to Complainant’s argument (ROC 3), the undersigned does
not believe RE Dollar was “removed from the Candidates Committee on
January 25, 1997.” His earlier interim appointment ended when Presbytery met
on January 25, 1997, and Presbytery did not choose to appoint him immediately
to the committee but only “pending approval by the CC Session.” (ROC 12).
But there was no specific provision made with respect to how long the “pending
approval” by Christ Covenant might remain pending.
The fact is, Christ Covenant Session did not act between the two
Presbyteries, and their argument that this was because RE Dollar had not told
them about the situations e ems specious in light of the fact that representatives
from the church were present during the January Presbytery meeting.
Consequently, the action of the Moderator in April declaring the January
appointment “null and void” was clearly arbitrary and erroneous. He had no
authority to conclude that the non-action of Christ Covenant Session between
January and April constituted an action which served to bring an end to the
matter. This is particularly true because of the admission in the Respondent’s
brief that exceptions can be granted by the Christ Covenant Session from its
policy of not allowing elders on sabbatical to serve on presbytery committees
Lacking any information to indicate that Christ Covenant Session had
considered and denied Presbytery’s tentative choice of re dollar to serve o nits
Candidates Committee, the Moderator erred in his ruling of “null and void” and
CCP erred in sustaining him. Thus, at the very least this case should have been
returned for proper handling by CCP.
Issue 2 Issue 1 is minuscule when compared with the much greater significance
of Issue 2. Respondent argues in its brief that the issue is “connectional
Presbyterianism” and the “grassroots” distinctive soft hep ca as opposed to
“hierarchical Presbyterianism.”

Contrary to this, the undersigned strongly affirms that once again, this is an issue of “Will the PCA be Presbyterian” or will we water down our historic principles of Biblical church order with notions which smack of independency. If the Presbytery is deprived of its original jurisdiction and power to select its own committee members and officers from among those fully qualified by Scripture and by ordination, the framework of Biblical Presbyterianism will be greatly weakened, and presbyterian integrity will be seriously eroded. We need to remind ourselves, however contrary it may seem to that zeal for democratic ideals which is so peculiarly true of Americans, that the Word of God does not teach of a spiritual edifice where power arises from the consent of the governed, but rather of a glorious kingdom whose sovereign is King Jesus. He has given to us the principles for the framework of our system of graded church courts, and the undersigned is persuaded that the sessional policy described in this case represents a tendency toward democratization of the Church which is inconsistent with that system.

Much is made at times of the concept of “grassroots.” We need to be careful of the application of such a concept. If “grassroots” means not only that local congregations should be guarded against unwarranted and tyrannical intrusion by higher courts (which I think we would all affirm), but also that the local Session can dictate to the Presbytery, by depriving qualified men from consideration, as to who the Presbytery’s committee members and officers shall be, we have gone too far. “Grassroots,” as thus applied, will have been shown to contain noxious weeds.

But, some would inquire, are you saying that all Ruling Elders should be eligible for participation in Presbytery meetings? No, of course not. It has long been determined that in order to achieve a measure of parity among the churches and between Teaching and Ruling Elders, individual churches should commission only a proportionate number for the Presbytery’s periodic meetings. But the work of Presbytery consists of a great deal more than what transpires at those meetings, and Presbytery surely has jurisdiction to select ordained Ruling Elders in good standing as it sees fit. For the Session, under the guise of “shepherding” its Ruling Elders, to foreclose totally to one or more of them an area of service requested by the Presbytery constitutes the very opposite of “hierarchical” Presbyterianism. It is a kind of Sessional tyranny unwarranted by Scripture. And this is true regardless of whether Ruling Elders are classed as “active,” “elders on sabbatical,” or “inactive” (a term not known to Scripture).

“...God alone makes elders. God makes elders, and the church’s duty is to discern which men God has given to the Church for teaching and ruling...God qualifies men with spiritual gifts; but the church must learn how to recognize those men so qualified and then must proceed to set them apart to the holy office of elder.” Lawrence R. Eyres, The Elders of the Church (Phillipsburg, NJ: Presbyterian and Reformed Publishing Co., 1975), pp. 7-9. If Mr. Eyres was correct in this incisive and helpful little book, and the undersigned believes that he was, the implications for this case are obvious. We must presume that Christ Covenant Church recognized Mr. Dollar’s gifts,

elected him, and ordained and installed him into the office of Ruling Elder, thus
confirming his calling by God to serve in that capacity. That range of duties
which any qualified Ruling Elder may carry out includes not only the many
responsibilities of the local church, which customarily are divided among the
elders based on principles of prudence and common sense, but also service to
the Presbytery in areas where his gifts are noted by the Presbytery as a whole to
be those such as would be of benefit to that higher court.
the calling of the ruling elder is avery high one, indeed 1 The
undersigned vigorously denies that the Session, for other than disciplinary
reasons, may act, by so-called “policy,” to prevent a duly elected, ordained, and
installed Ruling Elder from exercising God’s calling to him in the work of
Presbytery. If the Pastor or other Elders in a local congregation believe there are
providential reasons for advising presbytery against the selection of a particular
Ruling Elder, then let them make that advice known and then consent to the will
of Presbytery They have no proper “power,” however, in the undersigned’s
judgment, to impose their will upon Presbytery, which has original jurisdiction
in the selection of its committee members and officers. The original motion of
CCP to take an action, “pending” a local Session’s approval, should have been
seen to be abhorrent in its very form.
While this case will not be submitted to the General Assembly in 1998, the

will be noted by the Church at large and corrected.

## II. Statement of the Issue

1. Did Central Carolina Presbytery err when it declared null and void the appointment of a ruling elder to serve on a presbytery committee because his Session had failed to act on presbytery’s request to approve him?

## III. Judgment in the Case

1. Yes.

## IV. Reasoning and Opinion

The circumstance in this case centers on a request by a presbytery to a Session to “approve” a ruling elder to serve on a presbytery committee while he was not currently serving on the Session.

The issue is this: Did the presbytery err when it declared null and void the appointment of a ruling elder to serve on a presbytery committee because his Session had failed to act on presbytery’s request to approve him?

The Record of the Case [ROC] reflects that on 1-6-97, the moderator of Central Carolina Presbytery [CCP] made an interim appointment of a ruling elder from Christ Covenant Church [CCC] to serve on the Candidates Committee of the presbytery. According to the bylaws of CCP, the appointment was subject to the final approval of the presbytery at its next stated meeting [ROC, p. 5].

At the next Stated Meeting of CCP on 1-25-97, the presbytery adopted a motion to approve the interim appointment that included this amendment: “pending approval by the CCC Session.” [ROC, p. 12]. The rationale for CCP seeking the Session’s approval was based on the fact that the elder was “on sabbatical” from his active service as an elder on the Session of CCC. The presbytery was informed that the church had a stated policy that ruling elders should not serve on presbytery and General Assembly committees duties during the tenure of their sabbatical. The presbytery solicited the Session’s approbation before taking final action to approve the moderator’s interim appointment of the ruling elder to the presbytery committee.

There is no evidence in the ROC that the Stated Clerk of CCP ever officially or formally communicated this request to the Session of CCC. According to the ROC, the Session of CCC took no action on the CCP request to approve the ruling elder for service on the presbytery committee prior to the next stated meeting of CCP, which was held on 4-26-97.

At that meeting, the moderator ruled on the status of the ruling elder’s appointment to the Candidates Committee of Presbytery. We believe that the moderator incorrectly interpreted CCC Session’s failure to act as constituting a “disapproval” of the ruling elder’s appointment. As a result, the moderator was in error when he ruled “null and void” the ruling elder’s interim appointment to the Candidates Committee of CCP.
