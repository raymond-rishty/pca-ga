Case 2001-4
APPEAL OF RE MOON K. HAM
VS.
KOREAN CAPITAL PRESBYTERY

#### I. Statement of the Facts

1. Beginning in 1999, division developed within the leadership of Korean
Presbyterian Church Washington (KPCW). Initially the dispute centered
principally on the question about the finances of Washington Theological
Seminary which was connected with KPCW.
2. On December 30, 1999 the Session of KPCW submitted to Korean
Capital Presbytery (KCP) a reference asking KCP to help the session address
the financial question referred in item #1.
3. The situation escalated to the point that there were two groups both
claiming to be the true Session of KPCW. There was reported physical
violence at the church and police were called to resolve disputes. The
congregation was advised by civil authority that if the turmoil continued, the
church might be prohibited from meeting.
4. On June 14, 2000 REs Charles Kim and Moon K. Ham held a session
meeting and suspended RE David K. Han.
5. REs Kim and Ham on September 7, 2000 held another session meeting
and voted to have KPCW withdraw from KCP and restored Choon Soon Lee
and Joo Bok Suh as active elders without a vote of the congregation.
6. Certain church officers including RE Moon K. Ham sought in civil
court to have existing church trustees replaced without congregational
approval.
7. KCP subsequently received requests a) to investigate financial
mismanagement, b) a petition from members of the congregation of KPCW
asking KCP to dismiss RE Ham and c) letters reporting on “divisive and
destructive activities of REs Charles Kim and Moon K. Ham.” A total of 16
references, petitions, accusations and complaints were filed with KCP
regarding the situation at KPCW.
8. To deal with these 16 petitions, references, complaints and
accusations, on October 9, 2000 at the reconvened portion of its 17th Stated
Meeting KCP appointed a commission to “suspend the functions of the KPCW
church Session and have the Presbytery replace the functions and report to the
Presbytery meeting in three months.”
9. The Presbytery Commission, known as the Administrative
Commission and the Judicial Commission (it had been assigned both
functions), began its work on October 16, 2000 and commenced to act for the
session.

10. On October 15, 2000 the Commission arrived at KPCW to lead the
Sunday worship service but was prevented from doing so by members of the
congregation.
11. On October 16, 2000 three Ruling Elders and a Teaching Elder, acting
as the interim moderator of KPCW advised the commission that “the
undersigned fully recognize the authority of the Commission and Judicial
Commission formed by the decision made at the 17th Stated Presbytery
meeting...”
12. The Commission on October 20, 2000 sought and ultimately received
a civil court order “to protect the church properties and to secure the safety of
church members.” The order restrained certain Ruling Elders including RE
Ham from interfering with the commission.
13. The KCP commission summoned RE Ham to appear at a meeting on
December 16, 2000 to examine him and hear testimony before deciding on
whether to discipline and censure. RE Ham did not appear and he was cited to
appear on December 30, 2000.
14. At the meeting on December 30, 2000 RE Ham was suspended from
the sacraments for three years.
15. At a called meeting of KCP on January 8, 2001 Presbytery approved
the actions of its commission including its censure of RE Ham.
16. On January 17, 2001 RE Ham appealed his censure to the Standing Judicial Commission.
17. Appellant and Appellee waived hearing of the case and a five-man
panel of the SJC decided the appeal on December 5,2001 at the McLean
Presbyterian Church in McLean, VA.

#### II. Statement of the Issue

Was Presbytery and/or its Commission appointed on October 9, 2000
the proper court to assume original jurisdiction to receive and adjudicate the
charges against the Appellant Charles C. Kim?

#### III. Judgment

No, such original jurisdiction should be with the Session; therefore,
the Presbytery’s action and/or the Judicial Commission of Presbytery’s actions
in this matter are reversed.

#### IV. Reasoning and Opinion

The court of original jurisdiction for communicant members in any
local church is the Session of that church. The Appellant in this case was a
member of the Korean Presbyterian Church of Washington and thus, any
judicial action or censure against a member must originate within the court of
original jurisdiction, the Session. Since neither the KCP nor its Commission
was the proper court of original jurisdiction, the KCP Commission’s action to

adjudicate charges against the Appellant was contrary to the constitutional
standards, and the action of the lower court is reversed.
The Statement of the Facts of this case was prepared by RE John
White. The Issue and Judgment were prepared by the entire Panel of five
members. The Reasoning and Opinion was written by TE Jim Smith with
concurrence by TE Charles McGowan, RE Jack Williamson, RE Grant
McCabe and RE John White.

Case 2001-7
COMPLAINT OF TE ANDY LEE
VS.
KOREAN CAPITAL PRESBYTERY

#### I. Statement of the Facts

1. Beginning in 1999, division developed within the leadership of Korean
Presbyterian Church Washington (KPCW). Initially the dispute centered
principally on questions about the finances of Washington Theological
Seminary which was connected with KPCW.
2. On December 30, 1999 the Session of KPCW submitted to Korean
Capital Presbytery (KCP) a reference asking KCP to help the session address
the financial question referred to in item 1.
3. The situation escalated to the point that there were two groups both
claiming to be the true Session of KPCW. There was reported physical
violence at the church and police were called to resolve disputes. The
congregation was advised by civil authority that if the turmoil continued the
church might be prohibited from meeting.
4. Subsequently, additional requests were made of KCP a) to investigate
financial mismanagement, b) a petition from members of the congregation of
KPCW asking KCP to dismiss RE Charles Kim and c) letters reporting on

“divisive and destructive activities of REs Charles Kim and Moon K. Ham.”
A total of 16 references, petitions, accusations and complaints were filed with
KCP regarding the situation at KPCW.
5. Certain KPCW officers sought in civil court to have existing church
trustees replaced without congregational approval.
6. To deal with these 16 petitions, references, complaints and
accusations, on October 9, 2000, at the reconvened portion of its 17th Stated
Meeting, KCP appointed a commission to “suspend the functions of the
KPCW church Session and have the Presbytery replace the functions and
report to the Presbytery meeting in three months.”
7. The Presbytery Commission, known as the Administrative
Commission and the Judicial Commission (it had been assigned both
functions), began its work on October 16, 2000 and commenced to act for the
session.
8. On October 15, 2000 the Commission arrived at KPCW to lead the
Sunday worship service but was prevented from doing so by members of the
congregation.
9. On October 16, 2000 three Ruling Elders and a Teaching Elder, acting
as the interim moderator of KPCW advised the commission that “the
undersigned fully recognize the authority of the Commission and Judicial
Commission formed by the decision made at the 17th Stated Presbytery
meeting...”
10. On October 30, 2000 TE Andy Lee complained about the action of the
KCP in suspending the KPCW session and appointing a Commission to
replace the session. That complaint was denied by KCP at its stated meeting
on April 2, 2001.
11. TE Lee filed his complaint with the Standing Judicial Commission and
a five-man panel of the SJC heard the complaint on December 4, 2001 at the
McLean Presbyterian Church in McLean, VA.

#### II. Statement of the Issue

1. Did the Korean Capital Presbytery (KCP) err in its action of October
9, 2000, in suspending the Session of the Korean Presbyterian Church of
Washington (KPCW) and appointing a Commission to act for the KPCW
Session without the prior consent of the congregation?
2. Are the subsequent actions of the commission, acting as the KPCW
Session, in accordance with the BCO?

#### III. Judgment

1. Yes
2. No, therefore, all subsequent actions of the commission acting as the
Session of KPCW are annulled.

#### IV. Reasoning and Opinion

The issue in this case has to do with whether or not the Korean Capital
Presbytery had the BCO authority to suspend the Session of the Korean
Presbyterian Church of Washington and act for the Session of KPCW in the
manner set forth by the facts of the case.
An examination of the facts in the case is sufficient to establish that
there was great distress and turmoil within the corporate life of the Korean
Presbyterian Church of Washington (KPCW) as a result of allegations of
financial impropriety at the Washington Theological Seminary, a ministry of
the KPCW. On December 30, 1999 the Session of KPCW requested the
assistance of the Korean Capital Presbytery (KCP) by mean sofa reference
(BCO 41-1 and ROC p. 5 Attachment 1) in which the Session of KPCW asked
the KCP for “final disposition of these matters”. This request became the basis
for the Korean Capital Presbytery, at its 17th Stated Meeting on October 9,
2000, for taking the following actions: KCP suspend the Session of KPCW
and its functions for three months according to BCO 13-9 and appoint a
Commission to be clothed with judicial powers to resolve all matters of
conflict at KPCW.
The KCP’s appointed Commission acted for the Session of KPCW by
approving various assignments of duties and responsibilities of members of the
Commission for the Session functions, such as worship, finance,
administration and building management.
The KCP maintained that the presence of division and conflict within
the KPCW was such that the KPCW Session could no longer carry out its BCO
responsibilities. The KCP determined that a state of “anarchy prevailed, the
KPCW had completely lost its governing function and, practically speaking,
the KPCW Session ceased to function and exist” (Respondent’s primary brief
p. 3). The issue is whether or not the Korean Capital Presbytery had the BCO
authority to suspend the Session of the Korean Presbyterian Church of
Washington and act for the Session of KPCW without the prior approval of the
KPCW congregation.
The action of KCP to suspend the KPCW Session and appoint a
Commission to act for the KPCW Session was based on BCO 13-9, which
states that
The Presbytery has power to receive and issue appeals, complaints and
references brought before it in an orderly manner. In cases in which
the Session cannot exercise its authority, it shall have power to assume
original jurisdiction...
The Complainant correctly argued, however, that the BCO 13-9 can
not be understood in isolation from other relevant portions of the BCO. For
example, Preliminary Principle Six states in part
Though the character, qualifications and authority of Church officers
are laid down in the Holy Scriptures, as well as the proper method of

officer investiture, the power to elect persons to the exercise of
authority in any particular society resides in that society.
(emphasis added)
Furthermore, BCO 3-1 underscores the right of the congregation to be
governed by those duly elected by that body.
The power which Christ has committed to His Church vests in the
whole body, the rulers and those ruled, constituting it a spiritual
commonwealth. This power, as exercised by the people, extends to
the choice of those officers whom He has appointed in His Church.
The language of BCO 16-2 confirms the principle of the consent of the
governed as being foundational to our denomination’s understanding of
Biblical church government.
The government of the Church is by officers gifted to represent Christ,
and the right of God’s people to recognize by election to office those
so gifted is inalienable. Therefore no man can be placed over a
church in any office without the election, or at least the consent of
that church.” (Emphasis added)
The BCO of the PCA is clear when it speaks to the issue of
jurisdiction. The higher court may not act for the lower court to remove from
office those duly elected without the prior consent or request of the lower
court. “.. .The right of God’s people to recognize by election to office those so
gifted is inalienable...” (BCO 16-2). Thus, without the prior consent of the
lower court, a higher court may not act to dismiss or suspend the duly elected
officers of the lower court and replace them with others.
Based on the ROC, the KCP did not have the prior consent of the
congregation of KPCW to suspend its ruling elders and appoint a Presbytery
Commission to act for the Session of KPCW. The Korean Capital Presbytery’s
action of October 9, 2000 to do just that, to suspend the Session of the KPCW
without the prior consent of the congregation (BCO 24-6) and to form a
Presbytery Commission to act as the Session of KPCW was, in fact, a violation
of the BCO of the PCA.
The KCP Commission was erected and clothed with powers to act for
the Session of KPCW in a manner not provided for in the BCO. Thus, all
subsequent actions of the KCP Commission, appointed by the Presbytery to act
as the Session of KPCW, are annulled insofar as the Commission was not
constituted in accordance with BCO requirements.
While the ROC indicates that both the Session of KPCW and the
congregation of KPCW took action in which each attempted to recognize the
legitimacy of the KCP Commission to act for the Session of KPCW, such
actions were subsequent to and not prior to the KCP’s action to suspend the
Session and form the Commission. Furthermore, the congregational meeting
of December 3, 2000 in which the congregation did accede to the authority of
the KCP commission was a meeting called by the KCP Commission acting for

the Session. Since the Commission lacked BCO authority to act for the
Session, this congregational meeting was not in accordance with the BCO and
all actions resulting from such a meeting would have to be regarded as null and
void.
One final issue raised by the KCP does bear some consideration. The
KCP argued in its brief that case 2001 -7 was not properly before the SJC since
it believed the issues in this case to be identical with those found in case 2001-
1 (the Appeal of RE Charles C. Kim v. KCP). The Respondent for KCP
argued that “no complaint is allowable in a judicial case in which an appeal is
taken” (BCO 43-1). The SJC is of the opinion, however, that Case 2001-7 is
properly before it since the issues in the cases 2001-1 and 2001-7 are
fundamentally different and thus BCO 43-1 does not pertain.
