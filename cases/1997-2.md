#### II. Statement of the Issues

Did Rocky Mountain Presbytery err in receiving and immediately acting upon a
motion to cite Rev. Bruce Nickoley for contumacy and rendering the judgment to
depose him from the ministry without giving him reasonable opportunity to defend
himself under the due process provisions for a trial as outlined in the Book of Church
Order?

#### III. Judgment

Yes. This case is remanded to Rocky Mountain Presbytery with the
recommendation that it either (a) institute a prosecution against Rev. Bruce Nickoley as
a case under process on the charge of contumacy and/or other charges under the due
process provisions of the Book of Church Order for a trial, beginning with BCO §32-3.
Or (b) revoke the judgment of deposition from the ministry against Rev. Bruce
nickoley and reinstate hi mas a member of rocky mountain presbytery in good
standing.

#### IV. Reasoning and Opinion

Appellee relies on several Book of Church Order (BCO) provisions in its
justification for its action in “deposing from ministry” Rev. Bruce Nickoley. In the
ROC and in Appelle’s brief, app el lee relies o nits interpretations of b co provisions
§38-1, 34-4, 32-6, and 35-12 as constitutional justification for this action. We are of the
opinion that r mp has misinterpreted the applicability of these b co provisions to this
case. we shall give our reasoning and opinion as to the proper interpretation of these
BCO provisions to this case.

BCO §38-1
BCO §38-1 deals w ithaca se without process where a person “shall come
forward and make his offense known to the court.” RMP cites this provision as its
constitutional basis for judging that “Rev. Bruce Nickoley by refusal to submit to the
order of Rocky Mountain Presbytery admits his contumacy ” (ROC II-A-6). Likewise,
Appellee’s brief contains these arguments, to-wit:
1. “Because TE Nickoley continued to refuse to submit to RM P’s Order,
RMP found said refusal to be an admission of guilt for his contumacious
behavior.”
2. “TE Nickoley implicitly confessed his contumacy by continually
refusing to obey the Court’s order in the presence of all the presbyters.”
3. “Contumacious behavior is not an ordinary statement in the presence of
the Court It is sinful behavior which shows a lack of respect for the
Court Such rebellious and obstinate behavior is in actuality making his
offense known to the Court.”
4. “Failing to repent, TE Nickoley ‘made his offense known to the Court’
and left the presbytery no choice except to find him guilty of contempt .”
Thus by relying on BCO §38-1 as a justification for its judgment, RMP admits
that it did not follow the regular due process for a trial as prescribed by the BCO We
agree with Dr. Morton Smith’s interpretation of BCO §38-1 as he quotes 19th century
theologian Rev F. P. Ramsey, viz :
“In handling a confession of guilt it is essential that the person intends to confess
and permit the Court to render judgment without process. Statements made by him
in the presence of the court must not betaken as the basis of judgment without
process except by consent, for that would be to deprive him of his right ”
The facts in this case do not support a voluntary “coming forward” of rev
Bruce Nickoley. But, the facts in this case clearly contradict any voluntary confession
by him that would justify trying the case without due process The undisputed facts, in
the ROC, are:
1 “When TE Nickoley saw how BCO 38-1 was being used. . . he clearly
stated in unequivocal terms that he was admitting to no guilt whatsoever
regard the charge of contumacy ” (ROC 1-3).
2. “It was clear to RMP that TE Nickoley was not intending to confess to
the charge of contumacy and a full statement of the facts were never
given to the accused for his approval before the court proceeded to a
judgment ” (ROC 1-4).
3. “In the light of TE Nickoley’s clear denial of his guilt in the charge of
contumacy, the BCO is clear as to the process by which we must proceed
- if the accused . plead and take issue the trial shall proceed.” (ROC I-
6).
We are, therefore, of the opinion that since Rev. Bruce Nickoley did not invoke
BCO §38-1, rm phase r re din denying him aright to a trial, the right to be heard and
defend his actions, and the right to due process under the provisions of the BCO.

BCO §34-4
BCO §34-4 deals with citations to a minister ina case under process. In the first
instance, this section is not applicable to this case for this is not a case “pertaining to
process against a minister,” but RMP deals with it as a case without process.
Furthermore, a “citation” is merely a notice to a party to appear a ta trial. This entire
section deals only with a minister being notified to attend atria land the penalties that
may be imposed if the minister fails to appear For these reasons, this BCO §34-4 is not
applicable to the facts of this case. RMP then erred in citing this BCO §34-4 as the
constitutional basis for its judgment that “Rev. Bruce Nickoley be immediately deposed
from the ministry.” Rev Bruce Nickoley had not “failed to appear before the
Presbytery” or “refuse to attend” a trial (BCO §34-6) under the BCO provisions
“pertaining to process against a minister.” So we are of the opinion that BCO §34-4 is
not applicable to this case and cannot be relied upon by RMP as a constitutional basis
for its action.
BCO 6'32-6
BCO §32-6 deals with situations where an accused person “shall refuse to obey
a citation.”
The dictionary defines the word “citation” as:
“An official summons or notice given to a person to appear, as
before a tribunal of Justice.”
The BCO takes the same position as to the nature of a “citation” (BCO §32-2
(3), 32-4, 32-6, 34-4, Appendix G II and III). This is clear as these sections of the BCO
use the same notice to appear for a party and for witnesses, calling them “citation”.
BCO §34-4 provides that before a minister can be “deposed as contumacious” he must
have refused to appear for trial after having received 3 such citations.
Rev Bruce Nickoley did appear before RMP at its Stated Meeting on January
22-24, 1997, as he had been cited, to stand trial on charges that he had violated his
ordination vows It was during this trial that RMP gave him instructions relating to the
people picketing outside Deer Creek Community Church He was likewise present
when the motion was made and carried to convict him of contumacy and to depose him
from the ministry. He remained in the presbytery meeting until his trial for violation of
his ordination vows was declared “moot”. Then the RMP minutes read:
“Moved, seconded and carried to continue in Executive Session, with only Stated
Clerk Paul Gilchrist being allowed to stay. Mr. Nickoley was invited to leave the
meeting, which he did.”
There is absolutely no evidence that Rev. Nickoley violated any citations by
refusing or failing to appear
We are, therefore, of the opinion that RMP is in error in contending that Rev.
Nickoley violated BCO §32-6 and relying on this BCO provision as a constitutional
basis for its judgment against Rev. Nickoley
BCO §35-12
This provision deals with an “officer or member of the Church refusing to
testify” and on that basis, being censured for contumacy. This provision simply is not

applicable to the facts in this case. The facts are undisputed that Rev Nickoley has not
“refused to testify.” He was attending the presbytery and testified in the case against
him for violation of his ordination vows.
RMP inferred that because he refused to obey its instructions and because he
refused to repent, that these could be considered as a “refusal to testify” and thus
violations of this BCO §35-12. We disagree. Rev. Nickoley remained in the presbytery
until he was asked to leave. The undisputed fact is as stated in the ROC:
“At no point in the proceedings did RMP give opportunity for TE Nickoley to
make a defense of his action. RMP did this because they refused the right of
trial to TE Nickoley.” (ROC 1-7).
powers of presbytery
Appellee makes a strong argument for its immediate judgment against TE
Nickoley based on the general powers of a presbytery under BCO §13-9a “to . .
remove and judge ministers.” In its brief, Appellee states its argument in such words
as:
(1) “When such (contumacious) action is demonstrated before the body
under which a person takes avow to submit, then immediate censure
should be forthcoming,”
(2) “The RMP which has the authority over Mr. Nickoley has the power to
immediately render discipline.”
(3) “Without the power of immediate censure for rebellious acts occurring
before it by a member, rm pis disenfranchised o fits right to govern its
members.”
(4) “By logical inference therefore, when a person refuses a lawful order by
the presbyters who are his brothers in the Lord an immediate censure for
contumacy is allowed. . . Not allowing this would be handcuffing the
court and saying it has no immediate power of censure for unrepentant
and rebellious behavior occurring before its very eyes.”
(5) “The court ha san inherent authority to protect the integrity o fits
process. To allow men to manipulate the system would be a travesty of
justice.”
(6) “If a minister acts ina rebellious way to a lawful order directed to him
on the floor of presbytery, he must be immediately judged for contempt
in order to preserve the integrity and authority of the court ”
(7) “TE Nickoley continued to persist in his refusal to obey the lawful order
of RMP and was summarily charged and dealt with which is an inherent
power of the court for behavior contemptuous to it.”
(8) “Presbytery by virtue of its right to judge its member (BCO §13-
9a). . . and hearing from its member that he will not obey a lawful order
may issue a finding of contempt o factions of contumacy occurring
before its eyes. If presbytery is not allowed to do this, then justice is not
served in cases where ministers openly refuse to obey presbytery
decrees, and the power of dealing with contempt is stripped from the
authority being sinned against.”
