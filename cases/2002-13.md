4. There is mistaken dependence upon contemporary Reformed positions on the days of creation. The positions of individuals in the PCA and seminaries respected by the PCA is one thing. What our *Confessional Standards* say and mean is quite another thing. The former represents the research and tentative conclusions of individuals and institutions of learning. By their nature, such views are in process of development. Regarding seminaries, an informed allowance of differing views may allow and promote critical examination, exploration, and discovery. However, our *Confessional Standards* are by nature expressions of relatively settled dogma. They do not claim inerrancy, and thus are subject to amendment. But neither are they so fluid and ephemeral as to accommodate an emerging host of novel and mutually contradictory views on the days of creation. The fact that there is and has been diversity in the denomination on the question of the interpretation of Genesis 1 is completely irrelevant. It tells us what the church at large has been willing to tolerate. It does not tell us what the *Confession* says. The decision of whether or not an exception is necessary in a particular case, if it is to have any meaning at all, has to be based on what the *Confession* says, not on what divergences are currently generally assumed as permissible.

5. The cases cited as Judicial precedent on the days of creation do not support the SJC majority's decision that Eastern Carolina Presbytery did not err when it deemed the licentiate's view on creation days not to be an exception to the *Confessional Standards*. Only *Bowen vs. Eastern Carolina Presbytery* and *Mt. Carmel Session vs. New Jersey Presbytery* can reasonably be construed as lending support to the SJC majority decision. Of those two, *Mt. Carmel*, a quite divided decision within the SJC and the Assembly, contains the strongest and clearest expression of support for the current decision in these words: "it is by no means clear that the Westminster phrase 'in/within the space of six days' must be interpreted as involving 24-hour days." These words were not, in fact, written by the SJC, but rather by New Jersey Presbytery (*M26GA*, 1998, p. 107). To this, we respond that (1) church courts can and do err, as in this cited instance, and (2) the SJC opinion in *Mt. Carmel* states: "In this instance the SJC affirms it is not making a judgment with regard to the interpretation of the days of creation..." (*M26GA*, 1998, p. 107).

1. The opening sentence of the "Reasoning and Opinion" section, states that "the Record does not indicate exactly what particular view the licentiate holds..." in this matter. Yet, the SJC majority has deemed the licentiate's view as being within the parameters of the Confessional Standards of the PCA without their knowing what that view is. We believe and declare from our deliberations in the SJC that the majority does not intend to countenance any and every view of the days of creation. Neither has the SJC determined henceforth to overturn per se judgments of other Presbyteries wherein non-literal views may be judged to be exceptions to our Standards. Yet, the majority decision to affirm the action of Eastern Carolina Presbytery, without the SJC knowing this licentiate's views, except as it is recorded above, leaves the decision open to be wrongly construed that the SJC countenances any and every view of the days of creation.

2. It is a misleading overstatement to assert that the conservative Reformed community has generally assumed for more than 150 years "that the Confession articulates no particular position on the length of the creation days." Whatever individual writers such as Shaw, Hodge, Mitchell, and Warfield may have expressed on this question, they spoke as individuals, not as church courts. Nor is it surprising that their conservative brethren did not challenge them on this matter, when there were, in their day, so many other more crucial battles being waged in their church courts. Still less surprising is it that they were not challenged by the liberal consensus in their denominations. More to the point, whatever attitudes prevailed in the PCUS, PCUSA, and Church of Scotland, our concern is solely with the more truly conservative and Reformed PCA. The fact that there has lately been contention within the PCA over this issue can be fairly attributed to the very recent articulation and emergence within the PCA of non-literal views on creation days.

3. There is erroneous assertion that the original intent of the Westminster divines cannot be determined on this matter. The opinion admits that it "seems no evidence has been found of any view other than the Calendar Day in the writings of individual Westminster divines." The opinion further admits that "Recent studies...have documented at least five divines, and perhaps as many as twenty-one, who affirmed the Calendar Day view." The opinion rightly cites wording from the report of the PCA Study Committee on Divorce and Remarriage as follows:

It is a sound principle that the constitutional documents should be interpreted according to their original intent.
