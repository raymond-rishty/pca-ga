## APPEAL OF TE DAVID BAER VS ILLIANA PRESBYTERY

### I. A Summary of the Facts

On October 1, 1997 The Session of Westminster Presbyterian Church (hereinafter WPC) of Godfrey, IL (three Ruling Elder members functioning at that time) met with the Appellant and the Youth Pastor and differences between the two were discussed. They were instructed, according to approved Session Minutes, to apologize to the congregation and indicate their support for one another.

On October 9, 1997, after the Appellant had made a statement to the congregation on October 5th, the Session met again and expressed concern about the statement. They had understood that Appellant and the Youth Pastor were to meet together and then make individual statements to the congregation. Contrary to their understanding, the Appellant had made his own statement without showing it to the Youth Pastor or having it reviewed by Session. A motion was passed agreeing that the Appellant, Youth Pastor, and their spouses would get together “for fellowship and healing” before October 19th.

On October 15, 1997 a called meeting of the Session was held to discuss the “ongoing conflict” between Appellant and the Youth Pastor and “some long term concerns” with the Appellant’s ministry. The Session and Appellant discussed areas of disagreement, including the Session’s perception of the Appellant’s “lack of majority congregational support.”

On October 25, 1997 Appellant wrote to Session members, informing them that he had asked assistance from Illiana Presbytery and expected the Shepherding Committee would meet with him soon. He asserted that since he was a member of Presbytery and the Youth Pastor was under care, the Presbytery would have jurisdiction of their difficulties.

On October 30, 1997 a called meeting of Session was held to discuss Appellant’s ministry. A docket had been sent out which had been prepared by the Clerk of Session and which included several items of business, but Appellant asserted he had the right (presumably as Moderator) to set the docket and length of meeting. Therefore, he declared that the only item to be discussed would be his ministry. Unannounced, he also brought a Teaching Elder from Presbytery to the meeting, which at least one

Ruling Elder considered inappropriate. After discussion, two of the three Ruling Elders stated that because of a lack of trust in the Appellant, his ministry at WPC was “done.” The third Ruling Elder was not yet ready to join in that conclusion.

At a regular meeting of Session on November 6, 1997, Appellant reported that he had met with Illiana Presbytery’s Shepherding Committee and that they had advised that “his ministry was done at Westminster and he should get counseling immediately.” According to Session records, Appellant rejected their counsel on the ground that they were “not probably qualified” to give such advice. A second option proposed by the Shepherding Committee, that of Appellant’s taking a leave of absence for counseling and correction of his problems, was said by the Committee not to be the best solution, and they further doubted that Session would support it. More discussion of differences was held and all three Ruling Elder members of Session recommended that the Appellant resign on November 9th, that he attempt to bring about healing during his remaining time at the church, and that he seek professional counseling.

During a “business meeting” of the congregation on November 9, 1997, a member of the congregation asked for the floor and announced that Appellant had been asked to resign. This was denied by one of the Ruling Elders but he stated there had been many meetings and that the congregation would be notified shortly. In a letter that same day, but not delivered until November 10th, the Session formally requested Appellant’s resignation. They noted that if he did not comply, they would be forced to call a special congregational meeting to dissolve the pastoral relationship.

During a called meeting of Session on November 13th, Appellant stated that he declined to resign because it was a matter of “standing for the truth.” When pressed as to the Shepherding Committee’s advice re professional counseling, Appellant noted that the committee had thought that Session would not go along with the idea of an approved leave period while he sought professional counseling, and Appellant therefore concluded there “was no real need for it.” One Session member expressed “strong disagreement with the truthfulness” of Appellant’s statement. Appellant felt that the Session should have told him of problems earlier, but Session believed they had been telling him for several years.

On November 23, 1997, a lengthy congregational meeting was held regarding Session’s request that Appellant resign. Appellant declared: “[I]t is six weeks now they have been demanding my resignation” and later in the meeting stated: “Did you know the Session was demanding my resignation when it first started some six weeks ago?” Session members admitted that for weeks the Session had been asking Appellant to “resign on his own” and even “begging, basically begging him to resign on his own” but had not “told him from us that we were telling him to resign.” It was denied that Session had refused to let Appellant go to the Shepherding Committee. With respect to the difference between an initial and finally approved draft of Session minutes regarding Appellant’s agreement to speak to the congregation about his differences with the Youth Pastor, Appellant stated that “the minutes all of a sudden were rewritten to be in conformity with the way the elders saw things.”

At the conclusion of the congregational meeting, the vote to request Appellant’s resignation was 39 yes and 32 no. Since a 2/3 majority was required by the WPC bylaws, the request for the Appellant’s resignation was not carried.

## II. Statement of the Issues

A. Did Illiana Presbytery err in concluding that Appellant violated the 9th commandment, the 4th and 7th ordinations vows, and the 3rd installation vow by erroneously informing the congregation of WPC that the Session had “demanded” his resignation six weeks before the congregational meeting?

B. Did Illiana Presbytery commit such irregularities, manifest such prejudice, and commit such actions by their judgment and censure of the Appellant as to deprive him of the due process of the Book of Church Order and of righteous and Biblical judgment as is required by Scripture and the Constitution of the PCA?

## III. Judgment in the Case

A. No.
B. No.

## IV. Reasoning and Opinion of the Court

A. Much attention has been given to the meaning of the word “demand” as used by the Appellant on two occasions during the congregational meeting called to determine whether or not his resignation would be required. It cannot be denied that individual Session members had earlier expressed themselves strongly that for various reasons the Appellant should resign and that he should consider his ministry among the people of WPC as ended. Indeed, one elder admitted that he virtually “begged” Appellant to resign for the good of the congregation and its unity.

It is also accurate to say, from a technical standpoint, that one definition of the word “demand” would perhaps approximate the language used at times by individual Session members.

However, the Session as a whole took no formal action until just after the congregational meeting, and the previous viewpoints of individual members must be considered only as opinions of particular Session members at certain times.

Whether or not the Presbytery, through its Commission, was justified in finding the statement by the Appellant to be a violation of the 9th commandment must be evaluated in light of the circumstances in which the statement was made. In the stress and pressure of a lengthy and volatile congregational meeting, the Appellant should have been zealous not to overstate his position or inaccurately state it. Instead, his action in stating and then reiterating that the Session had “demanded” his resignation for six weeks, when in fact it had obviously sought to achieve its goal through voluntary resignation so as to maintain congregational peace and unity, served to put the Session’s actions in a much more unfavorable light than was justified by the overall circumstances and cannot be condoned. Indeed, Appellant has now admitted that the word “demanded” was an “emotionally charged word” which inflamed passions. The 77th question and answer of the Westminster Shorter Catechism declares that the 9th commandment requires “the maintaining and promoting of truth between man and man, and of our own and our neighbor’s good name, especially in witness bearing” (Emphasis added). Appellant was surely bearing witness, though not under oath, before the congregation in a matter of great significance to the people, and he had a duty to maintain a much higher standard than he did. We conclude, therefore, that Presbytery did not err in this specification.

B. Appellant avers that the charges to which he earlier pled guilty were essentially minor and he has attempted to explain them as such. However, in rendering its judgment and censure the Presbytery acted upon the total picture. We find no convincing reasons for overturning the Presbytery’s conclusion that he was guilty of the sins charged against him or for faulting their infliction of the censure of suspension until such time as Appellant repents. Presbytery and

Appellant continue to seek resolution of their difficulty, but their current relationship is outside the scope of this judicial case.

We have considered Appellant’s specific complaints as set forth in his appeal, brief, and argument. We find no convincing support for the allegation that any specific material available to the Presbytery has been improperly withheld from the Appellant, or, in any event, would be likely to have any significant impact upon the final judgment herein.

We likewise are not persuaded by written evidence or oral argument that any “missing” evidence, if such exists, would have any substantial impact upon our ultimate conclusion. There are, no doubt, some minor flaws in Presbytery’s records, but this is understandable in a case as prolonged and complex as the one before us. The record as a whole demonstrates a Presbytery which has not arbitrarily or summarily dealt with Appellant with a lack of concern for justice and pastoral concern. It has dealt with this matter over a prolonged period, utilizing its Shepherding Committee and an investigative committee before making the matter a specific judicial case. We cannot conclude the record justifies the SJC in substituting its judgment for that of the Presbytery in a matter of this type.

We find the principles set forth in *BCO* 39-3 to be very applicable. The SJC is required to “limit itself to issues raised by the parties in the case in the original (lower) court” and to give “great deference to a lower court regarding those factual matters which the lower court is more competent to determine, because of its proximity to the events in question, and because of its personal knowledge and observations of the parties and witnesses involved.” The same is true as to “matters of discretion and judgment.” We have sought to take very seriously these provisions and their application to this particular case. Therefore, while it may be argued that there were minor actions of the lower court which might have been better handled, we find no such flaws in the Presbytery’s overall handling of this case as to justify reversing of their judgment and censure. Accordingly, the Judicial Panel recommends to the Standing Judicial Commission that the appeal be denied.
