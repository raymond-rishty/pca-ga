Indefinite suspension is to be administered only when the person is impenitent; while definite suspension is appropriate only when the person is repentant. When the commission ruled that Mr. Baer should be suspended for a definite period of time, that commission judged that Mr. Baer had indeed repented. However, when the Presbytery imposed a censure of indefinite suspension, the court overruled that finding of penitence by the commission, and, in essence, determined that the man had not repented.

This sort of injustice is precisely what our rules and procedures are supposed to prevent. An accused had the right to plead with a court as to why he should not be found guilty, or impenitent, and why a certain censure should not be imposed. But according to the SJC’s own record of the matter, Mr. Baer was not present at the Presbytery meeting where the court, without having heard from him, proceeded to suspend him indefinitely, thereby branding him as a man who had not yet repented.

I do not wish to impugn the motives of the SJC or of the panel which heard the appeal. However, the erroneous judgment in this case is yet another example where the SJC has adjudicated in such a way as to pervert justice. By means of this formal objection, I hereby declare my repudiation of the judgment in this judicial case, and call upon the General Assembly, as a court, to condemn the opinion of the SJC in this matter.

/s/ RE Paul English, Central Carolina

B. The Assembly adopted the following response to the Objection to the judgement of the SJC in Case 98-10:

Response:

Said objection includes the following language in the first paragraph “sincerely hoping that the GA will deny the SJC decision and instruct Eastern Carolina that the Appeal of TE Byron Curtis is out of order and not to come before Eastern Carolina Presbytery.”

The General Assembly responds as follows: A recommendation of the SJC in response to a reference of a judicial case is a recommendation of the General Assembly as provided in BCO 15-5 (See also BCO 41-1).

Objection:

The Standing Judicial Commission agreed with the panel’s recommendation that the Complaint (sic) of TE Byron Curtis be returned (sic) to Eastern Carolina Presbytery with the instruction to hear it. I do object to the recommendation upon several grounds to be stated below, sincerely hoping that the GA will deny the SJC decision and instruct Eastern Carolina Presbytery that the Appeal of TE Byron Curtis is out of order and not to come before Eastern Carolina Presbytery.

My objection rests upon five major grounds: (I) errors in the Statement of Facts; (II) violations of the Constitution by the SJC; (III) the SJC ignoring the action of the CCB; (IV) the SJC contradicting the express understanding and intention of the Eastern Carolina Presbytery’s reference to the CCB; and, (V) apparent untruths in their reasoning.

I. Errors in the Statement of Facts

1. As to item #4 of the Statement of Facts written by the SJC, the Presbytery of the Ascension denied the transfer of TE Byron Curtis because the theology portion of his examination was not sustained. Certainly his declared exceptions were vigorously debated but the record does not declare these to be the basis of the denial of transfer. The SJC has drawn a conclusion as to why members of the presbytery voted in the manner they did so. They may be right but it is conjecture. I know why I voted and it was not merely TE Curtis’s exceptions.

2. Between items #5 and 6 should be added that the Presbytery of the Ascension granted permission to TE Byron Curtis to labor within their bounds for a period of one year to be reassessed annually.

3. As to item #10 the SJC states, “After receiving TE Curtis’s complaint, ECP sent that complaint along with TE Black’s sustained complaint as a reference to GA.” This is in error on two points. First, Eastern Carolina Presbytery did not formally receive TE Byron Curtis’s complaint (sic). Austin Leake, Stated Clerk, read the copy of TE Curtis’s Appeal (Appeal was the term TE Curtis used in his own document, the SJC altered it to “complaint.”) and notified the presbytery that it wasn’t before us as it was merely a copy of the appeal filed with General Assembly. The minutes of January 8 &amp; 9, 1999 demonstrate this in the wording of motion #75 “by acclamation the ECP appoint TEs Jeff Black and James Routszong as its respondent to SJC regarding TE Byron Curtis’ Appeal (sic) to argue the Appeal (sic) is out of order and/or defend ECP should the SJC take up the matter.”

Second, the reference to GA was directed not to the SJC but to the CCB as the minutes of May 8, 1999 prove with motion #20 “We do move that ECP clarify its reference (Motion #55 Winter 1999 Stated Meeting) not to be concerning the complaint of TE Black nor the appeal (sic) of TE Byron Curtis but this specific question to the Committee on Constitutional Business; “Does a presbytery have the authority to require a man not to teach his errors as true in any public forum save that of the courts of the Presbyterian Church in America after that man has been received into the presbytery?”
