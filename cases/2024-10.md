**MR. PAUL GOZÉ**

**v.**

**HILLS AND PLAINS PRESBYTERY**

#### DECISION ON APPEAL

**February 19, 2025**

This case is judicially out of order and cannot be put in order because it was
not timely filed. On January 10, 2024, in the course o fa trial, the trial court
determined the Appellant to be contumacious, and it suspended him from the
sacraments. The Appellant appealed that decision to Presbytery on May 8,
2024, more than thirty (30) days after notification of the decision of the lower
court. As the appeal was not timely filed as required by _BCO_ 42-4, it is
judicially out of order ( _OMSJC_ 10.5(a)) and must be dismissed.

This decision does not reach or address any substantive issue or allegation in
the case.

SJC reviewed the proposed decision and approved the final version of the
Ruling by vote of **19-1-0, 3 absent and 1 recused** .

**HILLS AND PLAINS PRESBYTERY**

**DISSENT**

RE Jim Eggert

March 10, 2025

The instant appeal was found “judicially out of order” because it was untimely.
“Judicially out of order” cases often result in a decision providing limited
information. Because the particular facts and circumstances underlying the
SJC’s decision are invisible to the reader, I offer this dissent in hopes that it
increases understanding of the rules governing the timeliness of appeals.

While technical, timeliness is a matter of real consequence (1) to censured
members who wish to secure higher court review of a judgment against, (2) to
courts of original jurisdiction that seek to achieve finality of their decisions in
the exercise of their rightful powers and (3) to the denomination whose polity
is strengthened by higher court review.

In general, a person censured b yo neo four courts enjoys aright of appeal,
making such censure “subject to the review and control of the higher courts,
in regular gradation.” ( _BCO_ 11-4). The right of appeal, however, is not
absolute, being circumscribed by the prescriptions of _BCO_ Chapter 42,
including the thirty-day time limit for filing an appeal set out in _BCO_ 42-4.

It is my view that the time limit imposed by _BCO_ 42-4 should be strictly
construed because it removes the rights of censured individuals and cuts off
higher court review, an interpretive approach favoring the preservation of both
the rights of individuals as well as the realization of the “mutual relation”
( _BCO_ 11-4) of our courts inherent to our presbyterian form of government.

_**A Quick Look at the Circumstances of This Case**_

In this case, the session conducted a trial of appellant o na four-specification
indictment. During his trial, the Moderator gave the Appellant “four warnings
for his behavior” in the trial proceedings and eventually determined that the

Appellant was contumacious pursuant to _BCO_ 32-6, which provides, “when
an accused person shall appear and refuse to cooperate with lawful
proceedings,” he shall be dealt with for his contumacy. _BCO_ 33-2 provides,
“When an accused person is found contumacious (cf. 32-6), he shall be
immediately suspended from the sacraments (and if an officer from his office)
for his contumacy.”

Having determined that Appellant had transgressed _BCO_ 32-6, the Session
suspended the trial, leaving the underlying indictment unadjudicated, and the
Moderator declared the censure of suspension from the Sacraments in the
Appellant’s presence.

The Appellant appealed the Session’s decision to the Presbytery some months
after the Session’s oral pronouncement. The Presbytery found that the appeal
was “not in order and thus should not be entertained” because Appellant “did
not meet the requirements of _BCO_ 42-2, nor was the appeal filed in a timely
manner in accordance with _BCO_ 42-4.” _BCO_ 42-2 provides: “The only parties
entitled to an appeal are those who have submitted to a regular trial…” _BCO_
42-4 provides: “Notice of appeal … shall be filed by the appellant with both
the clerk of the lower court and the clerk of the higher court, within thirty (30)
days of notification of the last court’s decision.”

Because the SJC’s decision cites only _BCO_ 42-4 relating to the _timeliness_ of
the appeal as the rationale for its decision, I will not discuss any implications
of _BCO_ 42-2 which would address whether Appellant was entitled to file an
appeal at all.

_**A Detailed Explanation of my Interpretation of BCO 42-4**_

I believe that the appeal was timely. The thirty-day period prescribed by _BCO_
42-4 never began to run because the Session never reduced its decision to
writing and never delivered the same to the Appellant in the manner prescribed
by _BCO_ 42-4.

_BCO_ 42-4 provides:

Notice of appeal may be given the court before its
adjournment. Written notice of appeal, with supporting
reasons, shall be filed by the appellant with both the clerk of

the lower court and the clerk of the higher court, within thirty
(30) days of notification of the last court’s decision.

Therefore, strictly speaking, any notice of appeal must be filed “within thirty
(30) days of _notification_ of the last court’s decision” (emphasis added).

To faithfully apply this rule, we must settle on a satisfactory definition of
“notification.”

I interpret _BCO_ 42-4 to mean that _notification_ refers to a _written notification_
from the court, meaning that a court’s in-person pronouncement of censure
does not start the thirty-day period.

The history of the development of _BCO_ 42-4 informs my interpretive
understanding.

At the commencement of the PCA in 1973 our polity did not calculate the time
for filing an appeal from “notification” of the court’s decision but measured
the appeal period from the date of the court’s “rising,” which is another way
of referring to the conclusion of the meeting of the court. _Rules of Discipline_
16-4 (M1GA 1973, page 153). In 1984 the General Assembly increased the
time for filing an appeal to fifteen days from the “meeting of the court.”
(M12GA 1984, page 88). Three years later the General Assembly increased
the time to thirty days from the same starting point. (M15GA 1987, page 95).
In the case of censures imposed _in absentia_, this rubric made it possible for the
appeal period to commence regardless of when the censured individual learned
of the censure against him.

In 2013 the General Assembly abandoned the “meeting of the court” rubric,
marking a sea change in _BCO_ 42-2. The preceding year Pacific Northwest
Presbytery had offered an Overture proposing that “the date the person
receives a copy of the court’s decision,” rather than the date of the “meeting
of the court,” should commence the appeal period. (M40GA 2012, page 697).
The rationale offered by the Overture stated:

The 30-day appeal clock should not start until the guilty
person receives a copy of the court's decision. For example if
a session judges a church member guilty and imposes a
censure, his 30-day clock should begin the day he receives a

copy of the session decision, rather than beginning on the date
of the session meeting. In addition, if he appeals to pres byte ry
and it is denied, his 30-day clock should begin the day he
receives a copy of the Presbytery decision on his appeal,
rather than the date of the Presbytery meeting. Presumably,
the decision would be delivered in person, by certified mail,
or in some other manner that ensures verification of the date

of receipt (similar to _BCO_ 32-4 regarding citations).

( _M40GA_ 2012, page 698). Notably, this proposal seemed to suggest that the
rule should be changed to require that the court reduce its decision to writing,
since the thirty-day appeal period would not begin to run until the censured
individual “received a copy” of the same. Obviously, an in-person censure
does not entail the “receipt of a copy” of anything.

Although the 40 [th] General Assembly (on the recommendation of the Overtures
Committee) did not adopt the Overture in exactly the form proposed, it adopted
its essential innovation that the time for counting the thirty day appeal period
would no longer be measured from the date of the “meeting of the court,” but
from the censured person’s “notification of the last court’s decision” and added
a paragraph describing how “notification shall be deemed to have occurred.”
Here is the change that was adopted:

Notice of appeal may be given the court before its
adjournment. Written notice of appeal, with supporting
reasons, shall be filed by the appellant with both the clerk of
the lower court and the clerk of the higher court, within thirty
(30) days following the meeting of the court of notification of
the last Court's decision. Notification shall be deemed to have

occurred on the day of mailing (if certified, registered or
express mail of a national postal service or any private service
where verifying receipt is utilized), the day of hand delivery,
or the day of confirmed receipt in the case of e-mail or
facsimile. Furthermore, compliance with such requirements
shall be deemed to have been fulfilled if a party cannot be
located after diligent inquiry or if a party refuses to accept
delivery. No attempt should be made to circularize the courts
to which appeal is being made by either party before the case
is heard.

(M40GA 2012, page 73). The rationale offered by the Overtures Committee
for the amendment was as follows:

The change provides a bright line for when the clock begins
for the filing of an appeal. Rather than having the clock begin
potentially before notification of a court’s decision becomes
available to an appellant, the clock begins in relation to
notification of the decision. Provision is made for various

forms of proof of notification, with language adapted from
_OMSJC_ 18.10.

(M40GA 2012, page 73). The following year, the General Assembly adopted
the change with the requisite Presbytery approval. (M41GA 2013, page 110).
_BCO_ 42-4 was amended again in 2021 when the General Assembly brought
the first two sentences of _BCO_ 42-2 into conformity with one another so that
the same phrase -- “notification of the last Court’s decision” -- is used in each.
(M48GA 2021, page 145-146). The relevant section now reads:

Notice of appeal may be given the court before its
adjournment. Written notice of appeal, with supporting
reasons, shall be filed by the appellant with both the clerk of
the lower court and the clerk of the higher court, within thirty
(30) days of notification of the last court’s decision.
Notification of the last court’s decision shall be deemed to

have occurred on the day of mailing (if certified, registered or
express mail of a national postal service or any private service
where verifying receipt is utilized), the day of hand delivery,
or the day of confirmed receipt in the case of e-mail or
facsimile. Furthermore, compliance with such requirements
shall be deemed to have been fulfilled if a party cannot be
located after diligent inquiry or if a party refuses to accept
delivery...

Some understand that the 2013 and 2021 revisions of _BCO_ 42-4 hold that the
court’s in-person pronouncement of censure is a qualifying “notification”
triggering the thirty-day appeal period. According to this view, the “deeming”
clause added to the second sentence of _BCO_ 42-4 simply afforded _additional_
methods of “notification” besides the in-person pronouncement. For example,

if a censured individual fails or refuses to personally appear for the declaration
of his censure, the “deeming” clause gives the court a means to institute the
thirty-day appeal clock. Similarly, the new provision afforded a means to start
the clock in cases where the convicted individual either cannot be found or

refuses to accept delivery. But, on this view, regardless of these additional
methods, “notification” would nevertheless be satisfied by a court’s in-person
announcement of censure, apparently rendering the express list of notification
methods either redundant or unnecessary where an in-person censure has been
imposed.

I am not persuaded of that interpretation of _BCO_ 42-4.

_**Considerations of Interpretation**_

While others read the phrase _notification shall be deemed to have occurred on_
_…_ to leave open other methods not in the list following the clause, I interpret
the mandatory verb “shall” to exclude any of the notification methods not
appearing in the list, such as an in-person pronouncement of censure. The
auxiliary verb “shall” ina law or rule usually signifies a mandatory obligation
or requirement and is often used to express that something must be done,
indicating a strong sense of compulsion or duty. _Shall_ is often contrasted with
_may_, where shall is mandatory and _may_ is permissive. Thus, we might read
_shall_ as _has a duty_ or _is required to_ . I propose that when the word “shall” can
reasonably be read as mandatory, it ought to be so read.

_BCO_ 42-4 begins, “Notice of appeal may be given the court before its
adjournment.” Here we find the use of the permissive _may_ . Thus, a person
dissatisfied with the court’s ruling _may_ give a “Notice of Appeal” at the
meeting of the court where the censure is imposed. This sentence does not
specify whether the “notice” must be in writing or may be spoken, but
presumably it could be either. Thus, we must at least consider the possibility
that “notice” might encompass an in-person statement for purposes of _BCO_
42-4, although this consideration is diminished by the fact that _BCO_ 42-4 uses
the word “notification” rather than “notice” in the parts of the text that are
dispositive of this matter.

The section continues, “Written notice of appeal”—the same phrase is used as
in the first sentence—”with supporting reasons, shall be filed by the appellant
with both the clerk of the lower court and the clerk of the higher court, within

thirty (30) days of notification of the last court’s decision.” Obviously, a
“Written notice of appeal” must be in writing. Therefore, in this instance _notice_
means notice reduced to writing rather than oral notice. The following phrase,
s _hall be filed_ means that the written notice of appeal _is required_ to be filed
“within thirty (30) days of notification of the last court’s decision.” But does
the word _notification_ in that last phrase refer to delivery of a written document,
delivery of an oral pronouncement, or either mode? This is the critical
question.

We may note in passing that to secure his appeal, an appellant must file a
_written_ notice with supporting reasons. Is it not intuitive that the court
imposing the censure should similarly reduce its decision to writing in
connection with providing “notification” of the same to an appellant? This
interpretation would assign a fitting parity between the written “Notice of
appeal” required of the appellant and the “notification of the last court’s
decision” required by the court imposing censure. The expectation of parity in
the form of a written decision is not proven by the text so far, but this
conclusion becomes clearer as we read forward.

Thus, we continue to the next sentence of _BCO_ 42-4: “Notification of the last
court’s decision shall be deemed to have occurred on the day of mailing (if
certified, registered or express mail of a national postal service or any private
service where verifying receipt is utilized), the day of hand delivery, or the day
of confirmed receipt in the case of e-mail or facsimile.”

The passive voice, _shall be deemed_, makes the interpretation of the full phrase
“notification of the last court’s decision shall be deemed to have occurred”

somewhat awkward. On the one hand, the section does not say, “the court shall
provide written notification in the following manner…” That would have made
it easy to understand that a censuring court ha san obligation to provide a
written notification in at least one of the prescribed ways.

Instead of this clearer formulation, _BCO_ 42-4 uses the phrase _shall be deemed_
_to have occurred_, using the auxiliary verb “shall” to modify the passive verb
phrase _be_ _deemed_ . We can fairly take this to mean that the court _has a duty to_
_deem_ or _is required_ to _deem_ the “notification of the last court’s decision” to
have occurred on the date prescribed according to the listed modes of delivery.
To “deem” means _to regard or consider in a specified way; to come to think_
_or judge; to hold_ . Therefore, for example, this phrase can be fairly reiterated

as “notification of the last court’s decision shall be _judged_ to have occurred on
the day of mailing…” I read the mandatory verb phrase “ _shall_ be deemed” to
create both a clarification of the preceding sentence of _BCO_ 42-4 and a
prescription for a written notification of the last court’s decision to the
censured person in one of the prescribed modes. The contrary interpretation
effectively, and I think mistakenly, supposes that a court “ _may_ ”—not “ _shall_ ”
as I understand the provision to mandate— _deem_, _judge_ or _regard_ notification
as occurring in some other way than the modes listed in _BCO_ 42-4 (such as,
for example, via the in-person imposition of censure).

I believe my conclusion is supported by the fair use and understanding of the
word “notification.” Notification usually means (1) _the act or an instance of_
_notifying_ or (2) _a written or printed matter that gives notice_ . ( _Webster's Ninth_
_Collegiate Dictionar_ y, 1991). Since the latter definition obviously mandates
delivery of a _written_ form of the last court’s decision, I will discuss only the
former sense which, for the reasons set out below, supports the same result.

Applying the former definition— _the_ _act oran instance of notifying_ —as
informing the word “notification” in the phrase “notification of the last court’s
decision” would mean that an appeal shall be filed within thirty days of _the act_
_or an instance of notifying the appellant_ of the last court's decision _._ To _notify_
may mean alternately (1) _to give notice of or report the occurrence of_ or (2) _to_
_give formal notice_ ( _Webster's Ninth Collegiate Dictionar_ y, 1991).

Taking the first definition of “notify” would mean that an appeal shall be filed
within thirty days of _the act or an instance of giving notice of_ the court’s
decision. But what does “giving notice” refer to? A dictionary analysis reveals
that _notice_ (which must be “given” _)_ bears a range of meaning. The “act” or
“instance” of “giving notice” might mean variously an _announcement_, _the_
_condition of being warned or notified_ (usually expressed by the phrase “on
notice”), _information_, or _a_ _written or printed announcement_ . This definition is
ambiguous because, considering the possible range of meaning described
above, “giving notice” might be plausibly satisfied either by the court’s _in-_
_person pronouncement_ of censure on the accused orb y delivery o fa _written_
_announcement_ of the same.

In my view this ambiguity should be settled in favor of a stricter reading of
_BCO_ 42-4 requiring a written decision. This is because (1) because cutting off
the right to an appeal is in derogation of the rights of individuals who have

been censured and (2) because cutting off the “review and control of the higher
courts” i sin derogation of the “regular gradation” inherent to our system of
polity. ( _BCO_ 11-4).

Having disposed of the first use of “notify,” we turn to the next. This would
mean “notification of the last court’s decision” would require that an appeal
shall be filed within thirty days of _reporting the occurrence of_ the court’s
decision _._ This definition would seem to require something more than the
occurrence of an in-person pronouncement of censure because certainly the
occurrence of the censure is a different thing than the _reporting of the_
_occurrence_ of that censure.

Similarly, if we assume that “notification of the last court’s decision” means
that an appeal shall be filed within thirty days of _giving formal notice_ to the
appellant of the court’s decision, we conclude yet again that written notice
would be required _. F_ o _rmal notice_ is commonly understood as official
information provided in writing advising someone of a specific action he needs
to take ora situation he needs to be aware of, usually with a clear deadline and
potential consequences if not followed. That definition would not be satisfied
by an unwritten in-person censure.

For all these reasons, I believe that the best interpretation of the express words
“notification of the last court’s decision” is that the thirty-day appeal period
only begins after a _written notification_ of the court’s decision is delivered to a
censured person.

_**Policy Considerations**_

Not only do the express words of _BCO_ 42-4 support my proposed
interpretation, but there are also good policy reasons for interpreting _BCO_ 424 as I do.

atria land resulting censure can b ea confusing and overwhelming experience
for an accused, and it would be neither just nor wise to permit an accused’s
thirty day appeal period to commence (and expire) based solely on the inperson censure of a court, thus forcing a censured individual to act based on
his perception of the court’s in-person pronouncement, subject a sit is to the
frailties and errors that may be the fruit of his own misapprehension,
confusion, or errors. Additionally, a court’s failure or refusal to memorialize

its decision in a writing delivered to the censured individual increases the risk
of miscommunication or misunderstanding about the court’s decision.

Further, the burden of providing written notification strictly in accordance with
one of the modes prescribed in the second sentence of _BCO_ 42-4 is rightly
placed upon the court imposing censure since its censure removes “rights and
privileges” that the accused would otherwise enjoy in Christ’s Church. ( _BCO_
6-4). In the absence of a conforming “notification” and proof of one of the
approved modes of delivery, I believe that the accused may file his appeal at
any time since the thirty days cannot commence until the censuring court’s
strict compliance with _BCO_ 42-4 has been established.

Also, the 2012 Overture’s Committee’s rationale that the General Assembly
create a new “bright line” for commencing the thirty-day appeal period
oriented around “notification” rather than the “meeting of the court,” is best
understood as preferring _written notification_ . The 2012 Overtures Committee
expressed its concern that, prior to the amendment, the thirty-day clock might
have been understood to begin to run “before notification of a court’s decision

[became] available to an appellant.” But, consistent with the Overture from
Pacific Northwest Presbytery, the Overtures Committee’s recommendation
rooted “notification” in the decision’s “becoming available to an appellant,”
which seems to point away from the idea that the in-person imposition of
censure should serve as the triggering mechanism, suggesting instead that the
delivery of a _written memorialization_ of the decision in one of the prescribed
modes is required to commence the thirty-day period. In common parlance,
we would not describe a court’s pronouncement of censure in person as a
court’s decision “becoming available” to an individual. We would much more
easily understand the phrase “become available” as referring to the delivery of
a written decision.

Further, the proposed “bright line for when the clock begins for the filing of
an appeal” that commended the adoption of the 2013 amendment seems to wax
dim if we suppose that an in-person pronouncement of censure counts as
“notification” to trigger the thirty-day appeal period. For example, if a court
reduces its verdict to a written decision and delivers the same to the accused,
but is delayed in imposing the censure in person, are we to understand that the
thirty-day clock starts anew on the day of the later in-person pronouncement?
Which “notification” counts? Where is the “bright line?” Or suppose the
matter is the other way around and the court imposes an in-person censure at

the conclusion of the trial but also later reduces its verdict and censure to

writing. In such a case will the thirty-day clock run from the date of the inperson pronouncement, or will it run from the date of notification provided in
accordance with the modes in the second sentence of _BCO_ 42-4? Again, unless
we interpret _BCO_ 42-4 to provide a unitary mode of written notification the
promised “bright line” of the 2013 amendment seems to grow obscure.

_**Concluding Thoughts**_

In my view, reading _BCO_ 42-4 to require a written memorialization of the
court’s decision delivered in the prescribed fashion, best implements the very
words of _BCO_ 42-4, vindicates the rights of individuals, advances the system
of our graded review, and fulfills the assured “bright line” test for determining
when the right of appeal will be extinguished by the mere lapse of time. It also
rightly imposes the burden on the courts of the church, who are removing
rights, to reduce their decision to writing and deliver it to a censured person in
one of the prescribed modes, thereby avoiding confusion and reducing the risk
of the loss of the right of appeal by misunderstanding or mistake.

I will add in passing that, while in this case the Session did not reduce its
decision to writing until after the Appellant filed his appeal, the Record seemed
to show that the Appellant at some point received a transcript of the
proceedings, which raised the prospect that perhaps a written transcript could
satisfy the written “notification” requirements of _BCO_ 42-4. Rather than
taking the time here to set out my analysis of that aspect of the case, I will
simply say that I was not persuaded that the date and mode of the written
transcript’s delivery satisfied the strict requirements of _BCO_ 42-4 to
commence the thirty-day period. I should add that, while I cannot say for sure,
it is at least theoretically possible that others on the SJC may have agreed with
my interpretation of _BCO_ 42-4 set out above yet disagreed with this portion of
my analysis. Since the decision lacks any detailed rationale, I leave open the
possibility that it may conceal disagreement on only this more limited point.

In the end, I learned in this case that others interpret _BCO_ 42-4 differently than
I do. Yet I wonder whether some reading this dissent might agree with my
conception. If so, I thought it would be helpful to share that the interpretation
I have described herein was, at least in this case, put into question. Thus, this
dissent is offered as a caution to both courts and our members to carefully
consider how the thirty-day rule might apply to a case in which they have (or

will have) an interest, and such individuals and courts should proceed with due

care.

Lastly, i respect t ha treasonable minds might disagree about whether ani n person pronouncement of censure should b ea sufficient “notification” for
triggering the thirty-day appeal period. To that end, the courts of our Church
might also consider amendments to _BCO_ 42-4 to ensure that the “bright line”
sought by the 41 [st] General Assembly is realized in a way that is most agreeable
with the collective wisdom of our courts.
