**CASE 2023-20**

**RE JOHN MARTINEZ**

**v.**

**PACIFIC PRESBYTERY**

#### DECISION ON COMPLAINT

May 24, 2024

**CASE SUMMARY**

by a vote of 5-3, a session adopted a policy that worship services should
close with a benediction and not a doxology. Complainant RE Martinez voted
in favor. The long-time Senior Pastor did not agree with that decision, and two
Session members filed a complaint with the Session, which was denied. Then,
over the course of several meetings, Presbytery alternately sustained, denied,
and sustained complaints related to this Session decision, with the final
Presbytery decision being that the Session erred in its decision.
The SJC concludes that the Session had the authority to make that
decision ( _BCO_ 39-3.4), and that the liturgical decision was not a clear error of
judgment or discretion ( _BCO_ 39-3.3), and therefore the Complaint is sustained,
and Presbytery's action is annulled in whole.

**II. STATEMENT OF THE ISSUES**

1. as a matter o flaw ( _BCO_ 39-3.4) did the Session have constitutional

authority to instruct the minister to close worship with benedictions
instead of doxologies?

2. In exercising that authority, was there clear error in the Session's

discretion and judgment in deciding to close worship in that manner?
( _BCO_ 39.3.3)

**III. JUDGMENT**

1. Yes

2. No. Therefore, the Complaint is sustained. Presbytery's October 3,

2023 action in sustaining the Svendsen Complaint is annulled. This
returns the matter to where it stood after Presbytery sustained the

Martinez Complaint on May 2, 2023, meaning the Session's July 11,
2022 decision regarding benedictions and doxologies has not been
annulled.

**IV. REASONING AND OPINION**

The Presbytery erred when it sustained the Svendsen Complaint on October 3,
2023 at its stated meeting in Calabasas, CA. We note that Presbytery did not
adopt a statement of the issue or adopt any reasoning for why it sustained the
Svendsen Complaint and annulled the Session decision. While _BCO_ 43 does
not require such, it would have been helpful to both the congregation and
Session of Valley Presbyterian Church to know the reasons why Presbytery
was annulling an action of the Session.

In this Complaint, RE Martinez identified what amounted to two alleged
errors. One questioned the timeliness of the filing of the Svendsen Complaint
on July 1, 2023, but this was sufficiently addressed and explained by
Presbytery. It was timely because it was a _BCO_ 43-1 filing (60 days to the
original court) and not a _BCO_ 43-3 filing (30 days to the higher court). The
main error that was alleged is as follows.

"This act [of Presbytery sustaining the Svendsen Complaint

and annulling the Session decision] infringes upon the
jurisdiction of VPC Session's duties and charge in
maintaining the spiritual governing of the church ( _BCO_ 125.e; 11-2; 3-2; 4-3; 39-3)." ... "The Session is in fact charged
with overseeing the whole service, with all of its elements.
That is from the Call to the Benediction ( _BCO_ 12.5.e)."

In his Brief, Presbytery's Representative framed the issues as follows:

1. "The complainant [RE Martinez] claims that [Pacific

Presbytery] infringed upon the rights and duties of VPC
Session in regard to its government of public worship."

[Brief p. 2, items (c) and (d)]

2. "The complainant cites _BCO_ 4-3 and 12-5.e to defend

[VPC Session's] authority to restrict the closing of
worship at VPC only with (sic) formal benedictions."

In this SJC Decision, the authority issue is addressed in Issue 1. But because
we find that the Session had the authority to render its decision, a second issue
needs to be addressed regarding the exercise of judgment and discretion. This
Case involves both a matter of law (i.e., differing constitutional
interpretations) and a matter of discretion and judgment.

Part of the argumentation in the first Complaint against the Session's
benediction decision, sustained by Presbytery at its January 2023 meeting in
Santa Barbara, included quotes from _BCO_ 50, 51, and 52 regarding the
minister's discretion and responsibilities in worship. However, those chapters
are not part of our Constitution. The second Complaint against the same
Session decision, which was sustained by Presbytery at its October 2023
meeting in Calabasas, argued primarily that the Session's decision bound the
conscience of the minister. The issue of conscience is addressed in the

Reasoning below on Issue 2.

Regarding Issue 1 and Session authority, the Parties disagreed primarily on the
interpretation of _BCO_ 12-5.e and on how extensive a Session's authority is
regarding worship services. Complainant contends a Session has more
authority than what Presbytery contends. The disputed section is below, with
enumeration added for clarity.

_BCO_ 12-5. The church Session is charged with maintaining the spiritual
government of the church, for which purpose it has power:

e. To exercise, in accordance with the Directory for Worship,

1. authority over the time and place of the preaching of the
Word

2. and the administration of the Sacraments,
3. over all other religious services,
4. over the music in the services, and
5. over the uses to which the church building and associated
properties may be put;
6. to take the oversight of the singing in the public worship of
God;

7. to ensure that the Word of God is preached only by such men
as are sufficiently qualified ( _BCO_ 4-4, 53-2, 1 Timothy 2:1112);
8. to assemble the people for worship when there is no minister;
9. _to determine the best measures for promoting the spiritual_
_interests of the church and congregation;_ (emphasis added)

Considering _BCO_ 12.5.e, and particularly the final clause, the SJC finds that
the VPC Session had the constitutional authority to make the decision it made,
i.e., to have worship services closed with benedictions and not with
doxologies.

To cite a constitutional example, _BCO_ 58-1 explicitly grants a Session the
authority to determine _how often_ a congregation observes the Lord's Supper.
If a Session has authority over that extremely significant matter, then it's
reasonable to conclude a Session has authority over whether worship will close
with a benediction. [17]

Issue 2 questions whether this Session's constitutionally permitted exercise of
discretion and judgment was a clear error of discretion and judgment. ( _BCO_
39.3.3) We note first, regarding worship, both a Session and Presbytery have
authority and duties under _BCO_ 11-2.

_BCO_ 11-2. Secondly, they [church courts] have power to
establish rules for the government, discipline, _worship_, and
extension of the Church, which must be agreeable to the
doctrines relating thereto contained in the Scriptures, the
circumstantial details only of these matters being left to the
Christian prudence and wisdom of Church officers and courts.

There are probably many churches that exclusively use benedictions to close
worship, so it would seem difficult to demonstrate this injures the Church. In
the 1645 _Westminster Directory for the Public Worship of God_, in the section
titled "Of Prayer After Sermon," the final paragraph says:

17 _BCO_ 58-1. "The Communion, or Supper of the Lord, is to be observed
frequently; the stated times to be determined by the Session of each congregation,
as it may judge most for edification."

The prayer ended, let a psalm be sung, if with convenience it may
be done. After which (unless some other ordinance of Christ, that
concerns the congregation at that time, be to follow) let the
minister dismiss the congregation _with a solemn blessing_

[emphasis added].

In the 1645 Directory section titled, "Sanctification of the Lord's Day,"
we read:

That all the people meet so timely for public worship, that the
whole congregation may be present at the beginning, and with
one heart solemnly join together in all parts of the public worship,
and not depart until _after the blessing_ [emphasis added].

The 1879 PCUS _BCO_ contained the following (in what is now _BCO_
8-5):

when a minister is called to labor as a Pastor, it belongs to his
office to pray for and with his flock, as the mouth of the people
unto God; to feed the flock by reading, expounding, and
preaching the Word; to direct the congregation in singing the
praises of God; to administer the sacraments; _to bless the_
_people from God; ..._ [emphasis added].

In his 1898 Exposition on this section of the 1879 PCUS Book, F.P. Ramsay

wrote:

It belongs to the Pastor _to bless the people from God_ ;
wherefore the benediction is by him pronounced as an official
declaration of the divine mind [emphasis added].

In light of these considerations, we cannot say that the Session's decision in
this case that a benediction rather than a doxology is to be used at thee nd of
the worship service was a clear error of liturgical judgment under the standard
of _BCO_ 39-3.3, despite the fact that its longtime Senior Pastor disagreed. The
Session has the power to establish rules for worship, and with that power came
the discretion to set the policy about benedictions, a practice supported by the
historic authorities cited above. Decisions about the rules for worship remain
within the discretionary authority of a Session, even if Presbytery or other

Sessions or its own minister might have a different opinion, as long as such
decisions are not "clear error" (BCO 39-3.3), do not violate the Constitution
( _BCO_ 39-3.4), and are not contrary to the Scriptures.

Regarding the assertion about conscience made in TE Svendsen's Complaint,
sustained by Presbytery in May 2023, we are not persuaded the VPC Session
preference for benedictions over doxologies would reasonably result in a
violation of a minister's conscience. In his Complaint sustained at the May
2023 meeting, TE Svendsen contended that when Presbytery allowed the VPC
Session decision to stand, the Presbytery had "bound the conscience of a
minister by requiring him to follow his session's motion." He contended, "If it
is not scripturally required, then it is a commandment of men o vera matter of
worship and cannot be anything less that the binding of the conscience."
Similar arguments were made by Presbytery in its Brief in this present Case. [18]

However, we are not persuaded the Session's July 2022 decision involved a
matter like what is referenced in _Westminster Confession of Faith_ 20.2:

"God alone is Lord of the conscience, and hath left it free

from the doctrines and commandments of men, which are,
in anything, contrary to his Word; or beside it, if matters of
faith, or worship. So that, to believe such doctrines, or to
obey such commands, out of conscience, is to betray true
liberty of conscience: and the requiring of an implicit faith,
and an absolute and blind obedience, is to destroy liberty of
conscience, and reason also." [19]

18 _BCO_ Preliminary Principle 1: "God alone is Lord of the conscience and has
left it free from any doctrines or commandments of men (a) which are in any respect
contrary to the Word of God, or (b) which, in regard to matters of faith and worship,
are not governed by the Word of God."
_BCO_ 11-2: First, [church courts] can make no laws binding the conscience; but
may frame symbols of faith, bear testimony against error in doctrine and immorality
in practice, within or without the Church, and decide cases of conscience. Secondly,
they have power to establish rules for the government, discipline, worship, and
extension of the Church, which must be agreeable to the doctrines relating thereto
contained in the Scriptures, the circumstantial details only of these matters being left
to the Christian prudence and wisdom of Church officers and courts.
19 See also _WCF_ 17.3, 18.1, 18.4, 23.4 & _Larger Catechism_ questions 28, 36,
55, 80, 83, 89, 96, 105, 151 & 175.

While the minister believed he should have more freedom in how he closed

worship services, it is not reasonable to see how the Session's restriction would
have caused the minister to sin. And if the minister's conscience deemed it as

sin, he was free to ask (and should ask) an eligible elder to issue the
benediction instead.

Finally, an ongoing liturgical dispute between a long-time pastor and a
majority of elders on a Session might be something reasonably expected to
harm the peace of the Church, or something unsuited to promote the welfare
of the Church. It might also be reasonable to expect this given there have been
three complaints to Presbytery from the Pastor and members of the VPC
Session on this matter, and now this present one to the SJC. One cannot read
the Record of this Case without wondering how the ordination promise of
"subjection to your brethren in the Lord" was applied. ( _BCO_ 21-5, vow 4 and
_BCO_ 24-6, vow 5) However, the prior Martinez/Cook Complaint contended
this matter had not disrupted the collegiality of the Session, and that contention
was not disputed in the adjudication of that Complaint or in this present one.
Regardless, the effect of this disagreement on the Session, or on the Pastor, or
on Valley Presbyterian Church was not an issue raised in the Svendsen
Complaint before Presbytery in October 2023, or in this present one.
