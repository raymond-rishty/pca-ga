**CASE 2023-21**

#### DECISION ON COMPLAINT

**October 17, 2024**

**SUMMARY OF THE CASE**

This Complaint was filed by three Teaching Elders of the Korean Central
Presbytery (“KCP”). The case involves some of the same parties and issues as
SJC Case No. 2021-11. Presbytery appointed an investigative committee to
examine allegations of misconduct by a Teaching Elder. The TE was laboring
out of bounds as the senior pastor of an independent church, Covenant
Fellowship Church (“CFC”) in Champaign-Urbana, Illinois. After reviewing
the alleged offenses and meeting with the Accused, Presbytery determined not
to indict the Teaching Elder and to take no further action. The Complainants
timely filed to complain against that decision.

**I.** **SUMMARY OF THE FACTS**

because the primary alleged victim of the offense declined to

**II.** **STATEMENT OF THE ISSUE**

Did Korean Central Presbytery clearly err ( _BCO_ 39-3.2 and .3) in its _BCO_ 312 investigation and determination not to indict and to take no further action on
the allegations presented against the Accused?

**III.** **JUDGMENT**

No.

**IV.** **REASONING AND OPINION**

Presbytery has decided to dismiss the complaints." In its hearing brief

the Complaint had some degree of merit. It is unfortunate that an apparent error
in the translation of a Korean word created confusion about whether KCP

The reports of misconduct underlying this case include the original allegations
adjudicated by the SJC in Case No. 2021-11. This case also presents additional
allegations of misconduct. This case is similar to the former case in that many
of those allegations occurred in the distant past. The allegations of financial
misconduct came from a church treasurer who served from 1997 to 2007. She
left CFC in 2008. Another former staff member alleged manipulative and
abusive conduct from 2018 and 2019. a church member alleged that a fellow
member had been disciplined too lightly, or not at all, for an alleged incident
of sexual assault that occurred in 2000. Another member, the wife of a former
staff member, relayed several actions of the Accused in a timeline from 1990
to 2009.

KCP’s Investigation Committee reviewed these written statements and
determined that the Accused “did not correctly handle” one incident of sexual
misconduct by a church member in 2000. The Committee further reported that
the Accused “lacked wisdom” in many of his words and actions, but that it
would be difficult to prove that those bad acts constituted spiritual abuse. The
Committee indicated that the Accused disputed some of the claims. In
summary, the Committee concluded that it could not recommend that KCP
indict the Accused based on the allegations presented. We note that KCP
specifically evaluated the age of these allegations (and presumably the passage
of time) as one reason for not proceeding.

On the Record presented, we cannot say that KCP _clearly erred_ in reaching
this conclusion. Our Constitution requires a higher court to exhibit “great
deference” to lower courts regarding factual matters ( _BCO_ 39-3(2)) and
matters of discretion and judgment ( _BCO_ 39-2(3)). Judgments and decisions
based on such fact finding or the exercise of discretion and judgment may not
be reversed unless there is clear error on the part of the lower court. While the
Complainants allege that the Investigative Committee erred in failing to be
diligent or thorough enough in its work, we cannot say that KCP clearly erred
in acting on its Committee’s recommendations. KCP and its Committee faced
daunting questions arising from serious allegations of misconduct over a

#### DISSENTING OPINION ON COMPLAINT IN CASE 2023-21

**By RE Caleb Stegall, TE Art Sartorius, RE Dan Carrell,**

**TE Eric Landry, RE John Pickering, TE David Garner,**

**RE John Maynard, and TE Jay Bruce**

**NOVEMBER 5, 2024**

The SJC decision frames this case as a question of whether the Korean
Central Presbytery (KCP) clearly erred in its _BCO_ 31-2 investigation—and
answers that question in the negative. Contrary to the SJC Decision, however,
this does not end the matter. Indeed, it might be fair to say that this misses the
Complainants’ primary point.

The heart of the Complaint, as we read it, is not necessarily that KCP
got it wrong with respect to some factual finding or discretionary decision, but
rather that KCP never gave itself the opportunity to make any reasoned
judgment at all because it did not adequately investigate the allegations. To put
this in _BCO_ terms, the Complainants suggest that KCP’s _BCO_ 31-2
investigation was constitutionally deficient because it failed to comply with
that provision’s “due diligence” requirement. See _BCO_ 31-2.

Whether a lower court’s _BCO_ 31-2 investigation is constitutionally
sound in its process (as opposed to its factual findings or discretionary
judgments) is not a question on which the SJC owes any deference to the lower
courts. Determining the meaning and scope of the constitutional demand for
“due diligence” in a _BCO_ 31-2 investigation involves the “interpretation of the
Constitution of the Church” and “[r]egarding such issues, the higher court has
the duty and authority to interpret and apply the Constitution of the Church
according to its best abilities and understanding, regardless of the opinion of
the lower court.” _BCO_ 31-3.4.

We must, therefore, perform the analysis the majority opinion lacks.
Namely, was the investigation at issue in this case constitutionally sufficient—
or did it fall short of the “due diligence” required by _BCO_ 31-2? The case arose
in September 2022 after six former members and former staff of Covenant
Fellowship Church (CFC) in Champaign, Illinois wrote to KCP presenting
written allegations against the Accused. These accusations were treated as

“reports” at the Presbytery meeting of October 11-12, 2022, and assigned to
an investigative committee to conduct a _BCO_ 31-2 investigation. The
committee was instructed to report back to the Presbytery by the next
Presbytery meeting, and to include in its report the committee’s decision on
whether to indict or dismiss.

Prior to the next Presbytery meeting of April 18-19, 2023, the
investigative committee met six times, but did not hold any interviews or pose
questions to the Accusers. Regarding the Accusers, the committee only
reviewed the written reports. The sole person questioned by the investigative
committee was the Accused himself. He was interviewed on April 11, 2023,
but the Record of the Case only records a broad summary of his statement.

Nonetheless, it is clear from the written report submitted by the
investigative committee that some of the reported accusations may have been
admitted to by the Accused. The committee summarized the Accused’s
statements as follows:

- The Accused was operating in the “context of Korean culture and
church ministry when he planted CFC.”

- The Accused did use “strong language when rebuking or correcting
people.”

- The Accused “did not remember the exact words that he said to [one
of his Accusers] in times of prophetic rebuking and prayer, but he
admitted that his words could have been errant.”

- The Accused “gave his side of the story that differed from that of the
allegations.”

Furthermore, when stating its conclusions, the investigative
committee went on to add that “the committee believes that [the Accused]
lacked wisdom in many of his words and deeds at CFC. He mishandled cases
of sexual misconduct. He spoke wrongly in moments of rebuke or counsel. He
did not uphold financial accountability and transparency. His ways of
leadership led to many damaged relationships.”

It would not be wise to attempt to set forth precisely what is required
during the course of _BCO_ 31-2 investigations. The _BCO_ itself does not do this
beyond the requirement for due diligence. But the concept of “due diligence”
is not foreign to any of us, and it must be given adequate constitutional teeth.
“Due diligence” is well understood with a commonly accepted definition.
_Black’s Law Dictionary_ defines due diligence as: “Such a measure of
prudence, activity, or assiduity, a sis properly to be expected from, and
ordinarily exercised by, a reasonable prudent man under the particular
circumstances; not measured by any absolute standard, but depending on the
relative facts of the special case.” We agree with this definition, and see it as
an accurate expression of what should be required from lower courts
endeavoring to comply with _BCO_ 31-2.

As such, due diligence is inherently definable only in relation to the
nearly infinite varieties of specific circumstances that may arise in the course
of an investigation. We cannot, then, say what all future _BCO_ 31-2
investigations _must_ look like. But we can make the more limited judgment as
to what this particular _BCO_ 31-2 investigation _should have_ looked like.

And in our judgment, KCP was obligated by the constitutional
responsibility of due diligence in this instance to do more. Given the nature of
the allegations (including allegations of hiding cases of sexual misconduct and
physically abusing some persons within the church); the statements of the
Accused admitting at least some guilt; and the conclusions of the investigative
committee finding serious pastoral deficits, due diligence required the
investigative committee to conduct at least some follow-up investigation.

We do not reach such a conclusion lightly. We acknowledge that a fair
measure of leeway must be accorded lower courts to fashion their own
investigations. We also affirm that due diligence does not require lower courts
to act as we would have acted. It only requires such “prudence ... as is properly
to be expected from ... a reasonable prudent man under the particular
circumstances.” This standard must, however, take into account the overriding
principle that such investigations exist in order that lower courts may “exercise
care over those subject to their authority” to the end that the “peace, unity and
purity of the Church, and the honor and majesty of the Lord Jesus Christ, as
the King and Head thereof” might be preserved. See _BCO_ 31-2 and _BCO_ 314.

When allegations of serious wrongs—which we believe all SJC
members would readily concede do great harm to the peace, unity and purity
of the Church—have been found to have at least some measure of evidentiary
support, the reasonably prudent course of action demands that the investigation
not simply end. Care for both the Accused as well as those Church members
under his authority demands this. Such further investigation would serve to
either vindicate the Accused (thus caring for and preserving his future
ministry) or to vindicate his Accusers (thus caring for them by according
justice and preventing future abuses). As it is, however, here neither outcome
was reached. By prematurely ending its _BCO_ 31-2 investigation, KCP was
constitutionally deficient leaving no party satisfied and leaving the “peace,
unity and purity of the Church” in doubt.

The proper outcome here, in our judgment, is to remand this matter
back to the KCP to complete its investigation in a constitutionally sufficient
manner. Because the majority fails to even consider this question, and leaves
in place a deficient _BCO_ 31-2 investigation, we dissent.
