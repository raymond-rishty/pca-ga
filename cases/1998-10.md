# CASE 98-10
## REFERENCE OF TE BYRON CURTIS VS EASTERN CAROLINA PRESBYTERY

### I. Statement of Facts

1. Byron Curtis was examined and approved for ordination by Eastern Carolina Presbytery on July 20, 1985, followed by ordination and installation on August 18, 1985. At the time of his examination TE Curtis stated orally four exceptions to the *Westminster Confession of Faith* and the *Book of Church Order*, including one on the six days of creation.

2. In the summer of 1991, TE Curtis received a faculty appointment as assistant professor of Biblical Studies at Geneva College.

3. In the Summer/Fall of 1992, TE Curtis applied for transfer to Ascension Presbytery.

4. TE Curtis was denied transfer by Ascension on the basis of his exceptions to *WCF* and *BCO*, but was granted permission to labor in its bounds.

5. At its meeting on October 16, 1993, ECP approved the call of TE Curtis to his position at Geneva College and granted him permission to labor outside the bounds of ECP. Presbytery also attached to its minutes a written copy of TE Curtis’ prepared statement of exceptions.

6. On January 24, 1998, ECP approved a motion to establish a committee to investigate the theological views and related issues of TE Byron Curtis.

7. At its next meeting on April 18, 1998, ECP sustained a complaint against the action creating this investigating committee and dissolved the committee. ECP subsequently passed a motion requesting from TE Curtis clarification of his exceptions.

8. On July 18, 1998, a motion for ECP to adopt a policy of not receiving any men for licensure or installation to ministry into the presbytery who take exceptions to the Standards was defeated. At the same meeting a motion to prohibit TE Curtis from teaching his exceptions also was defeated.

9. TE Jeff Black complained against ECP’s action in not prohibiting TE Curtis from teaching his exceptions. His complaint was docketed for the next meeting of presbytery on October 17, 1998. Unable to complete its business, the complaint was carried over to an adjourned meeting on November 7, 1998. By a vote of 12 to 10 ECP sustained TE Black’s complaint and prohibited TE Curtis from teaching his exceptions. TE Curtis was not present at the November 7, 1998 meeting of Presbytery.

10. TE Curtis forwarded a complaint to General Assembly against the ECP action and sent a copy to ECP. After receiving TE Curtis’ complaint, ECP sent that complaint along with TE Black’s sustained complaint as a reference to GA.

## II. Recommendation

The panel recommends to the Standing Judicial Commission that the reference from Eastern Carolina Presbytery, entitled Case 98-10, be returned to Eastern Carolina Presbytery with the instruction that the Complaint of TE Byron Curtis be heard. (BCO 43-1 and 43-2.)

## III. Reasoning and Opinion

Eastern Carolina Presbytery referred this matter to the Standing Judicial Commission (ROC page 199). The SJC Officers acceded to this reference and referred this matter to the Panel for consideration. While the complaint of TE Byron Curtis was prematurely directed to the Standing Judicial Commission because it was not first considered by the lower court, it was also mailed to and received by ECP in a timely manner. After receiving TE Curtis’ complaint, ECP formally referred the complaint to the SJC. In addition, ECP also referred the complaint of TE Jeffrey Black it had already sustained.

The Respondents for ECP subsequently argued to the SJC that what ECP had intended to do was to refer the matter as a “non-judicial” reference to the Constitutional Business Committee for advice. However, since the matters referred related to judicial complaints already in process, these cases cannot be considered “non-judicial” and, therefore, they could only be referred to the Standing Judicial Commission.

While the case before the Panel is “judicial” and is ready to be adjudicated, it is also clear that the Respondents do have some grounds for arguing that ECP did ask specific questions for advice. (ROC 199). Yet, this case cannot be referred for mere “advice” (either from the SJC or CCB). Given this element of uncertainty and the principle that the lower court ought to hear cases before it, it is the judgment of this panel that this case be returned to ECP for adjudication at the next available stated meeting and that the substance of TE Curtis’ complaint be heard.

**Note:** This Opinion was written by RE John B. White, Jr., with the concurrence of TE David Hall and TE Robert Ferguson.

JUDICIAL MATTER 99-1
REQUEST TO GENERAL ASSEMBLY TO ASSUME ORIGINAL
JURISDICTION IN THE MATTER OF A TEACHING ELDER
FROM WESTERN CAROLINA PRESBYTERY, ET. AL

I. Background Information
1. At its March 1999 stated meeting, Western Carolina Presbytery asked Tennessee
Valley Presbytery (TVP) to investigate allegations that “Cedar Springs
Presbyterian Church, Knoxville, Tennessee, has had women preachers to fill its
pulpits.” The alleged actions took place at successive Sunday evening worship
services on August 16 and 23, 1998.
2. At its spring 1999 stated meeting, TVP, having been advised about Western
Carolina Presbytery’s request and concern, discussed the matter. There was
further discussion on this matter at TVP’s summer 1999 stated meeting (see text
of overture #1 adopted by TVP on 10/12/99). the stated clerk oft vp
formally acknowledged receipt of western Carolina Presbytery’s
communication by a letter dated August 3, 1999.
3. It was reported to TVP at its fall meeting (October 12, 1999) that three teaching
elders from the presbytery “met with TE John Wood, moderator of the cedar
Springs Session, to thoroughly discuss this decision” [i.e., “the decision of the
session of cedar springs pct o allow a woman on staff, under their authority
and guidance, to speak from the pulpit on two Sunday evenings, to explain her
biblical approach to Christian Education”].
4. Western Carolina Presbytery (on July 20, 1999), Calvary Presbytery (on July 24,
1999) and Ascension Presbytery (on July 31, 1999) adopted resolutions
requesting the General Assembly to assume original jurisdiction over a teaching
elder whom they allege allowed women to fill the pulpit in a PCA church. They
were invoking the provisions of b co 34-1, which deals with process against a
minister.
5. At its fall stated meeting (October 12,1999), TVP discussed the matter again and
adopted the following resolutions:
a that the session of cedar springs presbyterian church
(1) is in conformity with the standards of the PCA;
(2) ha snot acted in anyway injurious to the testimony of christ or his
church, and
(3) is in submission to their fellow elders, and are eager to maintain the
peace and purity of the church.
B. That TVP openly and plainly declares that it is not proper for women to
preach and teach in the church’s corporate worship.

#### II. Statement of the Issue

Is this matter properly before the General Assembly?

#### III. Judgment of the Case

No. This matter is not properly before the General Assembly.
