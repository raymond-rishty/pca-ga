Case 2000-9
COMPLAINT OF TE SUNG KEON KIM
VS.
KOREAN CAPITAL PRESBYTERY

#### I. Statement of the Facts

1. Beginning in 1999, division developed within the leadership of Korean
Presbyterian Church Washington (KPCW). Initially the dispute centered
principally on questions about the finances of Washington Theological
Seminary, which was connected with KPCW.
2. On December 30, 1999 the Session of KPCW submitted to Korean
Capital Presbytery (KCP) a reference asking KCP to help the session address
the financial question referred in item 1.
3. The situation escalated to the point that there were two groups both
claiming to be the true Session of KPCW. There was reported physical
violence at the church and police were called to resolve disputes. The
congregation was advised by civil authority that if the turmoil continued, the
church might be prohibited from meeting.
4. Subsequently, additional requests were made of KCP to investigate
financial mismanagement, a petition from members of the congregation of
KPCW asking KCP to dismiss RE Charles Kim and letters reporting on
“divisive and destructive activities of REs Charles Kim and Moon K. Ham.”
A total of 16 references, petitions, accusations and complaints were filed with
KCP regarding the situation at KPCW.
5. Certain KPCW officers sought in civil court to have existing church
trustees replaced without congregational approval.
6. To deal with these 16 petitions, references, complaints and
accusations, on October 9, 2000 at the reconvened portion of its 17th Stated
Meeting KCP appointed a commission to “suspend the functions of the KPCW
church Session and have the Presbytery replace the functions and report to the
Presbytery meeting in three months.”

7. TE Sung Keon Kim submitted to KCP a complaint dated Nov. 1, 2000
against the action of suspending the KPCW Session and having its commission
replace the session.
8. The Presbytery Commission, known as the Administrative
Commission and the Judicial Commission (it had been assigned both
functions), began its work on October 16, 2000 and commenced to act for the
session.
9. On October 15, 2000 the Commission arrived at KPCW to lead the
Sunday worship service but was prevented from doing so by members of the
congregation.
10. On October 16, 2000 three Ruling Elders and a Teaching Elder, acting
as the interim moderator of KPCW, advised the commission that “the
undersigned fully recognize the authority of the Commission and Judicial
Commission formed by the decision made at the 17th Stated Presbytery
meeting...”
11. The Commission on October 20, 2000 sought and ultimately received
a civil court order “to protect the church properties and to secure the safety of
church members.” The order restrained certain Ruling Elders from interfering
with the commission.
12. On November 1, 2000 TE Kim complained to the Standing Judicial Commission, which complaint was determined to be prematurely filed since it
had not been adjudicated by KCP.
13. The Commission acting for the Session of KPCW called three
congregational meetings on November 5, December 3, and 31, instituted ten
temporary church steering committees, changed the signers of various church
bank accounts, and completed the transfer of the church facilities purchased by
KPCW. At the December 3 meeting, KPCW congregation voted to consent to
the commission acting as its session.
14. The Commission, acting for the Session of KPCW fired TE Sung
Keon Kim as assistant pastor of the church.
15. At a called meeting of KCP on January 8, 2001 Presbytery approved
the actions of its commission and dismissed TE Kim’s complaints.
16. On January 15, 2001 TE Kim refiled his complaint with the Standing Judicial Commission.
17. A five-man panel of the SJC heard the complaint on December 3, 2001
at the McLean Presbyterian Church in McLean, VA.

#### II. Statement of the Issue

Did the Korean Capital Presbytery (KCP) err in its action of October
9, 2000 . . to suspend the functions of the Korean Presbyterian Church
Washington (KPCW) Session and have the Presbytery (commission) replace
the functions” (of the KPCW Session) without the prior consent of the
congregation?

#### III. Judgment

Yes. Therefore, the subsequent actions of the Commission acting as
Session of KPCW are annulled.

#### IV. Reasoning and Opinion

1. The action of KCP clearly violated Preliminary Principle Six in the
preface to the Book of Church Order which states that “... the power to elect
persons to the exercise of authority in any particular society resides in that
society.” (Emphasis added) Here KCP has appointed an Administrative and
Judicial Commission to suspend the Session of KPCW and to replace the
functions of the Session, without the approval or consent of the congregation
of KPCW - as a matter of fact without even contacting the congregation of
KPCW. In this instance, the appointment of the Commission by KCP was
unauthorized by the BCO; and therefore, its actions are not binding on the
session and congregation.
A word of explanation may be helpful to reaffirm the history and
purpose of these eight Preliminary Principles. Since the 16th Century
Protestant Reformation, there have been numerous Reformed denominations
with varying forms of church polity - some more hierarchical and others more
democratic. These eight principles were originally adopted by the first
American General Assembly in 1789. Our American Presbyterian forefathers
had come to America with fresh memories of the persecutions under the Act of
Supremacy fostered by Henry VIII in England. They did not want to form a
denomination that was governed “from the top down” but “from the bottom
up.”
In 1787, when the original four Synods agreed to have a General Assembly, they appointed a committee to first draft a series of preliminary
principles to be approved before the Book of Church Order was written. This
Committee worked for a year and presented these eight Preliminary Principles
to the meeting of the Synods in 1788. These Preliminary Principles were
approved so that the denomination would not be hierarchical in its polity.
they then appointed a committee to draft a Book of Church Order based on
these eight Preliminary Principles. this Book of Church Order was adopted at
the first American Presbyterian General Assembly in 1789.
It is interesting to note that by 1973 when the PCA was formed, both
PCUS and UPCUSA had removed those eight principles from their respective
constitutions and had become hierarchical denominations.
In 1973, after we had decided to separate from PCUS and before the
PCA was actually formed, we called our group THE CONTINUING
CHURCH, meaning that we intended to organize a denomination continuing
the polity that our American forefathers adopted in 1789 based on these eight
principles.
This action of KCP violated Principle Six of the Preliminary Principles
in that it gave authority to its Commission to suspend those Session members

elected by the congregation “to the exercise of authority” in KPCW, and to
replace them with those of the choosing of KCP or its Commission.
This action similarly violated BCO 16-2 which states that “... the right
of God’s people to recognize by election to office those so gifted is
inalienable. Therefore no man can be placed over a church in any office
without the election, or at least the consent of that church.” (Emphasis added)
The dictionary defines the meaning of the word “inalienable” as follows:
(1) incapable of being surrendered
(2) incapable of being transferred
(3) that which one cannot give away or dispose of, even if one wishes.
KCP argues that it had authority for such action under BCO 13-9
which grants power to Presbytery:
“... In cases in which the Session cannot exercise its
authority, it shall have power to assume original
jurisdiction.”
KCP has misinterpreted this language for their interpretation conflicts
with the provisions of the Preliminary Principles and BCO 16-2, above. The
general rule of interpretation of the BCO is that no provision can be properly
interpreted if it conflicts with any of the Preliminary Principles. Thus, if a BCO
provision is interpreted so that it conflicts with a Preliminary Principle, such
interpretation is erroneous. Every interpretation of the BCO after the
Preliminary Principles in the Preface must be consistent with such Preliminary
Principles - otherwise the interpretation is in error. KCP made such a
misinterpretation.
2. This case sets before the SJC the constitutional status of the Presbytery
(KCP) and its relationship and power to deal with a problem in one of its local
congregations (KPCW). The PCA functions at three separate levels - the
General Assembly, the Presbytery and the local church (with the Session, as
chosen by the congregation, being the authoritative body at the local level).
Each of these levels exists in two ways or with two concurrent identities. First,
as an ecclesiastical body and second, as a civil body (the precise legal nature of
the civil identity is governed by local civil law). Each of these courts of the
church exercises two distinct kinds of authority - first ecclesiastical authority
and the second civil authority. Such ecclesiastical authority is defined in the
Preliminary Principles as “only ministerial and declarative” or “purely moral
or spiritual in its object.” (Preliminary Principles Seven and Eight). Such civil
authority is determined by the civil law where the church is located.
This ecclesiastical authority is morally binding on the lower court, but
it is not legally binding or authoritative o vera lower court ina civil sense. In
an effort to explain this distinction the 1986 General Assembly adopted the
final recommendations and concluding proposals of the Ad Interim Committee
on Structure which stated that a higher court may “Act On” or “Act Against” a
matter before it from a lower court; but a higher court cannot “Act For” the

lower court (14th General Assembly Minutes pp. 104-105). The General Assembly then defined the meaning of the term “Act For” as follows:
“Act For” - a higher court taking an action on a matter
over which a lower court had original jurisdiction, which
action is as conclusive of the matter as if it had been made
by the lower court and is similarly enforceable.” (14th
General Assembly Minutes page 105).
The 15th General Assembly defined the words “church power” to
mean:
“The ability to require (force) compliance with the
decision of the courts.” (Min. 15 G.A. p. 480.)
clearly the preliminary principles in the preface to the Book of Church Order state that such power is “only ministerial and declarative” and “must be
purely moral and spiritual in its nature” (Preliminary Principles Seven and
Eight of BCO Preface).
The remainder of the Book of Church Order is consistent in setting
forth the nature of church power as:
Section 3-2 “Ecclesiastical power which is wholly spiritual...”
Section 3-3 “The sole functions of the Church as a Kingdom and
government, distinct from the civil commonwealth, are to
proclaim, to administer, and to enforce the law of Christ revealed
in the Scriptures.”
Section 3-4 “The power of the Church is exclusively spiritual; and
that of the State includes the use of force.”
In this case, KCP sought to “Act For” the congregation and Session of
ICPCW, and in doing so KCP erred. The congregation and Session elected by
the congregation had original jurisdiction of actions or failures to act by
members of the Session of KPCW and KCP sought to “Act For” the lower
court. If KPCW did not follow the instructions of the Administrative and
Judicial Commission of KCP, then KCP would have had to go to civil court to
enforce its action, which it has no authority to do. KCP has no civil
connection to KPCW for that would be “Acting For” KPCW and is contrary to
the BCO. KCP and its Commission failed to properly interpret the power and
authority given it by the BCO.
3. BCO 14-7 provides for the manner in which previous decisions in
judicial cases “may be appealed to in subsequent similar cases as to any
principle which may have been decided.” (Emphasis added)
We appeal to a similar case and to the principle decided therein
namely:
RE Charles E. Chappell vs. Eastern Carolina Presbytery
(Case 90-4, PCA Digest Volume 1, Part III Pages 430-442).

In that case, Eastern Carolina Presbytery appointed a commission and
clothed “it with full power of Presbytery to resolve all matters of conflict at
Fuller Memorial Church, Durham” (p. 430).
The Commission of East Carolina Presbytery then suspended the
entire Session with the Commission acting as Session Pro-Tem (p. 430-C).
The Commission of East Carolina Presbytery argued that the basis of the
authority of Presbytery comes from the powers of Presbytery listed in BCO-13
(p. 431-E). This is exactly the same argument that KCP made in this case.
In this East Carolina Presbytery case two of the issues were stated (p.
433) as follows:
“2. Did East Carolina Presbytery have the authority
and power to suspend the members of the Session of
Fuller Memorial Church, without consent of the
congregation and without due process?
“3. Did Eastern Carolina Presbytery or its
Commission have authority and power to constitute
itself as a Session Pro Tem for Fuller Church and
make all decisions therefore?” (p. 433).
The Judgment in this East Carolina Presbytery case on the above two
issues was as follows:
“2. the Book of Church Order doe snot give a
presbytery the authority and power to suspend
members of a Session without the consent of the
congregation and without due process. The
Commission of Eastern Carolina Presbytery erred in
so doing.
“3. While the Commission may have believed its
replacing the Fuller Session was under taken with
consent (there was no formal vote on this issue), its
action had the effect of removing and replacing the
Session with one that had not been elected by the
Fuller congregation, which action of the Commission
exceeded the jurisdiction given by the Book of Church Order, (p. 433-434).”
We believe the principles in the East Carolina Presbytery case are
applicable in this case; and we appeal to it as precedent for our judgment
herein.
4. Another supporting reason for this Judgment is the lack of original
jurisdiction of KCP over the church members and session members of KPCW.
Consider the following Book of Church Order provisions relating to original
jurisdiction over church members and session members of KPCW, to-wit:

(1) BCO 33-1 states that “Process against all church members, other
than ministers of the Gospel, shall be entered before the Session
to which the members belong.
(2) BCO 31-1 states that “original jurisdiction in relations to church
members shall be, in the Session of the church of which he/she
is a member.”
Contrary to these explicit statements in the PCA Book of Church Order, KCP sought to exercise original jurisdiction over the members and
session of KPCW. The KCP suspension of the KPCW Session and the
appointment of the Administrative and Judicial Commission to act as and for
the Session, without the approval of the congregation and due process, was an
attempt to exercise original jurisdiction over the members and session of
KPCW. This action was contrary to the PCA - BCO and our historical
standards. Therefore, the commission’s actions are not binding on the session
or congregation.

White. The Issue and Judgment were prepared by the entire Panel of five
members. The Reasoning and Opinion was written by RE W. Jack Williamson
with concurrence by TE Charles McGowan, TE Jim Smith, RE Grant McCabe
and RE John White.

#### II. Statement of the Issue

Was Presbytery and/or its Commission appointed on October 9, 2000
the proper court to assume original jurisdiction to receive and adjudicate the
charges against the Appellant Charles C. Kim?

#### III. Judgment

No, such original jurisdiction should be with the Session; therefore,
the Presbytery’s action and/or the Judicial Commission of Presbytery’s actions
in this matter are reversed.
