**IV.** **REASONING AND OPINION**

Judgment 1
The Panel finds that Westminster Presbytery (WP) erred in four separate ways
in conducting the _BCO_ 31-2 investigation of Kacey Moehn.

First, WP erred by hampering their own investigation with an extra
confessional understanding of _WCF_ 24.6.

Although the corruption of man be such as is apt to study arguments
unduly to put asunder those whom God hath joined together in
marriage: yet, nothing but adultery, or such willful desertion as can no
way be remedied by the church, or civil magistrate, is cause sufficient
of dissolving the bond of marriage: wherein, a public and orderly
course of proceeding is to be observed; and the persons concerned in
it not left to their own wills, and discretion, in their own case.

The Presbytery does not believe the desertion clause applies to a professing
Christian. They believe that in order for desertion by a Christian spouse to be
a valid reason for divorce, such a person would have to be excommunicated

by the church prior to a divorce. The Confession, however, simply refers to the
act of desertion and not the spiritual state of the one who has deserted his
spouse (in a similar fashion, the Confession also speaks simply of the act of
adultery and not the spiritual state of the one who commits adultery). This extra
confessional understanding created a standard of guilt for the Moehn
Committee that Mrs. Moehn would never meet so long as she continued to
hold what they perceived to be a credible profession of faith regardless of the
choices she made.

Second, WP erred by dismissing Mrs. Moehn’s physical separation from TE
Moehn as evidence of desertion. The Moehn Committee was wrong when it
reported to the Presbytery that the fact of Mrs. Moehn moving out of the family
home was “outside of the scope of the accusations and the investigation.”
Clearly, a spouse moving out of the family home and into anew home, not to
mention accompanying that move with allegedly inequitable financial and
child-care responsibilities, must at least be considered in determining whether
that spouse may be guilty of desertion.

Third, Presbytery erred in not having before it all relevant evidence that would
bear on the accuracy of the conclusions reached through the _BCO_ 31-2
investigation. In particular, the Moehn Committee chose not to interview Dr.
Carolyn Arnold, the therapist who directed the Intensive Marriage therapy for
the Moehns in April 2023. She was the last counselor who worked with the
Moehns together. Surely, then, her conclusions regarding the Moehns’
relationship and Mrs. Moehn’s commitment to the marriage would have been
critical to ascertaining whether Mrs. Moehn might be guilty of desertion and
whether TE Moehn might have biblical grounds for divorce.

Fourth, WP erred in its 31-2 investigation of Mrs. Moehn by increasingly
turning its focus to TE Moehn, ultimately placing a significant portion of the
blame on him and his actions, which they believe led to the dissolution of his
marriage. Along with the Session of Walnut Hill and WP, the Panel recognizes
generally that both spouses share some blame when a marriage deteriorates.
The result of the investigation of Mrs. Moehn, however, led WP to adjudicate
informally TE Moehn’s role in the marriage’s demise without his having the
benefit of the protections offered by formal process or even a _BCO_ 31-2
investigation. Several examples should suffice:

- The Moehn Committee believes that if TE Moehn had pursued Mrs.
Moehn, neither the Moehns nor the Presbytery would be in this
situation today.

- WP blames TE Moehn for desiring to end the marriage, despite the
ROC indicating that both TE Moehn and Mrs. Moehn had
communicated to Walnut Hill’s Session their desire to seek a divorce

- WP finds it difficult to fault Mrs. Moehn for moving out of the family
home since TE Moehn slept in a different bedroom and asked for the
investigation.

- WP asserts that TE Moehn created an intolerable situation for Mrs.

Moehn at Walnut Hill PCA.

WP could have concluded their investigation of Mrs. Moehn and then
recommended that WP begin a new investigation of TE Moehn, but they did
not do so despite the many accusations made against him and his Christian
character that are now public.

Judgment 2
TE Moehn’s original request to the Session of Walnut Hill PCA included an
inquiry into whether or not he would have grounds for divorce. WP, having
consented to Session’s request that Presbytery take jurisdiction over these
matters, and having determined there was no strong presumption of guilt with
regard to Mrs. Moehn, concluded “Because we do not find a strong
presumption of guilt, it renders the question of the biblical grounds of the
divorce moot.”

While the word “moot” can have various meanings, it appears from the context
and by the arguments put forth by Presbytery’s representatives that
Presbytery’s conclusion was “there is no need to discuss whether TE Moehn
has grounds for divorce because Mrs. Moehn continues to be a professing
Christian and our _BCO_ 31-2 investigation gives us no reason to question the
credibility of that profession.” But, by extension, that means Presbytery’s
advice, albeit implicit, was that TE Moehn does not have biblical grounds for
divorce. In their briefs and arguments, both parties recognized this conclusion.
That determination, in turn, rests on Presbytery’s extra-confessional reading
of _WCF_ 24.6. Further, even if Presbytery’s understanding of _WCF_ 24.6 were
correct, the other errors in the conduct of Presbytery’s investigation would
render this conclusion suspect.

Finally, all parties agree that a determinative finding with regard to whether
TE Moehn has biblical grounds for divorce could only be arrived at after an
ecclesiastical trial, not simply as a result of an investigation. This is not to say,
however, that WP did not have the right to render advice to TE Moehn. But
that advice, when taken as a formal action of Presbytery, must be grounded in
a proper understanding of Scripture and our Constitutional Standards, and on
a proper investigation. Given the errors noted above, WP erred in stating that
lack of a strong presumption of guilt with regard to Mrs. Moehn rendered the
question of whether TE Moehn had biblical grounds for divorce moot,
particularly when the thrust of that conclusion clearly indicated that the
meaning was that TE Moehn did not have biblical grounds for divorce. This
error was compounded by the fact that so much of the Moehn Committee’s
report dealt with TE Moehn, who was not the subject of any formal
investigation, and by the fact that the results were reported and discussed in
open session, which could only harm the reputation of both parties.

Judgment 3
The Session of Walnut Hill PCA allowed Mrs. Moehn to remove her

membership and join a nondenominational church. WP no longer has any
jurisdiction over Mrs. Moehn, and it would be improper for WP formally to
conduct a new investigation into her Christian character.

A Final Note on the Judgment in this Case
Nothing in this decision can or does render a judgment as to whether Mrs.
Moehn was or is guilty of desertion or whether TE Moehn has biblical grounds
for divorce. Our ruling asserts only that the procedures and the confessional
understanding used by Presbytery were clearly deficient, and, therefore, the
conclusions reached by Presbytery cannot stand.

**DISSENT**
**RE Caleb Stegall**

**March 10, 2025**

May a marriage between Christian spouses be biblically dissolved on
the grounds of desertion? This question has vexed and divided scholars,
theologians, and elders within the reformed faith for centuries. The Confession
states: “nothing but adultery, or such willful desertion as can no way be
remedied by the church, or civil magistrate, is cause sufficient of dissolving
the bond of marriage.” _WCF_ 24.6. Whether the clause “as can no way be
remedied by the church” renders the grounds of desertion lawfully unavailable
to believing spouses ina christian marriage has remained a matter of some
debate and ambiguity.

This dissent is not the place to recount the lengthy scholarship,
exegesis, or historical record surrounding the debate. Indeed, the PCA has
largely completed and documented that work. The 16 [th] General Assembly of
the PCA appointed an ad-interim committee to study this question, asking it in
part “to consider whether there could be other legitimate grounds for divorce
besides ‘desertion by an unbelieving spouse’ or ‘adultery’.” Report of the AdInterim Committee on Divorce and Remarriage to the Twentieth General
Assembly at 183 (adopted as a Position Paper of the PCA by the 20 [th] General
Assembly and hereinafter referred to as the Report).

The Report acknowledges as a starting point that:

“the general consensus among Reformed believers is the view
that the Bible neither condones nor commands divorce, but
rather permits and regulates divorce due to sin. However, a
person can divorce only for adultery and _separation of an_
_unbelieving spouse_ . In the words of the Westminster
Confession of Faith: ‘...nothing but adultery, or such wilful
desertion as can no way be remedied by the church or civil

magistrate, is cause sufficient of dissolving the bond of
marriage.’ ( _WCF_ 24:6). John Stott and John Murray are
among those who insist that such permission is given only in
negative and reluctant terms. According to Stott: ‘Only if a
person divorces his partner on the ground of marital
unfaithfulness is his remarriage not adulterous. Only if the
unbeliever insists on leaving is the believer not bound.’”
Report at 204 (emphasis added).

Thus, the Report takes the rather unambiguous position that the natural
and historically accepted reading of _WCF_ 24.6 is that it does _not_ permit
divorce between Christian spouses on the grounds of desertion. The Report
does, however, give ample attention to the opposing view that neither the text
of _WCF_ 24.6 nor Paul’s scriptural injunctions (particularly those found in I
Corinthians 7) limit the lawful grounds for divorce between Christian spouses
to adultery. And specifically, that desertion must be understood as a lawful
ground of divorce even between believing spouses.

Perhaps the closest the authors of the Report take to committing to a
position is found in their recommendations (f) and (g), both of which were
adopted by the 20 [th] General Assembly. Recommendation (f) states: “That when
believers divorce for other than Biblical grounds, they should remain
unmarried or else be reconciled (1 Cor. 7:11).” Report at 293. And
recommendation (g) states: “That when an unbeliever separates from the
marriage relationship with a believer, the believer is free from that marriage
and free to remarry but only in the Lord (1 Cor. 7:15, 39).” Report at 293.
Read together, these recommendations at least create the reasonable inference
that if the “separation” is between two believing spouses, a subsequent divorce
is unbiblical and remarriage is not permitted as a consequence. Though the
inference is not made explicit.

This silence is consistent with the tenor of the Report as a whole which
throughout takes great care to render a good faith and fair recitation of each of
these opposing views, recognizing and taking into account all of the exegetical
complications, pastoral concerns for abused and victimized spouses, and
historical and cultural contingencies represented by both the scriptural and
confessional language. And in the end, given the difficulties and sensitivities
of the question, the Report does not actually answer the question it was charged
to answer. It does not directly declare a position for the PCA either way. In

other words, it does not affirm that biblically permissible divorce is limited to
only circumstances of “adultery” or “desertion by an unbelieving
spouse.” But neither does it reject that position as too limiting.

Instead, it commends the exhaustive nature of the Report and the
pastoral advice it contains to the courts of the church for their sound and
careful consideration as they are required to deal with factually unique and
complex questions concerning individual marriages within the church. In this,
the Report is assiduous to honor the principle that in “unclear cases …
judgment will have to rest with the Session in their application of biblical
principles” and that we “should allow Sessions the liberty to discern with much
prayer what would be the proper response in particular
circumstance[s].” Report at 223 & 227.

In my view, this approach is both wise and prudent—and it forms the
basis of my dissent. For the SJC decision in this case authoritatively declares
(at least for the parties and, practically speaking, for the PCA as a whole) that
_WCF_ 24.6 expressly permits divorce between Christian spouses on the grounds
of desertion. And it does so with almost no discussion.

The decision simply recites that Westminster Presbytery “does not
believe the desertion clause applies to a professing Christian” and then
declares that the “Confession, however, simply refers to the act of desertion
and not the spiritual state of the one who has deserted his spouse (in a similar
fashion, the Confession also speaks simply of the act of adultery and not the
spiritual state of the one who commits adultery).” The SJC describes the view
held by Westminster Presbytery (that the “desertion clause” does not apply to
“a professing Christian”) as an “extra confessional understanding” which
“created a standard of guilt”—that is, an evidentiary threshold for a finding of
biblical grounds for dissolving the Moehn marriage—that could never be met
“so long as [Mrs. Moehn] continued to hold what [Presbytery] perceived to be
a credible profession of faith regardless of the choices she made.” Decision at
6.10-20.

This outcome is made more surprising by the fact that _both_ of the
parties below hold the view that is condemned by today’s decision.
Westminster Presbytery clearly takes the view that desertion is only grounds
for a biblically sanctioned divorce if the deserting party is found to be an
unbeliever. But so too does TE Moehn. At the hearing on this matter, a panel

member asked TE Moehn, “Am I hearing you correctly, when you said you
agree that desertion has to be by a non-Christian spouse you mean by that one
who … either has no profession or who is behaving in a way that is inconsistent
with a profession?” TE Moehn answered, “Correct.” Hearing at 2:07:302:08:05.

It is clear the point of contention between the parties is _not_ whether
desertion o fa believing spouse creates grounds fora biblical ly sanctioned
divorce (the parties agreed it does not). They disagree instead over what
evidence may suffice for a court to declare that the deserting spouse is in fact
an unbeliever. Westminster Presbytery takes the seemingly untenable position
that excommunication is required, while TE Moehn takes the more reasonable
view that where excommunication is impossible (due to the deserting spouse
having left the PCA), a court may weigh the deserting spouse’s profession of
faith against that spouse’s actions and find it to be not credible. The SJC could
have resolved this matter on those grounds but chose not to.

Given the extraordinary pedigree of this debate within the reformed
faith broadly, and within the PCA specifically, it is remarkable that the SJC has
purported to resolve it in one scant paragraph. To be clear, today the SJC holds
that it is unconstitutional for a court of the PCA to consider the “spiritual state”
of the deserting party within a marriage when weighing questions of whether
a divorce may be lawfully permitted under our standards. For all practical
purposes, this amounts to a total rejection of what the Report described as “the
general consensus among Reformed believers” that the Bible and the
Confession permit divorce “only for adultery and _s_ eparation of an unbelieving
spouse.” Report at 204.

This dissent, like the Report itself, takes no position on the question. I
agree with today’s decision that the question is a constitutional one on which
the SJC owes no deference to the lower courts. _BCO_ 39-3.4. My position is
not precisely one of deference. Instead, I would find that on questions such as
this one—which have admitted to alternative interpretive stances across the
church, confirmed by long-standing practice, tradition, and authoritative
guidance—the SJC ought not find error when a lower court consistently
applies one of the acceptable interpretations. i would therefore commend a
resolution of the question to the various judgments of individual Sessions and
Presbyteries as they weigh the individual cases that come before them.

Thus, in light of the vexing nature of the question at hand, its historical
development, varying practice across the PCA, and the Report adopted as the
position of the PCA on these matters by the 20 [th] General Assembly, I would
not find error in Westminster Presbytery’s view of _WCF_ 24.6.
