1 We, the undersigned, respectfully submit this concurring judgment in support of
the majority decision in this case.
2. We support the Standing Judicial Commission’s judgment:
A. that the Westminster Confession of Faith, when it refers to “in the space
of six days” (WCF IV, 1) adopted substantially the language of the
English translation of the Bible, and
B. that the use of such scriptural language permits officers in the PCA
1 to interpret Genesis 1-3 as meaning that the world was created in
six consecutive 24 hour days, or
2. to hold other interpretations which affirm that God created the
world.
While there may be more than one way to interpret or understand the words, “in
the space of six days,” (which are not to be understood as exceptions to the
Confessional standards of the PCA), any purely naturalist evolutionary
interpretation is not compatible with our Confessional standards.
3. In support of the majority opinion we offer the following:
A. The issue in this case is whether there is only one acceptable
interpretation of the WCF phrase, “in the space of six days.”
1 The minority opinion holds that there is only one acceptable
view in the PCA - the “24 hour day” view.
2. This concurring opinion affirms that the “24 hour day” view,
while very plausible (indeed, a number of the undersigned hold
strongly to this view), is not the only possible faithful
interpretation.
B. The issue in this case is not about whether the Genesis account is
entirely true, factual and historical. It is! No one is questioning these
fundamentals:
1 that God created all things, by the word of his power, out of
nothing, and all very good,
2. that God created man male and female in his own image, endued
with righteousness and holiness, yet capable of sin,
3. that God created Adam and Eve without intermediaries, from the
dust of the earth, breathing life into them; mankind is not a
product of evolution, and
4. that Scripture does not err. What we read in Genesis 1 and 2 is
historically accurate and true to fact.

While our interpretations may err, Scripture does not err. Everyone is seeking to
be faithful to the text of Scripture, and desires not to misinterpret it.
C the Westminster Confession of Faith itself acknowledges that “all
things in Scripture are not alike plain in themselves, nor alike clear to
all” (WCF, 1:7). This is one of those places. While we may argue
strongly for our view of “24 hour days,” we have to admit that the text is
not as clear as we would desire. There are questions raised in the text
itself that have led some of our brothers to hold differing views as to the
time element. For example:
1. Why do the sun and moon not appear until the fourth day9
Hence, how could there be a ‘literal’ evening and morning during
the first three days? Also, Genesis 1:14 reveals that the heavenly
bodies were made on the fourth day in order to measure days and
seasons and years.
2. Are Genesis 1:12 and 2:9 presenting us with instances of plants
growing gradually over a period of time? of course, God could
cause them to grow instantaneously, but if we are being asked to
take them according to the natural sense of the words, are we not
compelled to view their growth as taking place over a period of
time?
3. Are we to take the account of Genesis 2:5-25 as occurring in day
six of chapter one? If so, it was a rather long and full “24 hour
day.”
4 Is not the Hebrew word for “day” used in at least three different
ways in Genesis 1:1-2:4 alone? Would this not wave a caution
flag concerning the use of the word “day”?
5. How are we to understand the creation of light on two different
days, the first and the fourth?
6. How come the events in Genesis 2 are mentioned in a different
literary order than those in chapter one?
7. Are we to understand from the text and from the whole context of
Scripture that we are still in the seventh day; and if so, how does
that reflect on our understanding of the firsts ix days?
these a rebut a few of the legitimate questions that rise, not from
science, but from the text; and not everyone is reaching the same
conclusions about the timing of the six days. All agree that God created
“in the space of six days,” but they do not agree that the word “day”
must only be interpreted as a “24 hour day.”
