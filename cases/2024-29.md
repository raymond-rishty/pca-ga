**February 19, 2025**

This case is administratively out of order and cannot be put in order. The
Complainant is not a member of any congregation of the PCA and lacks
standing to bring a complaint ( _BCO_ 43-1). This decision is controlled by the
SJC’s decisions in SJC Cases _Benyola v. Central Florida_ 2022-17, 18, and 19.
The Complaint in this case seeks to address the same issues presented in those
actions. The Complainant’s membership status has not changed since those
decisions. The Complaint is dismissed.

**IV. PROPOSED MANUAL CHANGES**

**Item 1 – Documents included in the Record**

_OMSJC_ 7.2 (c)

Add a new _OMSJC_ 7.2.c and reletter the current sections “c” and “d” to “d”

and “e,” with the new _OMSJC_ 7,2,c to read as follows:

Only documents provided to the Office of the Stated Clerk can be included in
the Record of the Case. No document shall be included in the Record of the

Case that must be accessed through external links, websites, QR codes, filesharing services, or other external digital platforms.

Rationale:

1. In recent years, many Presbyteries have moved to using links, Dropbox
links, or QR codes to allow Presbyters to access documents.
2. Sometimes the Presbytery or parties then continue that convention by
seeking to include document in the record o fa case by including a
reference to a link or code.

3. Members of the SJC do not have access to those links or codes. As a result,
they cannot access any parts of the Record that are included by those links
or codes.

4. Further, even if the SJC is provided with those links or codes, there is no
way to ascertain whether the documents thus accessed, assuming they are
even still available at the link, are the same as the documents that were
originally before the lower court.
5. This amendment simply ensures that all parts of the Record of a Case are
readily available to members of the SJC.

**Item 2 – Objections to the Record**

_OMSJC_ 7.4 (d) and (e)

1. Add a new paragraph d. and reletter the current paragraphs d., e. and f. to
e., f. and g., with the new paragraph d. to read as follows:

d. If a party objects to any portion of the Record, the other party
shall respond to such objection within 15 days of receipt of
notification of the objection. Such response shall be in writing
and must be filed by mail or electronic means with Stated
Clerk and the Panel Chairman, or the Chairman of the
Commission if the case is to be heard by the Commission. The
response must also be copied to the party that lodged the
objection. If a party, having received notice that an objection
to the Record has been filed, fails to respond to that objection
within 15 days they shall be deemed to have acceded to the
objection.

2. Amend the current paragraph d. (to be relettered to e.) as follows:

e. If the other party shall accede to the objection, it shall be so
stipulated in writing and made a part of the Record of the
Case. Such stipulation shall be filed by mail or electronic
means with the Stated Clerk and the Panel Chairman, or the
Chairman of the Commission if the case is to be heard by the
Commission, not more than 30 days after the date the last
party received such Record of the Case from the Stated Clerk.

**Rationale:**

1. The current provisions stipulate what happens if the other party accedes to
an objection or does not accede, but there is nothing explicit about what
happens if the other party fails to respond.
2. It is not evident why parties have only 15 days to lodge an objection to the
ROC but have 30 days to respond to an objection (assuming that is what
the current paragraph d means). Further, allowing 30 days will often
unnecessarily prolong the time needed to adjudicate the Case.

3. The current paragraph d is difficult to follow. If the last clause means the
other party has 30 days to respond to an objection, why is that tied to
receipt of the Record rather than receipt of the objection? What is meant
by “such Record?” etc.
4. Nothing in the current paragraph d requires that the party that lodged the
objection be notified of the other party’s response. If the party lodging an
objection must notify the other party, it seems reasonable that the other
party should have to notify the objecting party of their response.
5. Being clear that failure to respond to an objection will constitute acceding
to that objection will force parties to operate within the deadline.

10.1 Ordinarily, A Judicial Panel should shall consist of three at least

five qualified ( _OMSJC_ 2.3.d.) members of the Commission,
which should shall include at least one two teaching elders and
one two ruling elders. In more serious cases, larger panels may

10.2 At least one ruling elder and at least one teaching elder shall be

named as alternates for each Judicial Panel. An alternate will not

be expected to attend the meeting of the Judicial Panel unless he
is needed to meet the quorum. Should, in the judgment of the
officers, circumstances warrant, larger panels may be appointed
with an approximately equal balance between teaching and ruling
elders.

6.2 a quorum forth e transaction of business at any meeting o fa

Judicial Panel appointed hereunder shall be the number of the
members of the Judicial Panel appointed as principals a majority
of the Panel members, which shall include at least one teaching
elder and one ruling elder.

RATIONALE

Experience has taught us that the current provisions for alternates are
inadequate ina number of ways, e.g., it is critical f oran alternate, that might
be called upon to act in place of an incapacitated qualified member, to be fully

informed of all Panel proceedings, and yet he is not expected to attend Panel
meetings.
However, experience also suggests that the pragmatic reason for
alternates has expired, i.e., that there be members who could fill a quorum for
in-person meetings. The old rule was put in place when SJC Panels flew to
out-of-state, in-person Panel Hearings, which necessitated having a TE and an
RE alternate because rescheduling would be very costly. But those Hearings
are now conducted via videoconference.

The value of a larger Panel has also been demonstrated by experience,
as most of our Panels allow full participation of alternates except for
parliamentary actions. And, most of our Panels are nearly unanimous, even
including the views of the non-voting parties in their reports. A larger Panel
holds promise for a more likely representation of the full Commission, and
thus a more likely approval, and removes all the practical impediments of the
“alternates” system.
It is unlikely that this change will create more work for SJC members
or increase the time it takes to adjudicate a case.
