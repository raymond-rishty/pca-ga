**RATIONALE:**

Indefinite suspension from office is a distinct and insular censure of the Church
( _BCO_ 30-3) and is subject to review by the higher courts via appeal or
complaint.

By contrast, the procedural mechanism and kind of process applicable to
_elevating_ indefinite suspension from office to deposition from office is less
clear as has been demonstrated recently in the SJC case _TE Aaron Myers v._
_Iliana Presbytery_ ( _M51GA_, 2024, page 824) a swell as formerly in _Dallison v._
_North Florida Presbytery_ ( _M30GA_, 2002, page 156).

It would therefore be wise for the Assembly to enact changes to our Rules of
Discipline to promote clarity and uniformity regarding agreeable and
appropriate procedures for elevating indefinite suspension from office to
deposition from office.

**Elevation of Censure to Deposition for Teaching Elders**

With respect to teaching elders, indefinite suspension from office serves the
Church to protect the integrity of the gospel ministry. Since a Presbytery may

<a id="ga52-p1282"></a>
<!-- PAGE ga=52 pdf_page=1284 printed_page=1282 -->

conclude that the continuance of a convicted minister in office would

scandalize or otherwise hurt the Church (or even the minister), it may choose
indefinite suspension, adopting a “wait and see” approach particularly where
the Presbytery sees reasonable prospects for his repentance and restoration to
the ministry.

_BCO_ 37-8, governing the “Removal of Censure” in cases of suspension from
office, mandates that the Presbytery “proceed with great caution,” advancing
through deliberate stages of a gradual restoration, first admitting him to the
Sacraments (if he was suspended from them), and only later giving him “the
privilege of preaching on probation for a time, so as to test the sincerity of his
repentance and prospect of his usefulness.” It continues, “When satisfied in
these respects, the Presbytery shall take steps to restore him to his office,”
noting that “the case shall always be under judicial consideration until the
declaration of restoration has been pronounced. Notably, _BCO_ 37-8 prescribes
no procedural mechanism defining what the phrase “take steps to restore him
to his office” should entail, and there is no mention at all what procedural
“steps” might be involved in _elevating_ his suspension from office to deposition
from office.

Additionally, _BCO_ 30-3 (“Church Censures”) prescribes, “In the case of
indefinite suspension from office imposed due to scandalous conduct, the
procedure outlined in _BCO_ 34-8 (“Special Rules Pertaining to Process Against
a Minister”) shall be followed.” That provision says:

A minister under indefinite suspension from his office or
deposed for scandalous conduct shall not be restored, even on
the deepest sorrow for his sin, until he shall exhibit for a
considerable time such an eminently exemplary, humble and
edifying life and testimony as shall heal the wound made by
his scandal.

It adds:

A deposed minister shall in no case be restored until it shall
appear that the general sentiment of the Church is strongly in
his favor, and demands his restoration; and then only by the
court inflicting the censure, or with that court’s consent. The
removal of deposition requires a three-fourths (3/4) vote of the

<a id="ga52-p1283"></a>
<!-- PAGE ga=52 pdf_page=1285 printed_page=1283 -->

court inflicting the censure, or a three-fourths (3/4) vote of the
court to which the majority of the original court delegates that
authority.

Interestingly, while _BCO_ 34-8 prescribes a procedural mechanism for
restoration to office after deposition from office, it is frustratingly silent about
what steps should be taken to either elevate suspension from office to
deposition or to remove the same.

Clearly, the duration of a minister’s indefinite suspension from office is related
to the elevation of that censure to deposition, for once a minister is indefinitely
suspended, he must either be restored in time or be deposed. There are no other
options, and because the duration of the censure is presently governed by the
various considerations described in _BCO_ 34-8 and _BCO_ 37-8, the particular
facts and circumstances must guide the Presbytery’s judgment about how best
to proceed.

The question of a man’s deposition from the ministry is different in important
ways from excommunication. A minister both suspended from office and the
Sacraments might be deemed unsuitable to be restored to office yet remain a
genuine Christian and therefore be restored to the Sacraments upon
satisfactory evidence of repentance ( _BCO_ 36-5), sometimes well before his
Presbytery finds it appropriate to restore him to the ministry.

Elevating suspension from the Sacraments to excommunication as compared
to elevating suspension from office to deposition is like comparing apples and
oranges. Every Christian has a duty to repent of sin, but Christians have neither
a manifest duty to be eligible for gospel ministry nor to pursue (or be restored
to) the same. excommunication involves a determination that a man has
committed the offense of being “incorrigible and contumacious” ( _BCO_ 30-4),
matters regularly assessed in cases of process, but the determination of those
factors governing whether a man should be restored to the ministry as we find
them in _BCO_ 34-8 and _BCO_ 37-8 are not readily susceptible to adjudication in
a case of process with the appointment o fa prosecutor in the way that the
refusal to repent of sin is, nor are the stakes as high since deposition from office
does not remove a man from the communion of the Church.

The situation of an indefinitely suspended minister, particularly as time
marches on, is analogous to the irregularity we address in cases where a

<a id="ga52-p1284"></a>
<!-- PAGE ga=52 pdf_page=1286 printed_page=1284 -->

minister is “divested” from the ministry because he has been “without call” for
an extended period as governed by _BCO_ 34-10. The key difference is that
divestment “without censure” is expressly _not_ available to an indefinitely
suspended minister because the cause of his ineligibility arises from his having
committed an _offense_ rather than an inculpable “lack of acceptance to the
Church.”

This proposed amendment would adopt the general form of parliamentary
procedure used in divestiture cases as the procedure governing the elevation
of indefinite suspension from office to deposition from office which, while
less formal than a case of process, is both tested and familiar, requiring notice
to the suspended minister and an opportunity for him to be heard as well as a
proposed three-quarters (3/4) majority vote to obtain his deposition from office
rather than the lesser two-thirds (2/3) vote applicable to divestiture cases. This
heightened vote requirement affords parity as it corresponds to the same supermajority vote needed for a man’s _restoration_ to the ministry in such cases as
he has been deposed from office per existing _BCO_ 34-8. The heightened vote
requirement also mitigates whatever protection might be lost to the minister
resulting from the more informal nature of such proceedings. The deposed
minister is also protected by the right of appeal from the deposition, which
would mean that the reviewing court would overturn the ruling if “clear error”
were demonstrated. ( _BCO_ 39-3.3).

**Elevation of Censure to Deposition for Ruling Elders and Deacons**

A corresponding solution is proposed for the elevation of suspension from
office to deposition for ruling elders and deacons. Unlike the steps described
in _BCO_ 34-8 and _BCO_ 37-8, the present Rules of Discipline provide no specific
guidelines governing the restoration to office of ruling elders and deacons after
they have been indefinitely suspended.

The instant proposal would treat ruling elders and deacons the same as it treats
teaching elders, adopting the same standard for restoration from suspension
from office applicable to ministers under _BCO_ 34-8 and subjecting both their
restoration to office and deposition from office to a three-fourths (3/4) vote of
their Session pursuant to a parliamentary procedure subject to appeal.
