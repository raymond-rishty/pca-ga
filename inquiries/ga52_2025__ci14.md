**2. The History of "Good and Regular Standing" –** _**BCO**_ **20-3 and 24-3**

Since their adoption in 1879 ( _BCO_ 6:3:4), the elector conditions “good and
regular standing” (PCA _BCO_ 20-3, 24-3) did not guarantee voting privileges
to all communicants, or disallow local congregations from observing
additional voting rules. A member in “good standing” has a right to the Lord’s
Super in both his home church and neighboring congregations. This voter
condition excludes parties under censure of suspension or strong presumption
of guilt (cf. PCA _BCO_ 33-4; 42-6). “Regular standing” is the status members
enjoy in their home congregation and is indicative of their right to exercise
congregation-specific privileges (e.g. solicitation o fa letter of transfer;
eligibility for office; pursuit of judicial process, etc.) _exercised in according to_
_the rules of that congregation_ . This voter condition excludes non-members,
members with an abnormal relationship to the church (e.g. non-resident,
dismissed, or associate members—cf., PCA _BCO_ 25-3; 38-3, 46-4), and those
who fall short of the voting conditions in local church bylaws and/or applicable
civil laws (cf., _BCO_ 25-11). This means that the voter conditions “good and
regular standing” imply that congregations may observe additional voting
rules. It does not mean that all communicants must be counted as voters.

**3. PCUS Allowance for Minimum Voting Age**

For twelve years prior to the creation of the PCA in 1973, the Constitution of
the Southern Presbyterian Church _expressly allowed_ congregations to confine
voting to those who had reached a minimum age, and who were sufficiently
active in their church (1961 PCUS BCO, 5-1). In the publications of those
conservatives who helped to form the PCA, one finds written support for
minimum voting age, and some of the 259 churches which founded the PCA
entered the new denomination with a minimum voting age provision.

The minimum voting age provision adopted at the PCUS centennial assembly
(1961) reflected a perspective on congregational voting that was more
consistent with its 1861 founding, than the view that had developed in the 20 [th]
century. Leading members of the PCUS had come to embrace the PCUSA
position, holding that voting must be extended to all communicants regardless
of age. [26] However, it is evident that this perspective had not been universally
imbibed. The 1961 minimum voting age provision follows a 1959 overture
testifying to the facts that (a) already voting “practices and procedures vary
greatly between individual churches” and (b) “in some cases, the lack of
wisdom, and efficient and fairly administered procedures, result in the election
of officers unsuitable and unprepared for their responsibilities.” [27] (There
came t ob ea problem, for example, with congregations electing and installing
as Ruling Elders those who had hardly reached young adulthood. [28] ) It is of
little surprise, therefore, that only one Presbytery (Congaree) out of eightyfour objected to the 1961 minimum voting age provision. [29] Conservative

**6. Voting Age Presents No Conflict with** _**BCO**_ **6-4**

Some believe that _BCO_ 6-4 secures for communicants the unqualified right to
exercise all church privileges (including voting). On this view, congregational
bylaws which contain a minimum voting age, and/or a constitutional provision
allowing for congregations to adopt one, would contradict that provision.

_BCO_ 6-4. Those only who have made a profession of faith in
Christ, have been baptized, and admitted by the Session to the
Lord's Table, are entitled to all the rights and privileges of the
church.

Three considerations render untenable the view that all communicants have

unqualified rights to exercise all church privileges, including voting.

First, _BCO_ 6-4 makes communicant membership a necessary, rather than
sufficient condition for the enjoyment of all church privileges. It is true, for
example, that “Those only who are US Presidents are entitled to collect a
presidential pension.” Yet, an intervening condition must be met before a
president may take advantage of that privilege, namely, retirement. Likewise,
communicant membership ina local church must be understood as a necessary

**7. Common Grace and Societal Views**

Civil society regards minors as full _citizens_ but clearly doesn't regard them as
having the requisite discernment to think or act responsibly as an adult. The
age at which someone legally ceases to be a minor in the US varies by state.
It's 18 in most, but it's 19 in AL, CO, MD, and NE, and 21 in IN and MS.
Various government entities set a minimum age for military service,
purchasing prescriptions or alcoholic beverages, and for voting in federal,
state, county and local elections. Ordinarily, a minor can't legally consent to a
contract or open a bank account by himself. In most states, minors can't buy
spray paint, fireworks, knives, dry ice, paintballs, nail polish remover, or a
lottery ticket. Most states can remove a minor from his parent's home and place
him in foster care against his will if the state believes he's in an unsafe
environment. Only one US state allows a minor to choose which parent gets
physical custody in a divorce (Georgia), and even then he must be at least 14
and his choice must be approved by a judge.

Ordinarily, minors can't legally consent to their own medical care. The PCA
recently sent letters to government officials condemning, among other things,
the ability of a minor to make decisions regarding medical measures like
puberty intervention drugs. The letter rightly asserted minors have
_"vulnerabilities [that] can be preyed upon by powerful external forces, [and]_
_they are also susceptible to the internal confusions and instabilities often_
_accompanying childhood."_ The letter rightly observed: _"Children whose minds_
_and personalities are still developing do not yet possess the perspective or_
_maturity to make these decisions."_ [40] But many of those "minds and
personalities" also lack the competence, perspective, and maturity to vote on
whether to incur a church mortgage, petition presbytery to dissolve a minister's
call against his wishes, or to re-affiliate with a less orthodox denomination.

be entitled, regardless of their age, to vote on any motion to establish a
minimum voting age in their congregation. Most congregations probably
require a two-thirds majority to revise bylaws, and in churches without bylaws,
it seems Roberts Rules would require a two-thirds majority to adopt a rule
limiting the right to vote.

Understandably, this change could result in instances where a family transfers
to ap ca church with a different policy than their previous one. We trust
parents could explain to their children that our churches have the freedom to
set a minimum voting age, just like parents explain why some of our churches
celebrate the Lord's Supper with different frequency or with only juice. Similar
differences between congregations are already permissible. It's possible for a
minor to be a communing member of Church A, but when his parents transfer
to Church B, the new Session can decline to examine the minor for admission
to the Lord's Supper, at least per the SJC's interpretation of _BCO_ 57-2 in a case
20 years ago. [42] If a _Session_ has the constitutional freedom to do that _,_ it seems
a congregation should have the freedom t os eta minimum voting age different
than other congregations.

### CCB advice

**N.** Overture 22 from the Pacific Northwest Presbytery: “Amend _BCO_ 20-3,
24-3 and 25-1 to clarify ‘Regular Standing’ re minimum voting age”

In the opinion of the CCB, Overture 22 is **in conflict** with Preliminary
Principle 6 and _BCO_ 6-4, in that PP6 affirms that “the power to elect persons
to the exercise of authority in any particular society resides in that society” and
_BCO_ 6-4 acknowledges that every communicant member is “entitled to all the

<a id="ga52-p255"></a>
<!-- PAGE ga=52 pdf_page=257 printed_page=255 -->

rights and privileges of the church,” while the proposed _BCO_ 25-1 grants
congregations the right to remove a communicant member’s right and
privilege to vote.

CCB notes that adopting this change would create collateral consequences
regarding the quorums specified in _BCO_ 25-2, -3.
