### As referred / posed

##### OVERTURE 38 from the Southeast Alabama Presbytery (to CCB, OC)

“Amend _BCO_ 30-4 to Require a New Case with Process for Elevation
of Indefinite Suspension to Excommunication”

**Be it resolved** that _BCO_ 30-4 be amended as follows (underlining for

additions):

**30-4.** Excommunication is the excision of an offender from

the communion of the Church. This censure is to be inflicted

only on account of gross crime or heresy and when the offender
shows himself incorrigible and contumacious. Elevating
indefinite suspension from the Sacraments to
excommunication shall require a new case of process
establishing that the matter is a gross crime or heresy and that
the offender has shown himself incorrigible and contumacious.
The design of this censure is to operate on the offender as a
means of reclaiming him, to deliver the church from the
scandal of his offense, and to inspire all with fear by the
example of his discipline.

So that _BCO_ 30-4 as amended would read:

**RATIONALE:**

The SJC recently decided _TE Aaron Myers v. Iliana Presbytery_ ( _M51GA_,
2024, pages 824-834) in which it concluded that where there is a previous
finding of guilt and imposition of censure, a censured person is not entitled to
full process _de novo_ for the court to find him “incorrigible and contumacious”
( _BCO_ 30-4). The SJC, invoking the “intrinsic powers” of our courts and
“parliamentary law,” prescribed the following procedure:

the indefinitely suspended person has aright to a hearing in
the matter: he must be charged by the court supervising the
indefinite suspension with being “incorrigible and
contumacious,” he must be presented with the evidence to that
effect, he must be called upon to plead before the court, and he
would have aright to a defense before the original trial court.
The court, upon completing its hearing, would be called upon
to consider a motion to amend a matter previously adopted, to
elevate the indefinite suspension to excommunication. Passage
would require a two thirds majority (2/3), unless previous
notice were given o fan intent to offer a motion to amend a
matter previously adopted, the notice framed in such a way as
to avoid undermining the impartiality of the maker and thereby
disqualifying him from participation in the hearing.

<a id="ga52-p1277"></a>
<!-- PAGE ga=52 pdf_page=1279 printed_page=1277 -->

This procedure is not expressly prescribed anywhere in our Rules of
Discipline.

t hes jc previously treated a decision to elevate censure as a matter o fits
discretion without the necessity of further process in _Dallison v. North Florida_
_Presbytery_, ( _M30GA_ 2002, page 156, 160-161). As in _Myers_, _Dallison_ denied
that the Constitution requires a “new trial for new charges” for elevating
censure but afforded the court “discretion” in such elevations of censure

(subject to the “clear error” standard). But _Dallison_ never mentioned limiting
that discretion by a parliamentary procedure as the SJC only recently held in
_Myers_, evidencing an ongoing lack of certainty and uniformity in the standards
our courts should apply when imposing the Church’s highest censure in
elevation cases.

Contrary to the variable holdings on display in _Myers_ and _Dallison_, before a
member of the Church is excommunicated, he should be afforded a regular
case of process per our Rules of Discipline to guarantee that those conditions
peculiarly pertinent to excommunication have been established in accordance
with the time-tested procedures that Presbyterianism has long recognized as
decent and fair.

Such procedures ensure a meaningful record is made detailing the ground for
the court’s decision and affording an excommunicated person a meaningful
final review of his excommunication. Since the accused’s restoration to the

Sacraments is the intended result of indefinite suspension and
excommunication alike, the real difference between the two censures is
whether the accused has any right of review, a hollow right indeed in the
absence of the protections afforded by a case of process. Importantly, a person
under indefinite suspension from the Sacraments has no right to complain
against a court’s refusal to restore him, however meritorious his case may be,
since he i snot in “good standing.” ( _BCO_ 43-1). Therefore, an appeal from his
excommunication is the censured person’s last and only opportunity to seek
relief from a censuring court that has made an error about whether he is truly
“incorrigible and contumacious.” In deciding to elevate censure to
excommunication it is possible for a court to act irregularly, refuse reasonable
indulgence, receive improper evidence or decline to receive proper evidence,
hurry to a decision, manifest prejudice, make a mistake, or commit injustice in
the censure. ( _BCO_ 42-3). But if the censuring court’s actions are not regulated
by the familiar and well-defined boundaries afforded by a case of process, how

<a id="ga52-p1278"></a>
<!-- PAGE ga=52 pdf_page=1280 printed_page=1278 -->

can the accused obtain, and the higher courts afford him, a meaningful review
of his excommunication? The weightiness of this last opportunity for review
is evident when we understand that once he is excommunicated, he thereafter
lacks any kind of standing either to complain or appeal should the court of
original jurisdiction refuse to restore him, thus instantiating and insulating any
error of the censuring court, perhaps permanently.

This proposed amendment to _BCO_ 30-4 provides that the court, having
reckoned the case unfit for excommunication in the first instance, and having
fulfilled its responsibility to “frequently converse with” the accused “as well
as pray with him and for him, that it would please God to give him repentance”
( _BCO_ 37-2), must thereafter demonstrate that the censure of indefinite
suspension from the Sacraments should be elevated because those peculiar
conditions justifying the different and greater censure of excommunication
(i.e., that the case involves a “gross crime or heresy” and that the offender has
shown himself “incorrigible and contumacious”) have been established before
taking the extraordinary step of “excising him from the communion of the
Church.”

### CCB advice

**Y.** Overture 38 from the Southeast Alabama Presbytery: “Amend _BCO_ 30-4
to Require a New Case with Process for Elevation of Indefinite Suspension to
Excommunication”

In the opinion of the CCB, Overture 38 is **in conflict** with the Constitution in
that _BCO_ 30-4 refers to the infliction of an initial censure of excommunication

after the judgment o fa court and i snot intended to offer a definition of
excommunication per se, therefore this overture is incompatible with those

parts of the _BCO_ which require excommunication for acts not necessarily
categorized as “gross crime or heresy” such as _BCO_ 32-6, 33-2, -3, and 34-4.
