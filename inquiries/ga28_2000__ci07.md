## Majority Rationale of the Committee on Constitutional Business to Overture 2 from Grace Presbytery and Overture 10 From Mississippi Valley Presbytery in this matter of the Directory for Worship of the Book of Church Order

This majority rationale was prepared after the meeting of the committee and with the help of a number of the committee members; but it was not finally discussed, debated and approved in an open committee meeting prior to the meeting of the 28th GA.

### Background:

The Committee acknowledges the unique character of the Directory for Worship (DFW) and understands the difference of perceptions regarding its relationship to the constitutional documents of the church and the church-at-large. Beginning with the 3rd General Assembly, the adoption of the language of the DFW has continued to be a controversial matter. Some of that controversy finds its origins in the various philosophies with respect to the nature of “constitutional authority” and what it means that language may, or may not, have the “force of law” or be “obligatory.” It is beyond the scope of this report to deal with these issues and the Committee simply refers the Assembly to *BCO* 11-1; 11-2; 25-10 and 25-11, among others.

The very nature of proceedings in our first three General Assemblies required procedures of an extraordinary character. They were necessary, however, to get the organization functioning and move ahead with the business of the church (which at times may disregard the details of administration and “minutia”). And, we were, after all, men of good will having in our hearts the desire to serve Christ in complete conformity to His will, as revealed to us in Scripture. There was no intention or desire to confuse or confound the work of the Assembly; in fact, just the opposite was true.

The 3rd GA, then, approved the DFW in the same way that the first two Assemblies had approved the Form of Government (FOG) and the Rules of Discipline (ROD) – that is, acting as a constitutional convention. That procedure was clearly understood to be a suspension of the rules which suspension was necessary to get things started. ## The Majority Conclusion

The majority of the Committee believes that the actions taken by the first three Assemblies demonstrate that the commissioners understood that they were creating a document that came to be entitled the Book of Church Order. It is clear that this Book has three major sections and that in developing it they were working on the creation of one of the constitutional documents. This is further demonstrated by the adoption of the temporary statement, in the first place. Commissioners were not happy with the language of the Directory for a number of reasons.

During the Third GA it became apparent that the process was going to be much slower than anyone anticipated. The first two segments of the BCO were in place but some strategy was needed to break the impasse over the DFW. There was concern that if this language were part of the BCO and therefore the constitutional documents it would be enforceable and they would be obliged to it (in the same sense that any other language of the BCO is enforceable and obligatory), possibly in violation of their consciences. The way to include the DOW in the BCO and constitutional documents came in the form of the temporary statement. Those who did not like the language were satisfied that they would not be held to it when it became part of BCO. The DOW would be included in BCO but not be considered enforceable or obligatory (whatever that means). This compromise worked and the language was approved as part of the BCO. The language would be an approved guide and those who liked it could embrace it.

The minority opinion at this point invokes the definition of the Constitution and hangs onto the final phrase. The Preface to BCO, III defines the constitution as:

“The Constitution of the Presbyterian Church in America, which is subject to and subordinate to the Scriptures of the Old and New Testaments, the inerrant Word Of God, consists of its doctrinal standards set forth in the Westminster Confession of Faith, together with the Larger and Shorter Catechisms, and the

<a id="ga28-p85"></a>
<!-- PAGE ga=28 pdf_page=87 printed_page=85 -->

Book of Church Order, comprising the Form of Government, the Rules of Discipline and the Directory for Worship, *all as adopted by the Church* (Emphasis added)

The minority would agree that, in fact, the DFW was adopted, but it was adopted as an approved guide (taken from the “Temporary Statement” of the Preface of the DFW). This point we do not argue. The 3rd GA did, in fact, adopt the DFW as a temporary guide without the “force of law” and not “obligatory in all its parts.” None-the-less, it adopted it. And, it adopted it right smack in the middle of completing work on the BCO and under the special rules (suspension of the amendment process), which it created just for that task. So, the constitution of the church is comprised of *all* those documents listed in the Preface to BCO, “all as adopted by the Church.” All of the documents that it adopted which are listed in the Preface, by definition, make up the constitutional documents of the PCA. None of those documents are part of the constitution unless and until they are adopted, and no other document may be adopted as part of the constitution without changing this Preface. Granted that the framers of our constitution enacted language and then stripped it of its “enforcement authority” and “obligatory nature,” but such was the necessary compromise needed to complete the crafting of the constitution and finish the work begun by the 1st GA. But these are the items that make up our constitution, all as adopted by the court. Some were adopted without the force of law and obligation, but all are part of the constitution, “all,” as they were “adopted by the Church.”

At this point, the Committee is compelled to remind the Assembly that this report is not dealing with the “relative authority” of the constitutional documents nor the nature of the phases “force of law” and “obligatory in all its parts.” Care must be exercised not to characterize any of this discussion as addressing those concepts.

The fact that the Constitutional Documents Committee was charged to draft the temporary statement, . . .”until a more precise statement can be prepared” indicates that the language was a work in process. Indeed, the objective that the Assembly had in mind was a statement on the “relative authority” of the constitutional documents. Just how authoritative are these documents that we are including into the constitution? Will all portions of the BCO be as binding and authoritative as others – or as binding and authoritative as the WCF or Scripture? These were some of the questions leading up to the charge to the CDC. As a result, the DOW, which was adopted, was understood to be part of BCO but still a work that would undergo continued development by future Assemblies. It was as good as it could be at the moment and it was adopted into BCO as an approved guide but with language that would permit those with reservations to “live with it.” And that work did continue until the Assembly dismantled the SCJB replacing it with the SJC and CCB. There is currently no Assembly committee or agency formally charged with the continuing development of the DFW.

Even if one were to carve the DFW out of the constitution because it is not “enforceable” or “obligatory”, it would be very difficult to see how it is not part of the

<a id="ga28-p86"></a>
<!-- PAGE ga=28 pdf_page=88 printed_page=86 -->

$BCO$. All of the actions from the $1^{\text{st}}$ to the 3rd GA assumed that the DFW was part of the $BCO$. Every reference to these chapters in the records of the General Assemblies is a reference to “$BCO$ XX,” not to “Approved Guide chapter XX.”

The actions of the $8^{\text{th}}$ and $9^{\text{th}}$ GA’s granting three chapters “full” constitutional authority did bring the matter into sharper focus. This action granted a greater measure of accountability from our churches with respect to Chapters 56, 57 &amp; 58 as the Assembly changed the relationship of those chapters to our churches. This action was required by a need for clarity on the validity and obligatory nature of the vows contained in these three chapters. These parts of the $BCO$, indeed, these parts of the constitutional documents, are now invested with the force of law and obligation (in the same way that it is understood for the rest of $BCO$). This is what prompted the Stated Clerk to add the note of explanation to the minutes and that note was not challenged as to its precision. The note reminded the Assembly that the $3^{\text{rd}}$ GA had already adopted the DOW (now the DFW) as part of the $BCO$ making it an integral part of the constitutional documents. The time to clarify the action of the Assembly was at that precise moment. If that was not the intent or understanding of the GA, it should have taken action to say so. At this point, the approved, adopted, accepted, operative and historic language is in place and we must be guided by it unless and until future Assemblies act otherwise.

Further, it was necessary to send this change to the Presbyteries since granting this full authority required a change to the Preface. $BCO$ 27-2 was in full effect and the Assembly had no authority to act as it had formerly during the first three meetings. The three chapters were not being amended to the constitution or $BCO$ since they were already part of it but the new relationship of authority and obligation to the churches (in particular the vows in those chapters) required the churches to adopt the change to the Preface and the status of the language. The promise made earlier by the Assembly to the commissioners and churches was being annulled with respect to these three chapters and the constitutional change required the process of $BCO$ 27-2. Though the language of the three chapters was not being changed (though the process would allow changes if they conformed to the amendment process) the “relative authority” of these chapters to the constitutional documents was changing. These three chapters ceased to be a work in process and their temporary nature was removed. The balance of the language of the DFW retained its status as adopted by the 3rd GA. That point needs to be stressed – the action to change Chapters 56, 57 &amp; 58 did not in any way alter the nature, status or relationship to the Church of any other part of the DFW from its original adoption by the $3^{\text{rd}}$ GA.

The concern has also been raised that the DFW may not be amended under the provisions of $BCO$ 27-2 since it is not actually a part of the $BCO$ or constitutional documents. The actions of a number of Assemblies noted above demonstrate that the DFW is seen as part of the $BCO$ and therefore subject to the procedure of $BCO$ 27-2. There is also some concern that if the GA submits some portion of the DFW to the Presbyteries as part of an amendment procedure that any amendment would automatically be granted “full constitutional authority” in the same way that Chapters

<a id="ga28-p87"></a>
<!-- PAGE ga=28 pdf_page=89 printed_page=87 -->

56, 57 &amp; 58 were. This is not the case. The Presbyteries would have to take the same action as was done between the 8th and 9th Assemblies and vote on precise language to grant “full” constitutional authority. Indeed, the Preface language would also have to be removed or amended before any action to invest parts or all of Chapters 47-55 and 59-63 with “full” constitutional authority. This is an obligation that the PCA assumed back in the 3rd GA. A solemn promise was made to the churches and commissioners that the language of the DFW, though part of the BCO and constitutional documents, would be held as not enforceable or obligatory until more precise language could be developed and adopted by the normal amendment procedure. Great care must be exercised to guard that trust and obligation.

The Committee, therefore, respectfully submits its proposed responses to the 28th General Assembly in the matters of Overture 2 from Grace Presbytery and Overture 10 from Mississippi Valley Presbytery.

## Minority Report to the Answer of CCB to Overture 2 from Grace Presbytery and Overture 10 from Mississippi Valley Presbytery

### In the matter of the Directory for Worship of the Book of Church Order

In the CCB discussion of Overtures 2 and 10, serious questions arose concerning whether or not the Directory for Worship (DFW) chapters 47-55 and 59-63 have ever been adopted as part of our constitutional standards. The unique, even peculiar, nature of the "Temporary statement" which serves as a preface to the DFW has produced a situation, which members of the CCB often spoke of as a quagmire. Some members felt that DFW 47-55 and 59-63 were part of the constitution but unenforceable; other members felt that those paragraphs were somehow quasi-constitutional or partially constitutional; the Stated Clerk referred to them as having the authority of an "in thesi" paper; many members simply concluded that it is unclear or confusing as to whether or not the paragraphs are part of the constitutional standards of the P.C.A.

To help the Bills and Overtures Committee of Commissioners understand why the confusion exists, it helps to see the relevant portions of the BCO together.

## Preface: III The Constitution Defined

The Constitution of the Presbyterian Church in America, which is subject to and subordinate to the Scriptures of the Old and New Testaments, the inerrant Word Of God, consists of its doctrinal standards set forth in the Westminster Confession of Faith, together with the Larger and Shorter Catechisms, and the Book of Church Order, comprising the Form of Government, the Rules of Discipline and the Directory for Worship; all as adopted by the Church.

## Preface to the DFW

Temporary statement adopted by the Third General Assembly to preface the Directory for Worship. The Directory for Worship is an approved guide and should be taken seriously as the mind of the Church agreeable to the Standards. However, it does not

<a id="ga28-p88"></a>
<!-- PAGE ga=28 pdf_page=90 printed_page=88 -->

have the force of law and is not to be considered obligatory in all its parts. BCO 56, 57, and 58 have been given full constitutional authority by the Eleventh General Assembly after being submitted to the Presbyteries and receiving the necessary two-thirds (2/3) approval of the Presbyteries.

While the CCB did finally agree to conclude that these portions of the DFW seem to be constitutional, this minority of one was not persuaded to agree. It is the position of this minority of one that DFW chapters 47-55 and 59-63 are not part of our constitutional standards for the following reasons:

1. In "III. The Constitution Defined", it specifically delineates that the DFW is a component part of our constitution. However, it clearly qualifies this assertion by inserting that very important criterion: "All as adopted by the church!" The critical question for us to consider is not whether or not the church has ever adopted DFW 47-55 and 59-63, because it has -- this was done at our Third General Assembly.

The real question is, what were they adopted as? Were they adopted as part of our constitution? The answer is clearly - no. The "Temporary statement" Preface to the DFW is the umbrella under whose shadow all of the DFW was adopted at the Third Assembly. The "Temporary statement" specifically limits the nature of the adoption, which occurred at that Third Assembly:

- The DFW was adopted only as "an approved guide"

- No paragraph of the DFW was given constitutional authority until the Tenth and Eleventh Assemblies acted to grant such status to BCO 56, 57, and 58. (The status changed was only granted after paragraphs 56, 57, and 58 were subjected to the normal BCO amendment status as prescribed in BCO 26-2.)

2. The "Temporary statement" takes a second step to make it clear that the DFW 47-55 and 59-63 are not part of our constitutional standards by specifically stating "it does not have the force of law." It seems to this minority of one that the very nature of the constitution is that it consists of the standards of our church which do in fact function to have the force of law upon us. If it does not have the force of law upon us, then it can not be a component of our constitutional standards.

3. The "Temporary statement" takes a third step to insure that everybody will understand that DFW 47-55 and 59-63 can not possibly be misunderstood to be a part of our constitutional standards. The "Temporary statement" clearly teaches that this DFW is "agreeable to the Standards". This statement is compelling to this minority of one. Logic absolutely demands that if something is "agreeable to the Standards" then they are not in fact part of the Standards themselves.

4. The "Temporary statement" delineates that the three paragraphs (56-58), which have become part of our constitutional standards did so by going through the BCO 26-2 process. These three paragraphs had no privileged status, which one might expect if somehow they were already part of the constitution. These three paragraphs went through the exact same process that any other non-constitutional language must go through to be adopted as constitutional

<a id="ga28-p89"></a>
<!-- PAGE ga=28 pdf_page=91 printed_page=89 -->

language. These three paragraphs were new amendments to our constitution and had to be adopted as such.

## CCB Minority Report:

### Proposed Answer to Overture 2 from Grace Presbytery

It is the opinion of the minority that the request of Overture 2 (from Grace Presbytery) to rescind the current prefatory statement to the Directory for Worship (DFW) will not create conflicts with other portions of our Constitution.

It is the opinion of the minority, however, that the end effect of rescinding the prefatory statement will be radically different from the effect anticipated by Grace Presbytery. Grace Presbytery is mistaken when she assumes that the effect of rescinding the prefatory statement will be to grant the entire Directory of Worship full constitutional status and weight. Chapters 47-55 and 59-63 have never been adopted as paragraphs with full constitutional status. They have only been adopted as an "approved guide". While they are "agreeable to the Standards", they are not part of the Standards. Certainly, the procedure used to adopt BCO 56-58 as having full constitutional authority would also need to be used to adopt 47-55 and 59-63 as part of our constitutional authority. The procedure used to adopt BCO 56-58 is the normal BCO amendment process prescribed in BCO 26-2; this is a process which allows consideration, deliberation, debate, and amendment upon each chapter, and if need be each paragraph, of the newly proposed language. The real effect of rescinding the prefatory statement and doing nothing else will be to effectively remove all references to 47-55 and 59-63 from the printed pages of our BCO since the umbrella under which they existed in our book has now been rescinded.

It is the opinion of the minority that if Grace Presbytery wishes to accomplish her desired end, then she must make two proposals at the same time:

1. Request General Assembly to rescind the preface "Temporary statement" and
2. Request General Assembly to amend the BCO by adopting the present language of paragraphs 47-55 and 59-63 to become part of our constitution. (Each paragraph, of course, would be subject to the normal debate and amendment processes.)

## CCB Minority Report:

### Proposed Answer to Overture 10 from Mississippi Valley Presbytery

It is the opinion of the minority that there is no constitutional mechanism in place to change the language of DFW 53-6. The process prescribed in BCO 26-2 would not be appropriate to apply to DFW 53-6 unless it was the desire of Mississippi Valley Presbytery to see the General Assembly add 53-6 to the other three DFW paragraphs

<a id="ga28-p90"></a>
<!-- PAGE ga=28 pdf_page=92 printed_page=90 -->

(56, 57, 58) as part of our constitutional standards. This certainly would not be wise to do unless all of the rest of chapter 53 was also included.

Our Stated Clerk suggested at our meeting that these DFW 47-55 and 59-63 paragraphs might well be treated as we would an *in thexi* paper such as the Pastoral Letter on Spiritual Gifts or the Paper on Freemasonry. While there may be little purpose in seeking to change the language of such documents, it might be accomplished by asking the General Assembly to reconsider the document and then to change the language. The *BCO* 26-2 process would not be required -- only by majority vote at one meeting of our General Assembly.
