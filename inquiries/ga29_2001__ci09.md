# 29-28 Item 3 — General Assembly authority to amend or delete an SJC concurring opinion

*The CCB advised that the GA may not amend the SJC's report by deleting a concurring opinion, since under BCO 15-5 an SJC decision is the final decision of the Assembly.*

**Body:** Committee on Constitutional Business (CCB)  ·  **Type:** Constitutional inquiry  ·  **Assembly:** 29th (2001)  ·  **Provisions:** BCO 15-5  ·  **Disposition:** advice given

*Source: [ga29_2001 pp. 144–145](../markdown/ga29_2001.md#ga29-p144)*

## Digest headnote
*Editorial summary from the PCA Digest, Part II (Interpretations of the Constitution) — this is the Digest's wording, not the verbatim minutes. The authoritative text is the verbatim record below / linked above.*

The CCB answered a constitutional inquiry arising from SJC Case 99-1 concerning whether it is constitutionally permissible for the GA to amend the report of the SJC, by advising the Assembly that it may not amend the report of the SJC by deleting a concurring opinion. Numerous grounds were given, including that BCO 15-5 has specifically been framed to assert that an SJC decision is the final decision of the GA.

**Key words:** BCO 15-5

**In the minutes:** [ga29_2001 p.144](../markdown/ga29_2001.md#ga29-p144)

## Verbatim record

3) INQUIRY FROM THE BILLS AND OVERTURES COMMITTEE OF COMMISSIONERS IN RE. TO OVERTURE 35:

“In the John Wood matter (i.e. a matter in which the SJC conducted a *BCO* 31-2 investigation but determined not to file charges), is it constitutionally permissible for the GA to amend the report of the SJC by deleting a concurring opinion?”

**Answer:**

It is the opinion of the CCB that it is not constitutional for the GA to amend the SJC report on Case 99-1 (the John Wood Matter) by deleting any or all-concurring opinions.

**Reasoning:**

1. The SJC manual 20-2 specifically delineates that a concurring and/or dissenting opinion of any member of the SJC is a component part of the SJC report.

2. *BCO* 15-5a specifically delineates that the SJC members have the constitutional right to attach concurring or dissenting opinions to any SJC decision.

3. *BCO* 15-5 has specifically been framed to assert that an SJC decision is the final decision of the GA. Additionally, *BCO* 15-5 has gone to great lengths to place into our constitution safeguards to prevent SJC decisions from being amended on the floor of GA. Unless the provisions of *BCO* 15-5c (one third of SJC submitting a minority report) are brought into play, there is a clear constitutional bar against any complaint, appeal, change, modification, or amendment to an SJC report.

4. While the CCB is aware that the *BCO* 34-1 assumption of original jurisdiction is a constitutionally unique happening, it is the advice of the CCB that the *BCO* 15-5 bar to amendment is still in effect. Our reasoning is as follows:

<a id="ga29-p145"></a>
<!-- PAGE ga=29 pdf_page=147 printed_page=145 -->

a) When the 28th GA referred the “John Wood Matter” to the SJC, it recognized, at least de facto, that the matter constituted a case, since BCO 15-4 seems to allow only cases to be committed to the SJC. As such, all the provisions of BCO 15-5 will apply.

b) BO15-5 is the governing paragraph whenever a “case” is committed to the SJC.

1. Was a case submitted to the SJC? Yes.
2. Did the SJC make a decision? Yes.
3. Do the judges have a constitutional right to file a concurring and/or dissenting opinion? Yes (cf. BCO 15-5a)
4. Can the GA amend the SJC report of Case 99-1 to delete concurring opinions? NO (cf. BCO 15-5a)
