# III.C — Effect of presbytery division on advice and consent voting

*The CCB advised that a newly erected presbytery may give advice and consent on BCO amendments already voted on by its predecessor, but individual presbyters may not vote a second time after transfer.*

**Body:** Committee on Constitutional Business (CCB)  ·  **Type:** Constitutional inquiry  ·  **Assembly:** 47th (2019)  ·  **Provisions:** BCO 8-2.a.1, BCO 26  ·  **Disposition:** Yes to (1), No to (2); Adopted by the CCB

*Source: [ga47_2019 pp. 403–404](../markdown/ga47_2019.md#ga47-p403)*

---

## Verbatim record

**C** . Questions on the Effect of Presbytery Division upon Advice and

Consent
1. If a presbytery gives its “advice and consent” to proposed _BCO_

amendments prior to its division, is the newly erected presbytery
eligible to give its “advice and consent” on the same
amendments? Yes.
2. After reception into a new presbytery, must an individual

presbyter abstain from voting a second time on a proposed _BCO_
amendment? No. _Adopted by the CCB_

Rationale:
1. _BCO_ 26 does not limit advice and consent to Presbyteries in

existence when amendments are proposed. F.P. Ramsay appears

<a id="ga47-p404"></a>
<!-- PAGE ga=47 pdf_page=406 printed_page=404 -->

to be of this opinion: Section 142 “The enacting Assembly may
be any Assembly after a _majority of the Presbyteries existing at_
_the time of the enactment_ have given their advice and consent.”
( _Exposition of the Book of Church Order_, 1898, p. 168). Of
course, the PCA requires a 2/3 majority, and also imposes a time
limit regarding the enacting Assembly.
2. There is no basis in the constitution to exclude the vote of a

presbyter who is a member in good standing of a presbytery at
the time a matter is voted on.
