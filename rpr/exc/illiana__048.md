**Response [2019]:** Illiana recognizes the error and will strive to
accurately and fully document identified differences in the future.
The presbytery found that TE’s [name omitted] stated differences,
included here in his own words, were not exceptions as his view on
recreation on the Sabbath is commonly held, his view on creation
conforms to the analogical day position accepted by the PCA
General Assembly, and neither of these views were found to be
hostile to the system nor striking at the vitals of religion.

The only exception I take to the Standards is in WCF
21.8 regarding the prohibition on "recreations," also found
in WLC 117 and WSC 60 and 61. My rationale for this is
twofold.

First, to thebes t of my knowledge, the only proof text
that addresses this directly is Isa 58:13, which speaks of
"going your own ways, or seeking your own pleasure" on
the Sabbath. While at first blush this appears to prohibit
recreation, in my opinion, Isa 58:3 helps to interpret what
the prophet means by "pleasure": "Behold, in the day of
your fast you seek your own pleasure, and oppress all your
workers." In other words, "pleasure" here refers not to
common and lawful recreations, but injustice.
Second, the consistent and repeated Sabbath
prohibitions throughout Scripture are related to work and
commercial activity (e.g., Ex 20:10; Jer 17:19ff.; Neh 13).
Therefore, I believe this is the thrust of what we are resting
from, in order that we may worship the Triune God on the
Lord's Day. In short, so long as we prioritize public and
private worship, as well as acts of necessity and mercy, on
the Lord's Day -- which will necessarily consume the
majority of the day -- I do not believe it is sinful to engage
in limited amounts of non-commercial recreation.

I hold to an analogical view of the creation days. In
short, this means that I see the thrust of the creation account
being God's pattern-setting for His creation -- six days of
work, plus one day of rest (Ex 20:9-11). There is a certain
similarity between man being made in God's image, and
man's days being made in the likeness of God's days. And
just as God went out to work and then rest, so do we -- in a
similar way, but not in an identical way. Regarding the
length of the days, I am persuaded by the analogical view's
appreciation for their unspecified length. After all, the

Hebrew "yom" can take on many meanings and does not
force a 24-hour view. Likewise, other passages speak to the
mystery of how God relates to time (e.g., Job 10:5; Ps 90:4;
2 Pet 3:8).
In terms of my understanding of the Confession's use
of the clause "in the space of six days," I see this primarily
as a reaction against an instantaneous creation view (e.g.,
Augustine). While the private writings of several divines
indicate they held to ordinary days, their omission of 24hour language calls into question whether they intended to
bind men on this point, especially within a consensus
document. Moreover, the fact that there is but one allusion
to the length of the days suggests that this topic was not of
first importance, as creation ex nihilo and Adam's federal
headship were.
