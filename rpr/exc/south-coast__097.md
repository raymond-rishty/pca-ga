**Jan 25, 2022- TE [Name Omitted]-**
_WCF_ 21.2 _WCF_ 21.8, _WLC_ 117, _WSC_ 60- I do not
believe that Scripture requires. Sabbath rest from “words and
thoughts about their worldly employments and recreations.” I
believe that God made the Sabbath for man (Mk. 2:27) to
teach us to consecrate our earthly work, vocations, and
enjoyments to him (1 Cor. 10:31). Scripture seems to teach
that rest is the ultimate end of the Sabbath and that man, being
made in the image of God, is to remember our salvation in
him and to enjoy the benefits of our redemption in him
through acts of mercy, fellowship with the body, and
enjoyment of his creation on the Lord’s day.
_WLC_ 109- In forbidding the “making any
representation of God, of all or any of the three persons, either
inwardly in our mind, or outwardly in any kind of image or
likeness of any creature whatsoever,” i believe the l cis
taking the second commandment too literally, overlooking the
intent of the command, which is to love and fear God above
all else. In our reading of Scripture, the prologue of John,
Colossians 1, or Revelation 19, for example, it seems

impossible not to imagine Christ in our mind. I believe that
the 2nd commandment teaches us not to wrongly ascribe
worship to something that is not God, or in the case of the
Golden calf in Ex. 32-34, to wrongly ascribe worship to a
graven image, even if that image is attempting to capture the
triune God’s nature. Also, the confession would seem to
forbid the making of any symbol ascribed to God worthy of
worship, such as a cross. I do not believe this is the intent of
the 2nd commandment. That being said, i do believe it is
wrong to attempt to contain the first and third members of the
Trinity with imagery since they are invisible in substance.
_WCF_ 21.5- I take singing of psalms to mean the
hymnody of the Psalter and non-Scriptural spiritual songs and
hymns as the Apostle Paul means in Eph. 5:19. CHAR (10)
_WCF_ 24.6- I believe there may be otherwise and apt reasons
that align with the teachings of Scripture for a marriage to end.
In the cases of abuse, i believe this to be a violation of the 1st,
5th, and 6th commandments. Particularly, in the case of
repeated abuse, it may be un-biblical for the marriage to
continue, especially after church discipline has been carried
out and the abuser remains un-repentant. In this case, the
abusing spouse has neglected their duty to their family and
according to Eph. 5-6 and violated their marriage covenant. In
this case, I believe divorce is permissible. There may also be
cases where trust is broken repeatedly by violating the 8th,
9th, and 10th commandments within the marriage and family
that the same principles above are necessary. It also seems
appropriate to note that if one spouse apostatizes from the
faith, divorce may be permissible according to 1 Cor. 7. In
these cases, divorce may be advised to a member by the
session or presbytery.
**April 26, 2022- TE [Name Omitted]-**
It is with gratitude and conviction that I affirm my
subscription to the Westminster Confession of Faith and
Catechisms as containing the system of doctrine taught in the
Holy Scriptures.
In the interest of full candor, I make you aware of the
following:

1. _WCF_ 21.8 insists that that one’s actions on the

Sabbath “are taken up, the whole time, in the public and
private exercises of his worship.” It seems the Standards say
more about worship and duties and less about rest than do the
Scriptures. Public and private worship must be maintained on
the Lord’s Day as well as other duties of religion, yet to do
them “all the day” and “the whole time” would seem in
tension with the emphasis Scripture places on rest. While
avoiding the idolatry of our commercial culture, which never
pauses for rest or reflection, I believe restorative and recreative activities are not forbidden on the Lord’s Day and can
be understood as fulfilling the requirement of “rest.”
Furthermore, with the finished work of Jesus Christ manifest
in the resurrection from the dead, the New Covenant
community gathers for worship on the first day of the week
now known as “the Lord’s Day” (Rev 1:10). Though not in
tension with the Old Testament Sabbath, the New Covenant
terminology seems more appropriate.
2. _WLC_ 109 forbids the “making any representation
of God...either inwardly in our mind, or outwardly....” Images
of the Triune God which are intentionally or unintentionally
produced or kept as a means of aiding worship (especially in
the sanctuary where such worship is ordinarily conducted) are
disallowed. The second commandment forbids fashioning any
image which believers might bow down to or offer any form
or part of worship. At the same time, in forbidding all mental
representations of God in all contexts and for all purposes,
seems to require what is impossible.
3. I hold that God has chosen to relate with his people
through one overarching covenant of grace, which remains
constant throughout history and that, as the people of God,
Israel and the Church share in the same promises. At the same
time, I hold that on the last day, at the one return of Jesus
Christ, both those found alive along with the redeemed dead
will be raised to life, yet at a later time the unredeemed dead
with be raised for judgment. Because of statements in
Revelation 20:4-5, I understand that _WLC_ 87-88, which
speaks of the “general resurrection of the dead, both of the just
and the unjust,” may be temporally separate events. Though

much of the language in Revelation is symbolic, the
description of two resurrections in Revelation 20 lead met oa
“historic premillennial” position. Citation J. Gresham
Machen (“Premillennialism,” The Presbyterian Guardian 3/2

[24 October 1936], 45) argues:
“Subscription to the Westminster Standards in the
Presbyterian Church of America [e.g., OPC] is not to every
word in those Standards, but only to the system of doctrine
which the Standards contain. The real question, then, is
whether a person who holds the Premillennial view can hold
that system. Can a person who holds the Premillennial view
be a true Calvinist; can he, in other words, hold truly to the
Calvinistic or Reformed system of doctrine which is set forth
in the Westminster Standards? We think that he can; and for
that reason we think that Premillennialists as well as those

who hold the opposing view may become ministers or elders
or deacons in The Presbyterian Church of America. We think
that a man who holds that the return of Christ and the final

judgment take place not in one act, as the Westminster
Standards contemplate them as doing, but in two acts with a
thousand-year reign of Christ upon the earth in between, yet
may honestly say that he holds the system of doctrine that the
Standards contain.”
**Sept 27, 2022- TE [Name Omitted]-**
I take one exception to the Westminster Confession
of Faith on the issue of recreation on the Sabbath. _WCF_

Section 21.8 reads:

This Sabbath is then kept holy unto the Lord, when men, after
a due preparing of their hearts, and ordering of their common
affairs beforehand, do not only observe an holy rest all the day
from their own works, words, and thoughts, about their
worldly employments and **recreations; but also are taken**
**up the whole time in the public and private exercises of his**
**worship, and in the duties of necessity and mercy.**
I believe that the intent of the Scriptures regarding the
Sabbath is not to prevent recreational bodily activity that
might further our rest on Sabbath and enjoyment of the Lord.
The Confession grounds its reasoning for disallowing
recreation on a misreading of the words “doing your pleasure”

found in Isaiah 58:13. “Doing your pleasure” in the context of
Isaiah is referencing keeping one’s business open or not
letting their fields rest. It has to do with commercial activity
and not recreational activity. Thus, while our time in public
and private worship ought to orient the way we spend our time
on the Sabbath, I see nothing in the Scriptures which would
prevent a christian from lawfully taking a walk, hike, or
engaging in any other refreshing recreational activity,
especially when in the company of fellow saints.
