## Response found satisfactory — 52nd General Assembly (2025)
*[ga52_2025 p.686](../../markdown/ga52_2025.md#ga52-p686)*

**2023-01: May 31, 2022** ( _BCO_ 21-4.f) — Presbytery judged a
stated difference to be “merely semantic,” and that decision
needs clarification in light of the candidate’s own words as
recorded in the minutes.

**Response:** This is the exception to which the General
Assembly exception has been taken: “It is my view that the
Second Commandment’s prohibition against images is always
in the context of worship. Therefore, I believe that images in
a worship context (e.g. stained glass windows or icons
depicting Jesus in a church sanctuary) are a violation of the
Second Commandment. But I do not believe that images of
Jesus used in a pedagogical context (e.g. The Jesus Storybook
Bible or Sunday School curriculum for children) are a
violation of the Second Commandment. I also believe _WLC_

109’s prohibition against images of Christ “in our mind” goes
further than what Scripture requires and seems to me to be
unrealistic in practice as we read the Gospel accounts in the
New Testament. I believe that to worship any images of the
Triune God (whether external or mental) violates the Second
Commandment.” (Bold emphasis stated clerk’s). Pittsburgh
Presbytery had ruled this was merely semantic. In discussing
the highlighted sentence, presbyters agreed that it does seem
to raise the exception beyond “merely semantic” to the level
of “more than semantic, but not out of accord with any
fundamental of our system of doctrine.” It was m/s/c to adopt
the following response by Pittsburgh Presbytery: Upon further
reflection, the presbytery does agree that the words “goes
further than what the Scripture requires” does move the
exception to the level of “more than semantic, but not out of
accord with any fundamental of our system of doctrine,” and
the minutes have been notated accordingly.
