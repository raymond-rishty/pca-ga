## Raised — 51st General Assembly (2024)
*[ga51_2024 p.644](../../markdown/ga51_2024.md#ga51-p644)*

**2024-4: Jan 27, 2023** ( _BCO_ 21-4.g) — Presbytery granted a doctrinal
exception that appears to be out of accord with the fundamentals of
our doctrinal standards. This statement seems to say we can make
“images” that picture the incarnate second person of the Trinity, even
“placing them in a worship space,” so long as they are not made an
object of worship.

## Response found satisfactory — 52nd General Assembly (2025)
*[ga52_2025 p.709](../../markdown/ga52_2025.md#ga52-p709)*

**2024-04: Jan 27, 2023** ( _BCO_ 21-4.g) — Presbytery granted a
doctrinal exception that appears to be out of accord with the
fundamentals of our doctrinal standards. This statement seems

to say we can make “images” that picture the incarnate second
person of the Trinity, even “placing them in a worship space,”
so long as they are not made an object of worship.

**Response:** At a prior stated meeting of the Presbytery
(September 22, 2022), the transfer applicant submitted the
following stated difference with _WLC_ 109, which the
Presbytery ruled as “not out of accord with any fundamental
of our system of doctrine” [per _RAO_ 16-3.e.5.c]
_WLC_ 109 – I do not believe the making of any
representation of the Son of God is prohibited by Scripture as
the Divines state. Specifically, I do not believe Scripture
prohibits representations of Jesus for educational or artistic
purposes. The incarnation raises questions about the
representation of the Son of God which cannot adequately be
answered simply by an appeal to passages like Deuteronomy
4:15-19 (since in the case of Jesus the point would be
precisely that God did appear in the “likeness of sinful flesh,”

to quote Paul). Moreover, the second commandment has
reference to the narrow issue of worshipping by means of any
image of any created thing, not simply the making of images
per se. On several occasions God actually commands the
making of images, often to be placed in the very space in
which worship occurs (Ex 25:18, 31-33; 28:33; 1 Kgs 7:29; 2
Chr 4:3-4). Thus, the prohibition in view is not the making of
images, or even placing them in a worship space, but more
narrowly the use of any image as the object of worship.
The applicant submitted his exception, as pertaining
only to the prohibition of the making images of created things,
citing several instances in the Scriptures where images of
created things were placed in the temple. Since then, he, now
as a member of this Presbytery, acknowledged the poor
wording in his original stated difference that led to confusion
and clarified that his stated difference does not extend to the

image of Jesus being “placed in a worship space”.
He submitted the following clarification:
“I don’t have any objection to images of CREATED
things in the worship space for decorative purposes or to
create a worship aesthetic as was done in both tabernacle and
temple, and most church buildings I would think. I think RPR
misunderstood me to be saying that I thought it was therefore
okay to put an image of JESUS in the worship space. I actually
don't think that’s wise as it could encourage the use of an
image of Jesus as an object of worship or veneration, and it
distracts from the central place of the Word in worship ... I
don’t believe images of Jesus should be placed in the worship
space, and that my stated difference had to do merely with
representations of Jesus for artistic or educational purposes.”
At the 132nd stated meeting on January 24th, 2025,
the Presbytery judged this to be more than semantic, but not
out of accord with any fundamental of our system of doctrine.
