## Raised — 51st General Assembly (2024)
*[ga51_2024 p.520](../../markdown/ga51_2024.md#ga51-p520)*

**2024-2: Apr 28-29, 2023** ( _BCO_ 21-4.g) — Presbytery granted a
doctrinal exception that appears to be out of accord with the
fundamentals of our doctrinal standards. The examinee’s stated

different was: "“2. _WLC_ Q. 109. What are the sins forbidden in the
second commandment? A. The sins forbidden in the second

commandment are, all devising, counselling, commanding, using, and
anywise approving, any religious worship not instituted by God
himself; tolerating a false religion; the making any representation of
God, of all or of any of the three persons, either inwardly in our mind,
or outwardly in any kind of image or likeness of any creature
whatsoever... My exception here is in regards to the phrase “of any of
the three persons...” With reference to Jesus Christ, no image should
be made depicting His divinity. However He has appeared in the flesh,
presenting to us His image. To image the humanity of Christ I do not
believe is forbidden in the second commandment. Thus by application,
I have no problem with the making of films depicting the life of Christ.
God has imaged Himself in the Son. Secondly, the phrase “or
outwardly any kind of image or likeness of any creature whatsoever...”
I take to be specifically prohibited for the purpose of worship. To

disconnect it from that qualifier makes the commandment itself reduce
to absurdity because it would prohibit all paintings, art, photography
or anything else that represents any creature of God’s creation.”
As it stands, this statement seems to say we can make “images” that
picture the incarnate second person of the Trinity as opposed to merely
representing his humanity. If that is the position it would seem to strike
at the vitals of religion.

**Response:** RPR's objection to the stated exception is
unclear. We discern RPR is troubled by TE [Name Omitted]'s
statement that "no image should be made depicting [Jesus's]
divinity ... [but] [t]o image the humanity of Christ I do not
believe is forbidden in the second commandment … [because]
God has imaged himself in the Son." Upon review and further

consideration, Heartland Presbytery is convinced that this
articulation o fan exception to _WLC_ 109 does in fact strike at
the vitals of religion. If this was RPR's intended objection, we
agree with it. To articulate the exception in this way without
further clarification would deny the hypostatic union of the
two natures of Jesus Christ (fully God and fully man) united
inseparably in the one true person of Jesus, the incarnate Son
of God and second person of the Trinity.
For a stated exception to _WLC_ 109 not to strike at the
vitals of religion it must acknowledge that imaging the second
person of the Trinity can only imperfectly and incompletely
reflect the two natures of Jesus united in his one person. And
such an image can never purport to reflect only one of those
natures (fully man) lest it become a false Christ and fall into
the heresy of Nestorianism and a denial of the hypostatic
union.

In order to provide clarity on this issue we required
TE [Name Omitted] to engage with RPR’s concern, as best as
we understand it, and appear before Presbytery to state his
difference to _WLC_ 109, if any, as he currently holds it. His
stated different is:

First, let me clearly state that I firmly believe, contra
both Nestorianism and Eutychianism, the historic orthodox
view that Christ is one person in two natures (since the
incarnation), and remains so forever (inseparably and
indivisibly united). I agree with Calvin who states, “…we
affirm his divinity so joined and united with his humanity that
each retains its distinctive nature unimpaired, and yet these
two natures constitute one Christ.” [Calvin, Institutes,
2.14.1.]. Thus, with Calvin I affirm that though there is no
division in the unity of the person of Christ in regards to His
person, there is “distinction.” (For example, consider the
historic teaching of the “communicatio idiomatum”
(communication of properties).
Second, I wish to acknowledge that my statements
made in my transfer exam could have been clearer. i in noway
believe it is possible to make a representation of Christ that
truly images the fullness of the person of Christ. Similarly, I
in no way mean imply that we can or should ever make images

to somehow even capture the full human nature of Christ.
What I meant to refer to in my original comment on my
exception is the human physical body of Christ. Thus, I wrote,
“My exception here is in regards to the phrase ‘of any of the
three persons...’ With reference to Jesus Christ, no image
should be made depicting His divinity. However, He has
appeared in the flesh, presenting to us His image. To image
the humanity of Christ I do not believe is forbidden in the
second commandment.” (emphasis added). My last sentence
is unclear as I use both the terms “flesh” and “humanity” in a
way that may be understood to equate them. I should have
said, “He has appeared in the flesh, presenting to us His
image. To image Christ in the flesh I do not believe is
forbidden in the second commandment.”

i unpack i ta bit more hereby way of analogy. Though
i ti san imperfect analogy (since there is no perfect analogy of
the hypostatic union), all humans are comprised of body and
soul, and no picture of a person (drawing, photograph, stick
figure, etc.) can possibly capture their human nature; it at best
can capture imperfectly that which is visible (i.e. their
materially visible “tent”). Similarly, we cannot and should not
make images of the human or divine natures of Christ.
However, I do not believe that it is sinful to make or imagine
in our minds images of Christ dwelling in the flesh on earth
during his earthly ministry. I do not believe this position in
any way destroys or divides the unity of the one Person of
Christ in two natures.

Third, _WLC_ 109 forbids, “making any representation
of God, of all or of any of the three persons, either inwardly
in our mind, or outwardly in any kind of image or likeness of
any creature whatsoever…” The key concept that I take
exception to here is the prohibition to making any kind of
image “inwardly in our mind.” Consider our reading any of
the many descriptions of Christ in the gospels. How can we
not picture those events in our minds? How do we not picture
the hand of Christ scooping up clay to make salve for the blind
man’s eyes? How do we not picture the nails and sword
piercing Christ’s hands and side? How do we not picture
Christ walking with the men on the road to Emmaus or on the

water with Peter? On the contrary, I believe we are meant to
picture those events (as deficient as our images may be).
Therefore, though we are not to make images of God,
for teaching purposes I believe it is not sinful to make images
of Jesus’ physical form (flesh). i do not believe that tod o so
somehow divides the indivisible union of the two natures in

Christ in one person.
Heartland Presbytery finds this stated difference to be
more than semantic, but not out of accord with any
fundamental of our system of doctrine ( _RAO_ 16-3.e.6.c).
