## Response found satisfactory — 47th General Assembly (2019)
*[ga47_2019 p.491](../../markdown/ga47_2019.md#ga47-p491)*

**Exception: Jul 9, 2016** ( _BCO_ 21-4) – No record of stated differences
or how the Presbytery judged them.

**Response:** Presbytery agrees with this exception and pledges to
properly record candidate’s differences and Presbytery’s judgment
in the future. The candidate’s differences in his own words, which
were approved as more than semantic but not out of accord with any
fundamental of the system of doctrine, will be recorded in the
minutes and were as follows.

_**WCF-**_ **Ch 21.8.** Recreation on the Sabbath: I hold to the

WCF view of the Sabbath as a whole, but the scriptural
proofs the confession uses aren’t precise when it comes
to recreation. I believe that Christ teaches plainly that

it is lawful to do good on the Sabbath (Mt. 12:12).
Primarily this is about works of necessity, but I believe
this can also apply to family time or other similar godly
endeavors such as recreational time with friends. I
don’t believe it is sinful to toss a ball in the back yard
with your family, or togo on a walk with your wife on
the Sabbath, or something similar. Loving people well
is a high calling, and is permitted to do on the Sabbath,
an dido not consider that to be a form of work.

However, I would not encourage families or persons to
abandon worship to attend sporting events, neglect
worship for travel sports, or anything similar. Isaiah
58:18 indicates “pleasure” as breaking of Sabbath
commands, but that pleasure in context regards
conducting business activities, not recreation.
**LC 109.** With respect to images, i do not believe it is a
violation of the 2nd Commandment to use images
depicting Jesus’ humanity. The Bible is clear in its
instruction not to create an idol to worship; I do not
believe that the text prohibits the use of images for
pedagogical uses. We should be careful that we
distinguish teaching versus worship. The two are not
equal. It is possible to use teaching aids, and they not
be worshiped in place of the Lord, one example being
teaching aids for children in Sunday School.
Additionally, I agree with David VanDrumnen’s
conclusion concerning the intent of the Westminster
divines that, “If indeed the Westminster divines were
of a mind to omit pictures of Christ’s humanity from
the proscriptions in Larger Catechism 109, they would
surely have needed to have stated this explicitly”
(David VanDrunen, “Pictures of Jesus and the
Sovereignty of Divine Revelation: Recent literature
and a Defense of the Confessional Reformed View,”
The Confessional Presbyterian 5 [2009] 214-227).
Because of these noted reasons, I believe that the use
of depictions of the humanity of Jesus for teaching
purposes is allowed. If these images ae used, then it
should be stated that the images are only for teaching
and not an accurate portrayal of Jesus for worship.
