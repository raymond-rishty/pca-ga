## Raised — 52nd General Assembly (2025)
*[ga52_2025 p.655](../../markdown/ga52_2025.md#ga52-p655)*

**2025-02: May 7, 2024** ( _BCO_ 21-4.f; _BCO_ 57-1; 57-2) —
Presbytery granted a doctrinal exception that appears to be
hostile to the system of doctrine or strike at the vitals of
religion. In the candidates own words, he says, “As regards
who may partake of the Lord’s Supper, I differ with the idea
that the New Testament requires covenant children to make a
profession of faith (an idea not explicitly stated in the
Standards) or mature to the point of self-examination prior to
being admitted to the Table. I believe covenant children
should be welcomed to the Table of the Lord more or less

when they join the table in their homes. Three biblical
arguments persuade me of this view: the OT pattern of
Passover and other sacrificial feasts, the dubious
universalizing of 1 Corinthians 11:28, and the unity of
signification between the Sacraments.1
First, it is clear that children partook, and were
commanded to partake, of the Passover meal and other
sacrificial feasts in the OT (Exod 12:24-27; Deut 12:11-12;
16:10-11), which are forerunners to the Lord’s Supper much
as circumcision is to baptism. The unity of the Covenant of
Grace is a hallmark of Reformed thought. The Confession
says that “The sacraments of the old testament, in regard of
the spiritual things thereby signified and exhibited, were, for

substance, the same with those of the new” ( _WCF_ 28.5).
Given these points, there ought to be a presumption of
continuity as to the recipients of the sacraments of
nourishment (holy feasts/Lord’s Supper) just as there is for
the sacraments of initiation (circumcision/baptism).
Second, the chief warrant for seeing discontinuity at
this point is Paul’s injunction in 1 Corinthians 11:28: “Let a
person examine himself, then, and so eat of the bread and
drink of the cup.” It is argued that in this verse Paul issues a
universal prerequisite for all worthy partaking in the Lord’s
Supper. However, it is dubious whether this verse can be used
in this way. The NT letters are occasional documents (1
Corinthians especially so), responding to concrete
circumstances in first-century churches. Paul is clear in what
he is addressing—exclusionary, factious, and elitist behavior
toward fellow church members while ostensibly celebrating
the Lord’s Supper (1 Cor 11:18-20). Schismatic behavior that
tears at the unity of the body profanes the unity of the bread
and the participation in the body of Christ that partaking in the
bread signifies (1 Cor 10:14-17). Lots of legitimate
application can be made along these lines to the Church today.
But to universalize the application of this passage is to take it
too far. To deduce from it that Paul intended to set a minimum

maturity threshold for participation in the Supper is, in my
view, to stretch it beyond the breaking point. Moreover, even
if Paul’s injunction to self-examination is taken as broadly as
possible, the fact remains that there is a universal injunction
to repent and believe prior to baptism for adults which
(contrary to Baptists) we do not require of covenant children.
Third, Scripture teaches and our Standards affirm
that baptism and the Lord’s Supper fundamentally agree in
their significance: “the spiritual part of both is Christ and his
benefits; both are seals of the same covenant” ( _WLC_ 176).
Oddly to my mind, Bavinck, and the Majority Report of the
PCA on this subject with him, use this reality to assert the
insignificance of withholding the Supper from young
children. The children have baptism, the reasoning goes, so
it’s not a big deal if they don’t get the Supper yet. But to my
mind this point cuts precisely the other way. Since by baptism

they already bear Christ’s name, how can they be justly denied
his bread? By what token are they granted the one-time sign
of union with Christ while being denied the recurring sign of
union with Christ? The unity of signification between the
Sacraments compels me to think that the reasons for which we
baptize our covenant children hold for the question of whether
or not to commune our children as well.”
