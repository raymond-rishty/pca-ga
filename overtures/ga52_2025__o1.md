This dissent takes an opposing view and would state that not only did the Presbytery err but _clearly erred_ so as to require an annulment of the Presbytery’s dismissal on that issue. This case followed from actions taken by Presbytery at its meeting on July 22, 2022. At that time it received an Investigative Committee report from a _BCO_ 31-2 committee established to investigate reports affecting the Christian character of TE David Krueger. The Investigative Committee unanimously found there to be a strong presumption of guilt and recommended that the Presbytery institute process against said teaching elder. A motion to institute process was then adopted by the Presbytery and a prosecutor was appointed to prepare an indictment and conduct the case. The hearing of the case was assigned to a nine-member Judicial Commission (JC). Thereafter, the JC did not try the case but instead ended the judicial process as it submitted its July 9, 2024 “Decision on Trial” to the Presbytery. In that decision the JC stated: While the indictments and investigative process appeared to be in order and the considerations were of serious nature, there where ( _sic_ ) procedural considerations that require the Judicial Commission to dismiss the charges. The JC in that decision cited more specific reasoning for dismissal by referring to _BCO_ 32-20. The JC reasoning indicated that it would be “seven years between the event and the trial” and that during that time the church in which the matters are alleged to have occurred had moved out of the Reformed Church in America into the Presbyterian Church in America which led to “subsequent changes in how the pastor and elders engage shepherding.” The decision to dismiss Indictment 1 was approved by the JC by a vote of 4-3 on July 2, 2024, 10 days after a lengthy preliminary hearing held on June 22, 2024. Argument at that hearing never suggested _BCO_ 32-20 was being considered so as to justify a dismissal. Furthermore, argument and “evidence” presented at that hearing mitigate against an application of _BCO_ 32-20. More pointedly, the transcript of the hearing of June 22, 2024 reveals that other “procedural considerations” were in play which led to the JC improperly receiving evidence from the Accused while effectively declining to receive evidence from the Prosecution. Accordingly, the JC clearly erred in entering a decision to dismiss. It is helpful in seeing this clear error by first reviewing some of the facts contained in the Record of the Case. The Accused entered written pleas and oral pleas of not guilty to both Indictment 1 and Indictment 2. The oral pleas were also entered at a video hearing held on March 9, 2024. At that hearing, the JC allotted some time for the Accused to speak beyond his plea, and among other matters of which he spoke were his complaints about the process which led to the charges and how the matters of which he was accused could have been “hashed out” if people would have been willing to sit down “with a mediator.” Although the pleas offered at that hearing were accepted, the JC did not take the normal next step of scheduling a trial and citing parties or witnesses to appear ( _BCO_ 32-3), but instead, held two more preliminary hearings, which the Prosecutor, the Accused, and representatives of the Accused were invited to attend. At the first of those subsequent hearings held May 11, 2024, the hearing was called to order by the chairman of the JC who then went on to the identify the purpose for the hearing. He stated, “The business before us today is to set expectations” and “to hear preliminary motions.” During this hearing the emphasis of the Defense was to ask for time for the Accused and his representatives and the Prosecutor and his accusing witnesses to mediate with a goal of reaching restoration and reconciliation. After deliberation following that May 11, 2024 hearing, the JC granted 3 motions, including a motion which “highly recommend[ed]” that the Accused and his accusing witness use a third-party mediator with the hope of reaching reconciliation. Such an effort to pause a judicial process so as to possibly reach a mediated amicable result is noble. Yet, the proposed mediation did not occur, as the accusing witnesses “declined the invitation to mediation.” This disinclination to mediate was reported by the Prosecutor at the next, and last, preliminary hearing held on June 22, 2024. After reporting the position of the accusing witnesses, the Prosecutor stated that it was “the conviction of my heart that we should proceed to trial.” The prosecutor serves as “the representative of the Church, and as such has all its rights in the case.” ( _BCO_ 31-3) His conviction to proceed to trial after the finding of a strong presumption is one that should generally be honored.
