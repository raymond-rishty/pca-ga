D. Overture 11, p. 32 E. Communication 1, p. 28 F. Resolution from TE John Ragland and REG . O. Runnells, p. 51.. II. Statement of Issues Discussed A. There were several overtures dealing with thematter of abortion— Overtures 2, 7, 12, 27, pgs. 32 and 33. Inasmuch as allfour overtures were in substantial accord, Number 12 was chosen to be considered first as representative of the four. B. Overture 11, p. 32 regarding the consumption of alcoholic beverages and the resolution to declare “ in thesi” that total abstinence from alcoholic beverages, except in medical use, be the approved position of our denomination in this matter was discussed. The discussion by the members of the Committee was representative of the various views and positions across our denomination. The discussion also pointed out the difficult nature of resolving this issue within our denomination to the satisfaction of everyone. Recommendations 1. That Overture 32 be answered by referring the matter back to Grace Presbytery, directing them to initiate a study of the matter regarding the proper recipients of Christian baptism resulting in advice to particular churches with problems regarding this matter and should there be a need, overture the General Assembly to amend the statements of our constitutional documents. Adopted. 2. That Overture 12 be answered in the affirmative with the following amend ments: a. That an editorial change be made under Section 4, line 3, by placing a period after “ death” and that the following sentence begin with the words "In such a case, the premature rem oval. b. On the third line of the overture in the Handbook, delete the words "Evangel Presbytery overtures the Eighth General Assembly to” and in their place add “ that the Eighth General Assembly clarifies the action of the Seventh General Assembly and” . The Overture as amended is as follows: "Overture 12: From Evangel Presbytery WHEREAS, the General Assembly, as a court of jesus Christ, should speak with a united voice in affirming the sanctity of human life under the protection of the Sixth Commandment. THEREFORE BE IT

RESOLVED, that the Eighth General Assembly clarifies the action of the Seventh General Assembly and reaffirms the statement of the Sixth General Assembly on abortion: ‘ ‘That because Scripture clearly affirms the sanctity of human life and condemns its arbitrary destruction, we affirm that the intentional killing of an unborn child between conception and birth, for any reason at any time, is clearly a violation of the Sixth Commandment. ’ ’ Added to this statement are the following affirmations: "Concerning crisis pregnancies in which it appears that the premature removal of the unborn child from the mother is a necessary conse quence of medical procedures essential to preserving the mother’s life, we affirm that: 1. The Sixth Commandment not only forbids the taking of innocent life for whatever reason, but also requires the preserving of innocent life wherever possible. 2. The life of the mother as well as her child comes under the full protection of the Sixth Commandment and all moral medical wisdom and skill must be used to preserve her life as well as the life of her child. 3. To say that we are ever placed in the position of choosing between two sins (killing the child or neglecting the preservation of the mother’s life) is to surrender our belief in the Sovereign overrul ing providence of our All-wise and All-holy God (I Cor. 10:13), and to allege that we must "do evil that good may come” . . . a philosophy clearly condemned by Scripture. 4. An exception is not made even in the extremely rare case in which in the judgment of competent medical authorities, the unborn child’s continuing presence inside the mother's body will neces sarily lead to the mother's death. In such a case, the premature removal of the unborn child may be justified, provided that all medical wisdom, judgment, and skill are used to preserve the life of the child as well as the life of the mother. This premature removal of the unborn child shall be at that juncture of time where the greatest possibility for recovery is indicated for both mother and child. If life is lost in such a case, and the death occurs not out of criminal negligence, but merely out of the limitations of human knowledge and skill, all has been done morally that could be done. If we cannot save both lives, we are nevertheless morally bound to save the life we can. ” Grounds: The concern of the Sixth General Assembly was to stop abortion. The concern of the Seventh General Assembly was to preserve the life of the mother in crisis pregnancies. We believe this overture as amended addresses and reconciles the concerns of the two assemblies. Adopted. 3. That overtures 2, 7, and 27 be answered by reference to the action taken with regard to Overture 12. Adopted. 4a. That Overture 11 be answered in the negative. (11-2). Grounds: The grounds for answering this Overture in the negative are that to adopt position of total abstinence would go beyond the requirements of scripture, (Deut. 5:32,33; Col. 2:20-23) and is contrary to the Westminster Confession of Faith, (Chapter 20, subparagraph 2), which forbid the binding of the conscience by the commands of men.
