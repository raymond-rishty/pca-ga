# GA29 O30 — Affirm Diversity of Views on Creation Days and Reaffirm 28th GA Position

**Assembly:** 29th (2001)  ·  **Source:** overture 30 From North Georgia Presbytery (to B&O)  ·  **Disposition:** Answered in the negative

*Source: [ga29_2001 p. 200](../markdown/ga29_2001.md#ga29-p200)*

---

“Affirm position of 28th assembly on diversity of views on creation Days” Be it Resolved, that the North Georgia Presbytery overtures the 29th General Assembly of the Presbyterian Church in America to affirm the position of the 28th General Assembly, allowing a diversity of creation views, and likewise, with charity toward all our members, declare liberty-of-opinion concerning the intent of the westminster divines in their use of the phrase “in the space of six days.” be it further r esolved, that the 29th General Assembly recognizes that, within the church body, there are strongly held views concerning the days of creation; and. the Assembly acknowledges that for a creedal-based church, positions of diversity-of-view and liberty-of-opinion are not permanently satisfactory. Therefore, Presbyteries, Sessions and individual church members are encouraged to explore ways by which the scientific evidence of general revelation can be objectively studied and validated so that the church can move toward a unified understanding of how god created the universe and brought the earth into existence. Meanwhile, let us rejoice in our bonds of brotherhood and the hope we share in the reformed faith until our knowledge of his truth is more fully developed. Adopted by North Georgia Presbytery on April 17, 2001. Attested by: /s/ Dwight Allen, Stated Clerk that communication #4 from Western Carolina Presbytery be received as information, and spread upon the minutes of the 29th General Assembly. Grounds: We appreciate the concern and call for purity, peace, and unity in the denomination. A dopted COMMUNICATION #4 From Western Carolina Presbytery (to B&amp;O) "A call for Purity, Peace and Unity of the Church" Fathers and Brethren: Two years ago, Western Carolina Presbytery initiated inquiry into the question of a woman preaching at the Cedar Springs Presbyterian Church of Tennessee Valley Presbytery, and also into the views of the Rev. John Wood on the subject of women preaching. Our concern, shared by many throughout the denomination, was that a woman should not be allowed to preach in a PCA pulpit. Tolerating false doctrine and false practice is one way in which other denominations have degenerated in the past. We believe that failing to express our concern would not have fostered the purity, peace and unity of our Church. It has been our wholehearted desire to encourage and promote the peace of the church. While we confess that we have been far from perfect in that quest, we believe that we can, with clear conscience, testify to the fact that we were earnest and sincere in all of our actions with regard to the John Wood matter. It was never our intention or desire that the Cedar Springs Presbyterian Church should leave the PCA. We wanted to know the facts. Our hope all along was for this congregation and its pastor to conform to the obvious teaching of Holy Scripture with regard to female preaching. With the events that have transpired since that time, and the departure of the Cedar Springs Presbyterian Church from the PCA, Western Carolina Presbytery desires to issue a call to the whole PCA to bring closure to this matter and to endeavor to pursue together both the purity, peace and unity of the Church. A Committee of Western Carolina Presbytery interviewed members of the Standing Judicial Commission regarding various reports that had been circulated and found that the investigative committee of the SJC found and reported that there were no discrepancies in the testimony of witnesses. Mr. Wood acknowledged that the testimony of the witnesses was accurate, and he stated before the panel "he should have been more careful to articulate his belief that women should not be ordained to the teaching or ruling eldership and that they should not be permitted to 'preach' in the traditional sense of authoritative teaching from the word of God, and teaching elders are called and ordained to do." Though one may differ with the finding of the SJC, the WCP Committee found that the SJC had a full and frank deliberation of the investigating panel's report and voted unanimously that "the investigation has not resulted in a strong resumption of guilt on the part of TE John Wood . . . and therefore judicial process should not be instituted against TE John Wood for his expressed views regarding women." We accept the decision of the SJC. The WCP investigation found that the SJC followed the Manual of Operations in determining to disqualify any one of the SJC members from participating in the deliberation. It is because of all the controversy and alleged irregularities surrounding the John Wood matter that we, once again, found ourselves duty-bound to raise the question of possible impropriety when reports were circulated that one or more members of the SJC had deliberately sought to protect John Wood at the Vision 2000 Caucus, but had not recused themselves from debating and voting. The WCP Committee investigated these allegations, and are happy to report that we have not been able to confirm them. In a statement made to two members of our Presbytery, RE John White, Chairman of the SJC, stated that he had not tried to advocate any particular course of action for the Assembly to take prior to the meeting of the Assembly itself. Mr. White indicated that at a meeting of the Vision 2000 Caucus several months prior to the 2000 General Assembly, he had set forth what he thought was the Constitutional position, viz., that the Assembly could not overturn the SJC's determination in the John Wood matter. We hereby report Mr. White's recounting of the facts, in the hope that the rumor that one or more SJC members have engaged in improper lobbying regarding the John Wood matter can now be put to rest. As we come to closure on the John Wood Judicial Matter, we hope and pray that all of us in the Presbyterian Church in America have learned from it.
