Procedure for Hearing a Memorial Response: It is the advice of CCB that the “Procedure for Hearing a Memorial'’ raises significant constitutional issues. These issues include: 1) our concern that the procedures may reflect a definition of a memorial inconsistent with our historical Presbyterian usage since no clear definition is offered and the term has been variously used in other/former denominations ina manner that allows a higher court to act for a lower court. 2) The lack of clear BCO or SJC Manual definition of what constitutes a “memorial” being “administratively in order.” 3) Significant questions of due process such as the preclusion of appropriate briefing and argument. As an editorial note, we also remind the Stated Clerk that if the changes proposed by the SJC relating to new SJC Manual section 18 are approved, then the present references to SJC Manual 18 in SJC Manual 13-9 (and potentially other places) will need to be RE numbered. B. Considered a constitutional inquiry from Philadelphia Presbytery: “At its May 11, 2002, stated meeting, Philadelphia Presbytery voted to make a constitutional inquiry to the Constitutional Business Committee of the General Assembly, to wit: having accepted an appeal from a church member against a judgment rendered against him by the session of his church, and having referred that appeal to a duly appointed commission properly erected by Presbytery, does the Presbytery have the right: 1. to take back from said commission the matter committed to it before the commission can complete its task and render a judgment; and 2. to reverse, without hearing the appeal, the decision of the Session, which found the appellant guilty and suspended him from the office of ruling elder? The actual case in dispute has been in the commission's hands for sixteen months and the record of the case has still not be enable to be finalized to the satisfaction of the appellant, the session, and the commission. Philadelphia Presbytery’ needs and requests the advice of our fathers and brothers. ” It is the opinion of the CCB that in reference to question #1 of the inquiry, our answer is yes, the Presbytery may take back from said commission the matter committed to it before the commission has completed its task and rendered a judgment. In reference to question #2, our answer is no, the Presbytery may not reverse, without hearing the appeal, the decision of the Session. The Presbytery must follow the procedures detailed in BCO 42. Received a constitutional inquiry asking CCB to give advice on the constitutionality of the Committee of Commissioners recommendation #15. The question posed for CCB was as follows, Is it constitutional for the General Assembly to adopt the following recommendation: That the General Assembly mandate coverage in the PCA health plans for all active ministers and church lay employees who are current residents of the United States and who are not covered under a spouse’s group health plan, and that the Presbyteries be responsible for implementing this mandate. The CCB answered: No. It is the opinion of the CCB that in the absence of any evidence from BCO 14, the Westminster Confession of Faith and Catechisms, or Scripture as to the right of the General Assembly to make such a requirement; the General Assembly may not mandate coverage in the PCA health plan.
