“Amend _BCO_ 34-1 Regarding Advisors and Original Jurisdiction in Process Against a TE”

**Whereas** the issue of maintaining high moral and theological standards for ministers in the PCA continues to be of utmost importance; and

**Whereas** the _Book of Church_ order speaks to how these standards are to be upheld by Presbyteries; and

**Whereas** the Church of the Lord Jesus Christ and her ministers are under greater public scrutiny than at any time in recent memory; and

**Whereas** the officers of the church are charged with devising methods and strategies that enable us to maintain high standards for our ministers while also protecting the rights of the accused; and

**Whereas** every Presbytery could benefit from the voluntary input, engagement and concerns of their fellow Presbyteries, especially in controversial cases; and

**Whereas** it remains important to maintain the principle of original jurisdiction; and

**Whereas** removal of original jurisdiction should only take place when it can be grounded in the constitution; and

**Whereas** the PCA has grown considerably since the time _BCO_ 34-1 was last amended; and

**Whereas** the age of the internet has significantly impacted the way we share and receive information;

**Therefore, be it resolved** that _BCO_ 34-1 be amended as follows (underlining for additions, strikethrough for deletions): **34-1.** Process against a minister shall be entered before the Presbytery of which he is a member and may include Teaching or Ruling Elders from other Presbyteries as non-voting advisors. However, if at least five other Presbyteries believe that the Presbytery of original jurisdiction has failed to uphold the Constitution the Presbytery refuses to act in doctrinal cases or cases of public scandal and two other Presbyteries and if they can cite the specific portion of the Constitution that may have been violated, they may jointly request the General Assembly to assume original jurisdiction (to first receive and initially hear and determine), and the General Assembly shall do so So that the amended paragraph will read as follows: **34-1.** Process against a minister shall be entered before the Presbytery of which he is a member and may include Teaching or Ruling Elders from other Presbyteries as non-voting advisors. However, if at least five other Presbyteries believe that the Presbytery of original jurisdiction has failed to uphold the Constitution in doctrinal cases or cases of public scandal and can cite the specific portion of the Constitution that may have been violated, they may jointly request the General Assembly to assume original jurisdiction (to first receive and initially hear and determine), and the General Assembly shall do so. **Rationale for granting the ability to appoint advisors from other** **Presbyteries:** Over the last several years far too many ministerial ethics and doctrinal scandals have undermined the work of the Church. As Presbyteries have sought to address these scandals critics of their efforts have arisen from both inside and outside the church. One thing that all these critics seem to have in common is their concern that Presbyteries are incapable of or unwilling to sanction their own. The evidence supporting these claims is uneven, however, there is no doubt that there is a public perception that Presbyteries are networks of ‘good ole boys’ who protect their own from appropriate accountability, a perception that continues to damage the credibility of the Church. Setting aside the question of whether Presbyteries are actually guilty of shielding their own from discipline, any change that increases the accountability ina disciplinary investigation should be viewed as a good thing if done appropriately. In an effort to address the charge that Presbyteries are sometimes unwilling to sanction their own while continuing to uphold the integrity of our system of polity surrounding discipline, we propose that giving Presbyteries an option of including Ruling or Teaching Elder advisors from other Presbyteries may improve the transparency of an investigation which would also be a helpful step toward reclaiming some of the credibility that has been lost in recent years. In offering this amendment we recognize that Presbyteries always have an option to reference (See _BCO_ 41) to the higher court, however, it is our view that doing so skips an important step in our normal polity of trial by a jury of one’s peers in a court that remains as close to the original evidence and witnesses in the case as possible. Furthermore, this change keeps intact the important role that appellate courts have in reviewing the original proceedings and findings in any given case while also enabling presbyteries to receive more objective feedback from fellow officers on their disciplinary processes. As to the question of whether the corresponding investigative procedure in a local congregation should be changed to match the newly proposed language above, we do not think it is necessary since doctrinal and public scandal almost always pertains to teaching elders. **Rationale related to the change from “two” Presbyteries to “five”** **Presbyteries:** The rationale here is simply that “two” Presbyteries is far too low of a bar in the current state of play in the PCA and the age of the internet in which the “facts of a case” can quickly become diluted, taken out of context or missed completely. Originally there was no minimum bar for requesting a change of original jurisdiction. Then in 1988 the bar was set at two (2) Presbyteries. At that time the PCA had 44 presbyteries so this meant that about 4.5% of the Presbyteries would need to overture for the change in original jurisdiction. Today the PCA has 88 presbyteries.
